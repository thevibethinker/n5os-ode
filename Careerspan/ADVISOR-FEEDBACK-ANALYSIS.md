# Advisor Feedback Analysis: Candidate Output Format

Cross-reference of Alex Caveny's feedback against learnings from the meta-resume-generator skill.

---

## Part A: Extracted Advice from Session

### 1. Risks/Gaps Section Builds Trust
> "I like the risks and gaps to probe section... it builds a level of trust with the Careerspan output because, ultimately, the company knows the recruiter's always trying to close contracts ASAP. So when I've worked for companies that have used external recruiters, you're always super wary that they're hyping up the candidates too much."

**Core insight:** Leading with risks is counterintuitive but trust-building. It signals "we're not selling you."

### 2. Pair Risks with Interview Questions
> "If I pair risks/gaps to probe with interview questions that matter... [trails off approvingly]"

**Core insight:** These two sections are complementary—risks surface unknowns, questions give employers a tool to probe them.

### 3. Pills/Tags for Quick Scanning (Spikes)
> "Even if it is kind of a dumb way of communicating information, is still better than what I have right now... tiny little scale pills all the way at the top."

**Core insight:** Visual shorthand > dense text for first-pass scanning.

### 4. Dictionary.com Gradient Pattern
Alex screen-shared dictionary.com's synonym UI where words are:
- Ordered by relevance
- Color-coded by strength of match
- Not filtered down to 3—shows ~10 with visual priority

> "If you could take out 5-10 greatest positives about this candidate and have them ordered and color coded based on how directly applicable it is to this job AND the rarity of that skill/trait... could be easily digestible."

**Core insight:** Show more, organize better. Don't over-filter; let visual hierarchy do the work.

### 5. Rarity/Scarcity as a Sorting Dimension
> "Some combination of how directly applicable it is to this job AND the rarity of that trait or information."

V's response: "What would be even better is the relative scarcity of that *combination* of skills."

**Core insight:** Relevance + rarity = signal strength. Unique skill combos are the real differentiator.

### 6. Differentiate Story-Verified vs Resume Info
> "If you've started working with the company for the first time and they've never gotten a Careerspan report... you're like, 'oh, this info is from resumes, and this is from stories.' And then your priority ranking, maybe you bump the stories ones up a little... they're like, 'what the hell is a story?' And it can open up interesting conversations about why there's extra value here."

**Core insight:** Signal strength visualization isn't just for accuracy—it's a sales tool for Careerspan's differentiation.

### 7. Handle Uncertainty via Breadth
> "Your AI is not gonna be exactly precise because you're just taking information from a JD. You haven't done a full brain dump of the hiring manager. So if you optimize for just showing 3 things... that might not be enough. Whereas if you can show 10 and give them an ordering and a priority, you're saying 'I think these are most important, but there could be something on the lower important side that catches your eye.'"

**Core insight:** Acknowledge imperfect information by offering ranked breadth, not narrow precision.

## Part C: Areas of Agreement

### 1. Risks Build Trust (High Conviction, Both Sources)
The Anti-Resume framework made "negative space" the philosophical center. Alex independently validated this from a buyer psychology perspective: employers are *trained* to distrust recruiter recommendations.

**Implication:** Risks/gaps section should be visually prominent, possibly before strengths.

### 2. Interview Questions = Actionable Output
Both sources agree that surfacing "what we couldn't assess" and converting it to probeable questions is high-value.

**Implication:** Keep this section. Consider making it more prominent (Alex's pairing suggestion).

### 3. Signal Strength Differentiation
Learnings doc had this as "what worked." Alex elevated it further: it's not just transparency, it's a *sales mechanism* for Careerspan's value.

**Implication:** Signal strength should be visible, possibly with story-verified items getting visual priority boost.

### 4. Quick-Scan Visual Elements (Spikes/Pills)
Both arrived at the same conclusion: dense text loses. Visual pills/badges at the top allow 3-second scanning.

**Implication:** Keep spikes. Consider expanding from 3-5 to 5-10 with gradient/ordering.

## Part D: Area of Tension (Not Disagreement)

### Efficiency vs. Organized Breadth

| Learnings Doc | Alex's Advice |
|---------------|---------------|
| "Efficiency over completeness" — cut aggressively, show only the 3-5 most important things | "Show 10 with ordering... might not be enough if you optimize for 3" |

**Resolution:** These aren't contradictory. The Anti-Resume principle was about cutting *fluff* (generic soft skills, repeated evidence). Alex's point is about not over-filtering *signal*.

**Synthesis:** 
- Cut fluff (agreed)
- But show 5-10 *real* signals with visual hierarchy (add this)
- Let ordering/color do the filtering work, not omission

## Part E: New Insights Not in Learnings

These were absent from the learnings doc but surfaced by Alex:

1. **Rarity as a sorting dimension** — Relevance to JD is necessary but insufficient. Rarity of the skill (or skill combination) adds signal.

2. **Dictionary.com gradient pattern** — Specific UI reference for showing ranked items with visual weight. More elegant than hard cutoffs.

3. **Trust-building as explicit frame** — The learnings doc framed risks as "honesty." Alex reframed it as *strategic trust-building*. Subtle but important shift in how to position it.

4. **Uncertainty = show more, not less** — When you don't have perfect information (JD only, no hiring manager brain dump), breadth with ranking beats narrow precision.

## Summary: What to Carry Forward

**Keep (validated by both):**
- Risks/gaps section, prominently placed
- Interview questions paired with gaps
- Signal strength visualization (story vs resume)
- Spikes/pills for at-a-glance scanning
- Meeting-centric framing ("Should you take this meeting?")

**Add (from Alex):**
- Expand spikes from 3-5 to 5-10 with gradient/ordering
- Sort by relevance + rarity, not just relevance
- Position signal strength as Careerspan's sales differentiator
- Frame risks explicitly as "trust-building" in any sales copy

**Clarify:**
- Trade-off tables ("If you need X") — Alex didn't address. Test whether to keep.
