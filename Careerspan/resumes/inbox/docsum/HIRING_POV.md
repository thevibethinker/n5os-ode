# Hiring POV

## Explicit Requirements
- Full-stack experience with relevant technologies—Python, React/Next.js, PostgreSQL, or similar
- Experience taking something from 0 to 1 (led a project, founder, built impressive side project, shipped something real)
- Strong writing skills
- PostgreSQL familiarity (schema design, writing queries, basic optimization)

## Implicit Filters
- They want someone who has already demonstrated self-starter behavior in the wild—not just in corporate jobs
- They're filtering for people who don't need management or structure to be productive
- They want someone comfortable with ambiguity and constant change
- They're looking for people who write well—writing quality is being used as a proxy for thinking quality in async environments
- They want people who have already built things on their own initiative (side projects as genuine signal)
- They're filtering out people who view engineering as just a job or paycheck
- They want people comfortable talking to customers directly, not just PMs translating requirements
- They're filtering for people who can ship quickly without over-engineering—ship dates > code perfection
- They want someone comfortable taking ownership of outcomes, not just task completion

## Trait Signals
- Ownership mindset: "That's not my job" doesn't exist here
- Shipping velocity: Measures progress in days/weeks, not quarters
- Craftsmanship: Cares about clean abstractions, well-optimized queries
- Clear communication: Can explain complex concepts simply, writes well
- Pragmatism: Chooses simplest solution, intentional about tech debt
- Customer empathy: Talks to users directly, lets usage inform decisions
- Self-direction: Doesn't wait for direction, drives work forward
- Optimism: Not discouraged by setbacks, iterates through problems
- Low ego: Kind, flexible, cares about outcome > being right

## Red Flags
- No evidence of building something from scratch or shipping 0→1
- Poor writing quality in application or communications
- Cannot explain past work simply and clearly
- Expects hand-holding or ticket-based work
- Over-engineering obsession: talks about perfect solutions, not shipping
- Reluctance to talk to customers directly
- "Not my job" language or mentality
- Cannot point to something they owned end-to-end
- Slow shipping mindset: talks about quarters, not days/weeks
- High ego behavior in communication or interview
- No side projects or self-initiated work to show
- Uncomfortable with async communication style

## Validation Questions
- Has this person actually built and shipped something real, or just worked on existing systems?
- Can they work independently without daily direction?
- Do they write clearly and think through problems in writing?
- Are they comfortable talking directly to customers?
- Can they balance shipping fast with writing good code?
- Do they take ownership or wait to be assigned work?
- Are they pragmatic or do they over-engineer?
- Can they explain technical concepts simply?
- Do they collaborate well and give/receive feedback?
- Have they worked in async environments before?
- Do they have genuine enthusiasm for building, or is engineering just a job?

## Culture Markers
- Ownership > task completion
- Shipping fast > perfect code
- Writing quality matters deeply (async-first culture)
- Pragmatism > perfection
- Direct customer contact (no PM buffer)
- No tickets/assignment culture (self-directed)
- Low ego, high collaboration
- Technical debt as intentional tool, not villain
- Modular monolith, event-driven (not over-architected microservices)
- Remote-first, async-friendly
- Founding mindset (equity, impact, shaping direction)

## Story Types Valued
- Stories about building something from 0→1 and shipping it real users
- Stories about identifying a problem and solving it without being asked
- Stories about fixing something broken that "wasn't my job"
- Stories about talking to customers and discovering unexpected insights
- Stories about shipping quickly and iterating based on feedback
- Stories about making a hard tradeoff decision and explaining why
- Stories about taking ownership of a failed project and turning it around
- Stories about side projects built out of genuine curiosity
- Stories about async collaboration and clear documentation
- Stories about pragmatic technical choices (choosing simple over clever)
