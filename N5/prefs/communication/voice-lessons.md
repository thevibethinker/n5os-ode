# Voice Lessons

This file contains accumulated lessons from V's edits to Zo-generated content.

## How Lessons Are Organized

Lessons are scoped by **content type** (cold_email, linkedin_post, memo, etc.).
Some lessons are promoted to **global** when they apply across types.

## Lessons by Content Type

### Cold Email
#### Lesson: Remove all polite hedging and filler phrases that 
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #2)
**Pattern:** Remove all polite hedging and filler phrases that soften the message
**Avoid:** "Hi [Name]", "wanted to reach out because", "Would you be open to", "Best, [Name]"
**Prefer:** Start directly with value (no greeting), eliminate hedging verbs, end with confident low-friction question (no sign-off)

#### Lesson: Replace vague appreciation with concrete proof poi
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #2)
**Pattern:** Replace vague appreciation with concrete proof points and measurements
**Avoid:** "really enjoying it", "interesting synergies", "quick call sometime"
**Prefer:** Use specific evidence: "integration took 10 minutes", "just recorded a video", "15-min call" - quantifiable proof over abstract praise

#### Lesson: Use concrete language instead of corporate buzzwor
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #2)
**Pattern:** Use concrete language instead of corporate buzzwords and generic terms
**Avoid:** "interesting synergies", "our products", "really enjoying"
**Prefer:** Use conversational specificity: "something here", "what we built", "honestly impressed" - sounds like a real person, not marketing copy

#### Lesson: Convert tentative language to confident low-fricti
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #2)
**Pattern:** Convert tentative language to confident low-friction asks
**Avoid:** "Would you be open to" (tentative, high-friction framing)
**Prefer:** "Worth a 15-min call?" (confident judgment call framing, specific time commitment reduces friction)

#### Lesson: Lead with your strongest value proposition, not an
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #2)
**Pattern:** Lead with your strongest value proposition, not an outreach hook
**Avoid:** Start with generic outreach ("wanted to reach out because"), bury value in middle
**Prefer:** Start with concrete demonstration ("just recorded a video"), put proof first, then connect to request

#### Lesson: Reduce length by removing filler, not substance
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #2)
**Pattern:** Reduce length by removing filler, not substance
**Avoid:** Long messages with polite padding that dilute impact
**Prefer:** Shorter messages with higher substance density - 291→238 chars while adding 3 concrete proof points

#### Lesson: Remove meta-commentary and self-referential statem
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #3)
**Pattern:** Remove meta-commentary and self-referential statements that dilute the message
**Avoid:** "I had to reach out", "There's real partnership potential here" - stating the obvious or commenting on your own action
**Prefer:** State your purpose directly without announcing you're stating it. If you built something impressive, say "Your tech is impressive" without "I had to reach out" preamble

#### Lesson: Frame CTAs around interest, not availability
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #3)
**Pattern:** Frame CTAs around interest, not availability
**Avoid:** "Are you free for a 15-minute call" - focuses on recipient's schedule
**Prefer:** "Would you be open to a brief call" - focuses on recipient's interest, signals you're not just looking for any opening

#### Lesson: Add concrete metrics and specific qualifiers to bu
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #3)
**Pattern:** Add concrete metrics and specific qualifiers to build credibility
**Avoid:** "we're in conversations with several other top API providers" - vague, unconvincing
**Prefer:** "just our discord has over 1K+ users, and we're growing fast" - specific metric with momentum qualifier

#### Lesson: Position with authority - advisor, not helper
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #3)
**Pattern:** Position with authority - advisor, not helper
**Avoid:** "helping Zo build out their data partnerships" - positions as subordinate doing work
**Prefer:** "now an advisor recommending partnerships" - positions as expert with judgment

#### Lesson: Combine related information into dense, flowing se
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #3)
**Pattern:** Combine related information into dense, flowing sentences instead of breaking them apart
**Avoid:** Separate paragraphs or sentences for related credentials and role information
**Prefer:** Combine credentials and role into single flowing sentence; combine partnership status and value proposition into one dense sentence with colon usage

#### Lesson: Use direct, confident word choices that increase a
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #3)
**Pattern:** Use direct, confident word choices that increase authority and specificity
**Avoid:** "Google Flights assistant", "Quick context:", "helping Zo build out", "discuss", "free for"
**Prefer:** "fighter booker" (more specific), "For context:" (more professional), "advisor recommending" (more authority), "explore" (more active), "open to" (interest-focused)

#### Lesson: Use parenthetical analogies to make technical conc
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #3)
**Pattern:** Use parenthetical analogies to make technical concepts more relatable
**Avoid:** Plain technical descriptions that may not immediately click
**Prefer:** "(basically Manus if it had access to filesystem and was persistent)" - immediately frames the concept in familiar terms

### Linkedin Post

#### Lesson: Use specific names and credentials for authority
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #4)
**Pattern:** Use specific names and credentials for authority
**Avoid:** Generic or unattributed teaching ('I'll show you' without context)
**Prefer:** Name specific speakers with their affiliations and roles (e.g., 'Ben Guo (Zo), Logan Currie (Careerspan)')

#### Lesson: Replace abstract concepts with concrete examples
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #4)
**Pattern:** Replace abstract concepts with concrete examples
**Avoid:** Vague terms like 'fundamentals,' 'crash course,' generic descriptions
**Prefer:** Specific use cases and examples (e.g., 'meeting intelligence, professional brand upkeep')

#### Lesson: Provide direct CTA URLs without ambiguity
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #4)
**Pattern:** Provide direct CTA URLs without ambiguity
**Avoid:** 'Link in comments' or other indirect references that require user to hunt for action
**Prefer:** Direct URL with explicit action verb ('RSVP: [direct URL]')

#### Lesson: Frame collaborative work with multiple voices
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #4)
**Pattern:** Frame collaborative work with multiple voices
**Avoid:** Solo teaching frame ('I'll show you,' 'I'll teach you')
**Prefer:** Name collaborators and frame as joint effort ('Ben Guo, Logan Currie, and I')

#### Lesson: Use descriptive, casual terminology over formal la
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #4)
**Pattern:** Use descriptive, casual terminology over formal labels
**Avoid:** Formal or generic labels ('workshop,' 'crash course')
**Prefer:** More descriptive, casual language ('session,' 'full stack of personal productivity')

#### Lesson: Include human acknowledgment for collaborative wor
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #4)
**Pattern:** Include human acknowledgment for collaborative work
**Avoid:** Failing to recognize partners, community, or contributors
**Prefer:** Explicit thanks and community context ('A big thanks to @Ben Lang,' 'Next Play community')

#### Lesson: Prefer narrative flow over bulleted lists
**Learned:** 2026-01-19 (from pair #4)
**Pattern:** Prefer narrative flow over bulleted lists
**Avoid:** Bullet-point list structure that interrupts reading flow
**Prefer:** Narrative paragraphs with integrated value statements
