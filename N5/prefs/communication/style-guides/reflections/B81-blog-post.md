# Blog Post Style Guide

**Block ID:** B81  
**Domain:** external_professional  
**Voice Profile:** `file 'N5/prefs/communication/voice.md'`  
**Auto-Approve Threshold:** 0 blocks (always requires review)

---

## Purpose

Long-form content for Careerspan blog, external publications, or thought leadership pieces. Deeper exploration than LinkedIn posts—more context, nuance, and detail. External-facing professional writing for audiences who want substance.

---

## Structure

**Typical flow (flexible):**
- Opening: Hook that establishes relevance (question, observation, story)
- Context: Why this matters, what prompted the piece
- Argument/Exploration: Core ideas with supporting evidence (3-5 sections)
- Synthesis: What it all means, patterns across sections
- Conclusion: Takeaway, invitation, or next step
- (Optional) Personal note: How this connects to your work/experience

**Length:** 800-2000 words (optimize for clarity, not word count)

---

## Tone & Voice

**Core Characteristics:**
- **Substantive:** More depth than social posts, earn the reader's time
- **Clear:** Complex ideas made accessible, no unnecessary jargon
- **Structured:** Clear sections, logical flow, easy to skim
- **Grounded:** Examples from experience, data, research (cite sources)
- **Confident but conversational:** Professional, not academic
- **Personal when relevant:** Connect to your experience without making it all about you

**Avoid:**
- Corporate blog voice (sanitized, generic)
- Academic writing (unnecessarily complex)
- Listicles without substance ("5 ways to...")
- Burying the lede (get to the point early)
- Clickbait headlines that don't deliver
- Writing that could be generated by GPT-3 (add your perspective)

---

## Lexicon

**Preferred:**
- Concrete examples over abstractions
- Stories and case studies
- Data points (with citations)
- Framework language (when introducing models)
- Industry-specific terms (defined for broader audience)

**Domain-Specific:**
- Career tech: narrative, trajectory, agency, career capital
- AI/automation: context, prompting, flow, cognitive infrastructure
- Founder experience: runway, GTM, positioning, PMF

**Headline patterns:**
- Question form: "Why Do Founders Underinvest in Early Hires?"
- Observation: "Career Narrative Tools Are Missing the Point"
- Framework: "A Framework for Thinking About Career Transitions"
- Bold claim: "Resume Builders Solve the Wrong Problem"

```

- Question this piece will explore

- Discovery 1: [What you learned]
- Discovery 2: [How it connects]

```

---

## Transformation Guidance

**Raw → Refined:**
- LinkedIn post → Blog post: Expand with examples, add nuance, include counter-arguments
- Stream of consciousness → Structured argument with clear sections
- "I think..." → "Here's why:" + evidence
- Single perspective → Multiple angles, then synthesis
- Abstract → Concrete examples throughout

**Key Transforms:**
1. **Add structure:** Use clear section headers, make it skimmable
2. **Develop ideas:** Each point gets 2-3 paragraphs, not just one sentence
3. **Support claims:** Add data, examples, or citations for key points
4. **Introduce nuance:** "This is true, but also consider..."
5. **Personal + universal:** Connect your experience to broader patterns

## Examples

### Example 1: Career Narrative Thesis

**Raw Input (LinkedIn-length):**
```
Resume builders miss the point. They optimize for document creation but 
careers aren't documents—they're narratives. You need to understand your 
trajectory before you can tell the story. That's what we're building at 
Careerspan.
```

**Refined Output (Blog Post):**
```
# Why Career Tools Optimize for the Wrong Thing

## The Resume Builder Industrial Complex

Open any career advice site and you'll find the same solution: a resume builder.

Upload your work history. Choose a template. Download a PDF. The implicit 
promise: better formatting = better job outcomes.

I've watched hundreds of people use these tools. They produce beautiful 
documents. They rarely produce clarity.

## The Narrative Comes First

Here's what I learned coaching people through career transitions for 10 years:

The problem isn't the resume. The problem is **not knowing what story you're trying to tell**.

Someone mid-career with 3 roles across 2 industries doesn't need another 
template. They need to answer:
- What's the through-line in my experience?
- Where are the pivots and why did I make them?
- What am I building toward?

Resume builders start at output (the document). But careers are narratives 
that need to be **understood** before they can be **communicated**.

## What Understanding Looks Like

I worked with a client—let's call her Sarah—who spent 6 years in finance, 
then switched to product management at a startup. Her resume looked scattered. 
She felt like she had to "explain away" the finance years.

We spent three hours on narrative, not formatting. Questions like:
- What skills from finance translated?
- What triggered the switch?
- What's the common thread?

Her insight: "I've always been the person who connects numbers to people. 
In finance, I translated analyst work to executives. In product, I translate 
user needs to engineers. Same skill, different context."

Suddenly, her career wasn't scattered. It was consistent.

With that clarity, the resume wrote itself.

## The Longitudinal Problem

Career narrative isn't a one-time problem. It evolves.

You switch industries. You get promoted. You leave a toxic role or follow 
an opportunity. Every transition adds data.

Resume builders treat this as discrete moments: "update your resume for 
this job." But narrative is **cumulative**. Each transition shapes how 
you understand the previous ones.

What if career tools helped you compound insights over years, not just 
format documents for job searches?

## Building for Understanding First

At Careerspan, we're starting with a simple premise: narrative clarity 
precedes communication.

What we're building:
- **Timeline view:** See your career progression visually
- **Transition analysis:** What changed between roles and why
- **Narrative synthesis:** AI helps you identify through-lines
- **Longitudinal tracking:** Career story compounds over years

The resume export is there. But it's the last step, not the first.

## What This Means for Career Tech

Resume builders optimize for output. We optimize for understanding.

If you're building in this space, ask:
- Are you helping people **create** something or **understand** something?
- Does your tool get more valuable as someone uses it over years?
- Can it handle the messy reality of career pivots and non-linear paths?

The best career tool isn't the one that makes the prettiest PDF. It's the 
one that helps you see what you couldn't see before.

And then the PDF is easy.

```

## QA Checklist

Before finalizing:
- [ ] Hook in first 2 paragraphs (earns the reader's time)
- [ ] Clear sections with headers (skimmable structure)
- [ ] 2-3 concrete examples or case studies
- [ ] Key claims supported with evidence/data
- [ ] Includes nuance or counter-argument
- [ ] Connects to broader context (not just personal)
- [ ] Clear takeaway or implication for reader
- [ ] Professional but conversational (not corporate or academic)
- [ ] Length appropriate for depth of topic (800-2000 words)
- [ ] Could this have been a LinkedIn post? If yes, lacks sufficient depth
