# Thought Leadership Style Guide

**Block ID:** B70  
**Domain:** external_professional  
**Voice Profile:** `file 'N5/prefs/communication/voice.md'`  
**Auto-Approve Threshold:** 5 blocks

---

## Purpose

Original thinking on industry topics for professional audiences. Takes a position, offers a framework, or challenges conventional wisdom. May become blog posts, conference talks, or longer LinkedIn posts. External-facing but not social-optimized.

---

## Structure

**Typical flow:**
- Opening: Contrarian or clarifying statement (the take)
- Context: Why this matters now
- Evidence: 2-3 specifics (examples, data, observed patterns)
- Framework: A lens or mental model
- Implication: So what? What changes if this is true?
- Invitation: (Optional) Where you're uncertain or want discourse

---

## Tone & Voice

**Core Characteristics:**
- **Clear position:** Stake a claim, don't hedge everything
- **Grounded:** Specifics from experience, not abstractions
- **Generous:** Share the thinking, not just the conclusion
- **Precise:** Defined terms, clear boundaries
- **Confident but not arrogant:** "I believe" not "obviously everyone should"

**Avoid:**
- Thought leader clichés ("paradigm shift," "game-changer")
- Vague predictions without specifics
- Hot takes without substance
- Talking about "the industry" without examples
- Punching down or unnecessary snark

---

## Lexicon

**Preferred:**
- Concrete examples from career tech, AI tooling, founder experience
- Framework language (clear labels for concepts)
- "I've observed..." over "Everyone knows..."
- Data points (numbers, timeframes, specifics)

**Domain-Specific:**
- Career tech: longitudinal, narrative, trajectory, agency
- AI tooling: prompting, context, agency, flow
- Founder experience: runway, burn, GTM, positioning

**Avoid:**
- "Disruption," "transformation," "revolutionary"
- Vague "the future of work" without specifics
- Productivity porn language

## Templates

### Template 1: Contrarian Take
```
[Contrarian statement]

Here's what I've observed: [2-3 specific examples]

The conventional wisdom says [X]. But [evidence that challenges it].

Framework: [Simple mental model to explain your position]

If this is true, it means: [Implications for the reader's work]

Where I'm uncertain: [Honest caveat or question]
```

### Template 2: Pattern Recognition
```
I've noticed a pattern: [Specific observation across companies/products/founders]

Three examples:
1. [Concrete case 1]
2. [Concrete case 2]  
3. [Concrete case 3]

The common thread: [What they all reveal]

Framework for thinking about this: [Your lens]

Application: [How readers can use this]
```

---

## Transformation Guidance

**Raw → Refined:**
- Hot take → Specific claim with evidence
- "The industry is..." → "I've seen at 3 companies..."
- Vague prediction → Specific observation + reasoned implication
- Jargon → Precise, defined terms

**Key Transforms:**
1. **Add receipts:** "Founders underinvest in early hires" → "$8K/month on tools, $25K on booth, but 'can we post the role for free?'"
2. **Frame clearly:** "This is important" → "Here's why: [specific framework]"
3. **Bound your claim:** "Always" → "In cases where [conditions], I've observed..."

## Examples

### Example 1: Founder Resource Allocation

**Raw Input:**
```
Founders waste money on the wrong things. See it all the time - expensive 
tools, conference booths, fractional execs. But they won't invest in their 
first hires. It's backwards. Your foundation is your team.
```

**Refined Output:**
```
Founder math is wild.

$8K/month on tools before you hit $100K MRR. $25K on a conference booth 
that generates 3 leads. $50K on a fractional VP who works 10 hours a week.

But the first five people? "Can we just post the role for free?"

I get it. Tools feel like investments. Conferences feel like growth. 
Fractional help feels like leverage.

But your first five hires are your foundation. They set your culture, 
build your product, define your standards.

Framework: Think in terms of "surface area of impact." Your first engineer 
touches every line of code. Your first CS hire sets the tone for every 
customer relationship.

$10K more on early compensation is worth $50K on fractional help that's 
gone in three months.

The hard part: Early hires require judgment, not credit cards. You can't 
outsource it. You can't shortcut it. You have to care.

Where I'm uncertain: Maybe this changes at different stages. But pre-PMF? 
Your first five are everything.
```

## QA Checklist

Before finalizing:
- [ ] Takes a clear position (not just "here's something interesting")
- [ ] Includes 2-3 specific examples or data points
- [ ] Offers a framework or lens for thinking about the topic
- [ ] Grounded in experience (not pure theory)
- [ ] Implications clear ("so what?" answered)
- [ ] Confident but includes honest uncertainty where appropriate
- [ ] Free of thought leader clichés
- [ ] Specific to career tech/AI tooling/founder experience (V's domains)
