Here's a comprehensive breakdown of Vrijen's philosophy and advice on resume customization
and the master resume concept:

The Fundamental Shift in Resume Thinking
Vrijen identifies a common misconception about resumes: most people think they should create
one definitive document that lists their best qualities and experiences. Instead, he proposes
thinking about ourselves as "an amorphous cloud of skills and experiences and competencies."
The key is understanding that different jobs need different aspects of ourselves highlighted.

The Master Resume Concept
Vrijen advocates creating a comprehensive document he calls a "master resume" that serves as
a complete repository of every possible resume bullet point you could write about yourself.
Here's how it works:

Core Purpose:
- Acts as an ongoing career inventory
- Captures the totality of your professional experience
- Serves as a source document for creating tailored resumes
- Helps overcome "one-page blindness" that limits how people view their capabilities

Implementation Process:
1. Document every resume bullet you've ever written
2. Continuously add new accomplishments and experiences
3. Include different versions of similar achievements
4. Keep track of metrics and specific outcomes
5. Store both technical and soft skill demonstrations

The Customization Process
Once you have a master resume, Vrijen describes the customization process:

1. Start with the job description:
- Identify key responsibilities
- Note required skills and competencies
- Look for specific language and terminology used

2. Search your master resume for:
- Direct matches to required skills
- Similar experiences that demonstrate transferable skills
- Achievements that align with job responsibilities

3. Create "coverage" by ensuring:
- At least one bullet point addresses each major job requirement
- Your strongest relevant experiences are highlighted
- Language aligns with the company's terminology

The "Five-Second Rule"
Vrijen emphasizes that recruiters make initial judgments in about five seconds before deciding
whether to spend thirty seconds on a deeper review. Therefore:
- Every bullet point must be immediately relevant
- Customization should be obvious at first glance
- Generic content risks immediate rejection

Strategy for Selection
When choosing what to include from your master resume, Vrijen advises:
- Focus on relevance over impressiveness
- Consider the specific context of the role
- Be willing to omit great achievements if they're not directly relevant
- Remember that every word must earn its place

The Psychological Benefit
Beyond its practical application, Vrijen notes that the master resume approach has
psychological benefits:
- Helps people see their full professional value
- Reduces anxiety about leaving things out
- Makes customization feel like curation rather than limitation
- Builds confidence by documenting all achievements

Common Mistakes to Avoid
Vrijen identifies several pitfalls:
- Trying to include everything from the master resume
- Not being selective enough in customization
- Focusing on quantity over relevance
- Forgetting about the five-second rule
- Not maintaining the master resume regularly

Long-term Career Management
The master resume becomes a tool for ongoing career development:
- Regular updates force reflection on recent achievements
- Helps identify patterns in your career progression
- Makes future job applications more efficient
- Provides material for performance reviews and promotions

This approach represents a fundamental shift from seeing your resume as a fixed document to
viewing it as a dynamic tool that evolves with your career and adapts to each opportunity. It's
about moving from a "one-size-fits-all" mindset to a strategic, targeted approach to job
applications.

Vrijen emphasizes that this method requires more upfront work but pays dividends in the quality
of applications and the clarity it brings to your own career narrative. It's not just about getting
jobs; it's about understanding and effectively communicating your professional value.
