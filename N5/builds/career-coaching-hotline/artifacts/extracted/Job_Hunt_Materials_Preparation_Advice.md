Resume
Why should I be customizing my resume?

  ●   Resumes are the bread and butter of job seeking, but generally, you don’t want to be
      applying everywhere with a single resume
  ●   That’s because a resume is only “good” or “bad” relative to the job posting it is created
      with reference to
  ●   Also, at most jobs, the first “person” to review your resume is actually a computer,
      specifically an Applicant Tracking System that is going to scan your resume for the
      presence of desired keywords
          ○ You never know exactly what this list of desired keywords is, but it’s almost
              certain that most of them come from the job posting itself
                  ■ When looking at a job posting, it should be pretty easy to identify
                       important key words
          ○ In this way, a recruiter can filter out resumes that don’t have a certain %age of
              keywords present or is missing keywords defined as critical - this is why most
              people’s resumes don’t get seen by a human
  ●   That’s why tailoring a resume is just reframing yourself in terms that would be appealing
      to a particular employer based on what is being asked for on the job description. This
      reframing is achieved by:
          ○ Using resume bullets that reflect achievements aligned with the responsibilities in
              your target role
          ○ Emphasizing the classes, research projects, or extracurriculars that support your
              qualifications for the target role
          ○ Selecting relevant skills and certifications
          ○ Rephrasing your professional summary to align with the job description
  ●   Along the way, you want to include every keyword from the job description that applies to
      you. The more the better - but there is absolutely such a thing as overstuffing. If you feel
      like you’re at the risk of that, seek targeted feedback from a career coach or writer
  ●   A good resume will be
          ○ Clear and concise when talking about your previous responsibilities and
              achievements
          ○ Contextualizes and quantifies applicant’s impact on their organization
          ○ References the specific job posting - or even a generic one for the role - while
              being prepared
          ○ Adapts the language of the resume to reflect the capabilities and qualities
              relevant to your new role

How should I be customizing my resume

●   The way we recommend managing resume development is to have a “master” resume
      that is multiple pages and contains everything you could ever say about yourself
          ○ Every time you apply for a job, you remove the irrelevant information and plug in
              anything new from the job description that’s relevant
          ○ In this way, you’re “subtracting” your way to a tailored resume versus creating
              many different versions of your resume that are varying degrees of different
                   ■ It also gives you a central place to log your experiences - a great habit
                       that you should cultivate from the moment you start job seeking
  ●   Version management is extremely important - it’s all too easy to end up with hundreds of
      similar word/pdf docs. Here are a few related tips
          ○ Create a dedicated folder (ideally on Drive or iCloud, because you never know
              when you need a digital copy) where you store all your resumes. Then maintain
              the discipline of appropriately storing things in that folder
          ○ Properly label your resumes - date, job title, company, and version number if
              relevant
          ○ Store both PDF and word versions of your resume - generally, submitting the
              latter is better, but sometimes you need the former
          ○ Track the “variations” of your master resume

How do I write a good resume bullet?

  ●   Resume bullets are the headlining act of a resume i.e. what people are really here for
  ●   At minimum a resume bullet should reflect the capabilities that a candidate developed in
      their previous roles i.e. what you did? For example:
          ○ Taught English to high school students at the Deerfield public school
          ○ Prepared analysis of the current pricing model
          ○ Assessed cars for wear and tear as part of insurance inspection process
  ●   Remember, recruiters only care whether your previous roles have directly or indirectly
      prepared you to take on this new role. That’s what you ultimately need to convey

Note on creating a “quantitative” resume

  ●   One of the main things that distinguishes a good resume from a bad resume is how it
      showcases an applicant’s achievements
  ●   The best way to help people properly contextualize your achievements is to use a metric
      that helps the reader understand the degree to which you impacted the situation
  ●   The best way to illustrate your achievement is by using either an impact metric or a scale
      metric in your bullet
          ○ Impact metric - a number that reflects the degree of your impact e.g. sales closed
              for a salesperson, average class GPA for a teacher, social media follower growth
              for a marketer, etc.
          ○ Scale metric - a number that showcases the level at which you’re operating e.g.
              number of clients for a consultant, essays graded for a professor, posts per day
              for an influencer, etc.

●   Sometimes, the best metric to pick is the most obvious one because they don’t need
      explaining - the connection between your actions and the outcomes are self-explanatory
  ●   This advice is generally true for any situation in which you’re talking about your
      achievements - not just resumes

What are some general resume tips?

  ●   Submit .docx when possible; it’s much easier for ATS systems to parse
  ●   Nowadays, it’s more acceptable to have a 2-page resume
          ○ If you do opt for a 2-pager, just remember that everything you include should be
              relevant. Don’t annoy a recruiter by having them read irrelevant details
  ●   Don’t go below size 10 font; instead, add another page if you have to. Legibility matters
      most of all
  ●   Unless you’re in a creative field where the aesthetics of your resume are consciously
      being judged, prioritize a simple, clean layout with minimal graphics or images (better for
      ATS) and minimal formatting (so you aren’t constantly doing consistency checks for little
      things + better for ATS)


Cover letter
  ●   The first thing to figure out about writing a cover letter is whether you even need to write
      a cover letter at all!
           ○ Lots of major companies don’t require cover letters as part of their application
           ○ Looking up info about the hiring process on Glassdoor is very useful
           ○ If you have the recruiter’s contact info, you can ask them directly
  ●   If you don’t need to do a cover letter, then the question is whether submitting one
      anyway will add value. A few things to consider when deciding that:
           ○ Is there something in my application that requires explanation or context (failed
              college class, 3 months or more without work, etc.)? If so, I can use the cover
              letter to elaborate on an area that I know the employer might be concerned
              about?
           ○ Do I have something meaningful to say about the company or role? There are
              many things that you can connect with in a role or organization. If you have
              something thoughtful to say about that connection, the cover letter is the place to
              do it e.g. As someone that helps at his local soup kitchen, I love that your
              company encourages volunteering in the community
                   ■ The biggest danger of doing this is sounding generic - referencing things
                       that are true of any company or being generally complimentary without
                       being specific about why YOU care about the thing you’re talking about
           ○ Is there stuff that wasn’t sufficiently highlighted in your resume? Maybe you’ve
              done research in the same area of science as a company’s products or you have
              a niche skill that’s highly relevant to this role and you want to reinforce that point?
              Either way, you might want to write a cover letter

●     It makes sense to write a cover letter in all of these situations for one simple reason: it’s
         adding new information that’s out of scope for the resume
             ○ Additional explanation or context around your candidacy
             ○ Specifics about your interest in the company
             ○ Drawing their attention to something vital in their resume
   ●     Bottom line: Do not repeat information from the resume, only build on it

Do’s and Don’ts

Do:

   ●     Keep it brief - avoid going over 300 words unless you
   ●     Focus on drawing a connection between yourself
   ●     Stick to one point per paragraph
   ●     Quantify any achievements you talk about
   ●     Address the letter to the hiring manager or recruiter
   ●     Build them from the ground up - you can re-use portions or points across cover letters,
         but ideally, it’s totally custom to a specific company and role

Don’t:

   ●     Repeat information that’s already on your resume
   ●     Use hedging language like “I think I like” or “I believe I can” - just say “I like” or “I can”
         instead
   ●     Use fancy graphics or images
   ●     Use informal language or slang
   ●     You can materially increase your chances of success if you take the time to look up the
         relevant hiring manager or recruiter and addressing them directly
