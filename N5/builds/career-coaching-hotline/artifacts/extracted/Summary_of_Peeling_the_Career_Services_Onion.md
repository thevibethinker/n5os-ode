High Level Summary
Here is my elaboration of the key arguments and thesis of the white paper "Peeling The 'College
Career Services Office' Onion":

The core thesis of this white paper is that college career services offices are failing to
adequately support first-generation and low social capital students in securing quality
employment after graduation. Despite these offices ostensibly existing to help all students
transition successfully into the workforce, in practice they provide limited value to those who
need their services the most. The paper illuminates deep systemic issues that render career
services largely performative rather than substantive in their impact.

The analysis begins by situating career services within the broader university context,
highlighting the stark contrast between how well most campus facilities fulfill their stated
purpose compared to the career center. While housing, dining, libraries and health services

generally do what students expect, the same cannot be said for career services. This
disconnect between promise and reality sets students up for disappointment and frustration.

To substantiate the claim of career services' ineffectiveness, the paper draws upon two key
external analyses. First, Strada's "Bridging The Gap" report which found that only 27% of
seniors have a good job lined up before graduation, while 50% end up underemployed. These
outcomes are even worse for students of color and those from low-income backgrounds. Strada
identifies several contributing factors, including the low prioritization of career services by
university administration, cumbersome and outdated processes, over-reliance on students'
personal networks, and lack of meaningful career exploration opportunities.

The paper pushes back on Strada's assertion that part of the problem is students not
recognizing the applicability of skills gained through their coursework. While this may hold true
for some technical majors, the authors argue that many of the supposed "transferable skills"
touted by colleges from liberal arts classes do not actually translate into competencies valued
by most employers. Administrators' claims that they are teaching universal critical thinking and
communication abilities are often more rhetoric than reality.

Next, the analysis examines venture capitalist Ryan Craig's provocatively titled essay "Abolish
Career Services." Craig is highly critical of Handshake, the software platform now ubiquitous in
career offices, arguing it serves as a smokescreen obscuring their ineffectiveness, especially for
non-STEM students. He chronicles how Handshake muscled out competitors through
aggressive pricing and sales tactics to achieve market dominance while providing a clunky user
experience and negligible results for most job seekers.

More broadly, Craig contends that the digitization of career services over the past two decades
has coincided with growing skill and experience gaps that leave many students floundering in
the job market. Rather than actually addressing this challenge, career centers have adopted
Handshake to maintain an illusion of progress and abdicate responsibility for outcomes.
Ultimately, he advocates dramatically restructuring career preparation by making it a core
function of academic departments and the explicit responsibility of faculty members.

Building upon this prior work, the heart of the paper is the authors' own novel research surfacing
three deeper issues stymieing the effectiveness of career counseling. First and most pervasive
is the "muzzling effect" - strict institutional constraints on what counselors are permitted to say to
students. Constructive criticism and hard truths are heavily discouraged in favor of vague
platitudes and false encouragement. Counselors cannot be direct about the limited real-world
value of many degrees, the extraordinary effort required for an effective job search, the
inadequacy of Handshake for actually securing offers, or the gap between students'
expectations and likely entry-level prospects.

This muzzling exists on a continuum with the grade inflation now rampant in higher education.
Just as professors are pressured to hand out easy A's and tell students their work is excellent
regardless of quality, so too must career counselors affirm that everyone is a highly qualified

candidate poised for success no matter how their background actually aligns with employer
needs. Allowing too much candor risks generating complaints or even protests from students
regarding their grim job prospects, which administrators are keen to avoid.

The second issue is that career centers are not structured or resourced to provide the
high-dosage 1:1 coaching that at-risk students require to become competitive applicants.
Meaningful support is limited to surface-level resume tweaks and referrals to the Handshake
black hole, while more intensive interview practice, networking assistance, and application
troubleshooting are not offered. Contrast this to the highly personal guidance from senior
practitioners that is standard in professional schools like law. Some wealthy families even hire
private coaches to give their new grad extensive hands-on help, but few first-gen students can
afford such services.

Finally, the third challenge is that many career counselors simply lack sufficient real-world hiring
experience to advise students effectively, even if not constrained by institutional pressures. Most
have never been managers or made employment decisions themselves, so they struggle to
understand employer perspectives and translate them to students in ways that resonate.
Exceptional counselors with industry backgrounds do exist, but they are all too rare.

Stepping back, the paper identifies some promising initiatives working to fill the void left by
dysfunctional career services. Nonprofits like Year Up and Per Scholas bypass college to train
underserved youth in high-demand technical skills and place them directly into good jobs.
Braven and Beyond 12 partner with universities to provide structured group coaching and foster
social capital development. The authors' own startup 1Up offers intensive 1:1 support.

But ultimately, systemic change is needed. The paper calls for a new paradigm of career
counseling that starts with proactive data-based outreach to the students most at risk of
floundering, obtains permission for "real talk" unencumbered by muzzling, sets clear
accountability for employment outcomes, and delivers a substantive volume of highly
personalized coaching. Experienced former hiring managers should be recruited as counselors
and given free rein to provide unvarnished feedback.

Only by peeling away the illusions and teatrics enveloping career services can universities get
real about providing all students, regardless of family background, with an equal opportunity to
launch successfully. Doing so will require significant shifts in mindset, resource allocation, and
service delivery. But the current model sells far too many students short. It's time to abolish the
charade of career services as it exists today and invest in reimagined support structures
positioned to make good on higher education's implicit promise of socioeconomic mobility. The
futures of first-generation graduates, and a more equitable society, depend upon it.

1Up’s Assessment of the Problem
The white paper's original research uncovered three core problems severely limiting the
effectiveness of college career services in adequately supporting underserved student
populations:

1. The Muzzling Effect
Career counselors face strict institutional constraints on the guidance they can offer to students,
even when a candid assessment would be most beneficial. Frank discussions of student
weaknesses and employment challenges are heavily discouraged in favor of generic platitudes
and an unrealistic veneer of positivity. This "muzzling" exists on a continuum with rampant grade
inflation - just as professors now feel pressured to award high marks regardless of work quality,
career offices are expected to affirm strong employment prospects for all advisees irrespective
of their actual competitiveness.

Diving deeper, this forced rosiness manifests in an inability to deliver hard truths across four key
domains:

a. Degree value limitations: Counselors cannot directly state that certain non-STEM,
non-vocational majors at less prestigious schools often fail to open recruiting doors by
themselves. While some technical and high-demand fields confer a significant signaling boost,
the qualitative "thinking skills" touted by many liberal arts programs carry less immediate
currency in a competitive entry-level market. Students with family connections can overcome
this, but those lacking social capital need an unvarnished assessment of the inherent limits of
their academic credential.

b. Job search effort requirements: Advisors are discouraged from quantifying the daunting time
and energy required for an effective job search, especially for those without a clear on-campus
recruiting path. Suggesting that anything less than 10 focused hours per week throughout senior
year is likely insufficient for success risks overwhelming students, yet allowing casual effort to
masquerade as adequate preparation is ultimately a grave disservice. Counselors must be
empowered to set expectations clearly and coach the required discipline.

c. Handshake platform limitations: While a revolutionary connector in some respects, the
Handshake system alone is inadequate for most students' job searches. Its database skews
toward larger employers and posted opportunities are deluged with applications, depressing
success rates. Advising a multi-pronged approach of networking, direct company outreach, and
cultivating referrals is crucial, but counselors hesitate to undercut the platform for fear of
seeming unsupportive of a major university partnership. Framing Handshake as the primary
solution when supplemental efforts are needed is misleading.

d. Entry-level job expectations: Calibrating students' aspirations with market realities is a fraught
process that many career offices prefer to sidestep. First-generation and low-income graduates
can feel a particular sting when discovering that transformational outcomes trumpeted by their

universities, such as jobs at top consulting firms or tech giants, are far out of typical reach.
Counselors must be equipped to discuss probable trajectories and worthwhile stepping stone
roles to ground expectations productively and mitigate disillusionment.

Overcoming muzzling requires both empowering individual advisors to deliver hard truths when
needed and shifting the institutional mindset toward candid assessment as a core responsibility.
An "opt-in" framework that allows students to request unfiltered guidance, perhaps through an
intake form disclaiming the potential for difficult messages, could be a starting point. At a
broader level, tying career services funding and leadership incentives to meaningful
employment outcomes, not just engagement metrics, would create accountability for surfacing
and addressing real challenges. Sugarcoating the employment landscape is a short-term salve
that ultimately burns students; constructive honesty must be normalized.

2. Insufficient Support Dosage
The depth of support required for many first-generation students to succeed in the job market
far exceeds the bandwidth and resources typically available through university career services.
Preparing these students to compete effectively for quality positions often requires dozens of
hours of high-intensity 1-on-1 coaching - personalized, real-time feedback to help them extract
the full value of their experiences, develop compelling personal narratives, navigate nuanced
social cues, troubleshoot application processes, and ultimately convert interviews to offers.

Such concentrated assistance from experienced advisors can be transformative, but lies outside
the scope of how career offices currently operate. At best, most offer brief resume reviews,
practice interviews, and general tips, all necessary but insufficient. Workshops and employer
events provide value but cannot substitute for individual coaching. Caseloads are too high and
appointments too short for substantive work; there is no systematic framework to provide
repeated practice and feedback cycles for those starting furthest from employer expectations.

This paucity of high-dosage developmental support in career services stands in stark contrast to
the intensive preparation top professional schools provide. At elite law programs, for example,
students are coached on interview techniques and networking strategies until they demonstrate
mastery, with extensive personalized feedback from seasoned attorneys. Leading business
schools have similar models pairing students with executive mentors for in-depth guidance.
Admittedly these are better resourced efforts geared toward more specialized pipelines, but they
highlight what historically underserved populations need to reach their potential - not just brief
transactional advice, but ongoing high-touch cultivation.

Some privileged families recognize this and hire private career coaches for their liberal arts
graduates. These pricey services help students package themselves in the most marketable
light and tap valuable social networks. Wealthier students effectively buy the level of job search
support they need to thrive. In contrast, most first-generation graduates lack both the financial
means to access quality coaching and the contextual savvy to even recognize what they're
missing. An "advice desert" leaves them stranded in subpar employment situations, never
reaching the socioeconomic mobility their degree should enable.

To truly level the playing field, universities must drastically expand high-impact 1-on-1 career
development as a core student service. Counselor caseloads should allow for weekly meetings
with struggling students throughout their job search, not one-off appointments. Driving
meaningful outcomes may require uncomfortable shifts in resources and priorities, perhaps
drawing staff time from employer relations and event planning toward intensive coaching. Some
advisory capacity could be carved out by empowering high-performing peer mentors, successful
alumni, and even retired executives to help deliver personalized guidance at greater scale.
Schools could also explore partnerships with external organizations that have cracked the code
on effective career coaching for underrepresented talent. What's clear is that episodic,
surface-level support is insufficient for the students who need it most; a new high-dosage
paradigm is essential.

3. Career Counselor Experience Gap
A third barrier to effective career services for marginalized students is the limited real-world
industry and hiring expertise of many university advisors. Having robust frameworks to coach
students through the job search requires direct experience evaluating candidates, observing
success factors, and making actual employment decisions. Yet a large proportion of career
counselors have never held hiring authority themselves, recruiting from a student services
background rather than the fields their advisees pursue.

This creates knowledge gaps that make it challenging to guide students incisively, even with the
best of intentions. Developing strong interview techniques requires having conducted scores of
interviews and seen the subtle cues that shape decisions. Honing personal narratives to
resonate requires first-hand exposure to the perspectives and unstated preferences of hiring
managers in that field. Advising on employer outreach, referral generation, and offer negotiation
is far more potent when informed by experience on the other side of the table. Lacking such
context, even caring counselors can devolve to generically cheerleading students' existing
approaches rather than concretely elevating their competitiveness.

The structural roots of this experience gap are multifaceted. Hiring directly from industry is
difficult when career services roles pay a fraction of corporate salaries; universities struggle to
compete for the practitioners most in-demand to share their expertise. The field is also
handicapped by constrained mobility and limited leadership pathways - advisors typically work
their way up within student services and can plateau in development compared to more
dynamic professions. These roles rarely attract ambitious top performers from other domains,
given the muted impact and reward potential; turnover is high among those who do enter from
industry.

Narrowing this gap will require a multi-pronged approach. To recruit more practitioners,
universities should create flexible arrangements such as professional-in-residence programs
where industry leaders rotate into career services for 1-2 year terms, bringing fresh hiring
perspectives. For existing advisors, ongoing exposure to employer partners through job
shadows, interviews, and feedback sessions can deepen their real-world lens. Developing

robust mentorship between senior career leaders and junior advisors, with substantive time
spent on applied coaching approaches, will progressively elevate the entire field. The core
competencies and training of the career services profession need to shift closer to an industry
orientation over time.

Some forward-thinking institutions are pushing the boundaries. The University of Chicago has
piloted an intensive pre-professional program for humanities students staffed entirely by former
hiring managers. Grads compete for slots and receive 150+ hours of focused advising,
trouncing outcomes achieved through traditional pathways. Initiatives blending liberal arts rigor
with industry fluency point to what's possible at the high end, but also underscore how far
mainstream practices lag. Until career offices broadly close the experience gap through creative
resourcing and strategic staff development, they will struggle to maximize outcomes for those
who most need their support.

Takeaways
The muzzling effect, insufficient high-impact advising, and limited industry experience endemic
in career services leave first-generation and underrepresented students profoundly
underserved. This triad of challenges demands a fundamental rethinking of the assumptions,
structures, and incentives governing the university's role in employment outcomes, especially
for vulnerable populations. Empowering advisors to deliver hard truths, scaling transformative
1-on-1 coaching, and infusing real-world hiring expertise are essential to moving beyond
surface-level support toward meaningful socioeconomic mobility.

Addressing these issues will require uncomfortable conversations about resources, priorities,
and success metrics in both the career services function and higher education more broadly.
Universities that champion diversity and inclusion cannot be content with episodic advice and a
platform login - they must grapple earnestly with the depth of support required to launch all
students with excellence. Career offices need the mandate, tools, and talent to push every
graduate to reach their full professional potential, especially those starting without systemic
advantages. Until first-generation outcomes become a paramount focus from the boardroom to
the advising center, progress will remain painfully slow.

The enormity of this challenge is daunting, but the opportunity for transformative impact is too
pressing to ignore. University leaders hold not just the futures of their own graduates, but the
seeds of a more equitable workforce in their hands. By thoughtfully dismantling the flawed
practices holding back marginalized students and pioneering a high-impact career development
model, higher education can make good on its promise as the great equalizer. The time for
incremental change has passed - it's time to peel away the layers of dysfunction and make
career services a potent engine of economic justice.

Strada’s Assessment
Strada Education Network's seminal "Bridging The Gap" report offers a rigorous examination of
the systemic challenges and structural barriers hindering successful school-to-work transitions
for recent college graduates. The study's findings paint an alarming portrait of widespread
underemployment, inequitable outcomes, and institutional deficiencies that demand urgent
attention from higher education leaders, policymakers, and workforce development
professionals.

A. Core Statistics
The report's most striking revelations center on three key data points that quantify the extent of
the school-to-work gap:

1. Only 27% of college seniors secure what the study deems "good jobs" prior to graduation.
While the precise definition of a "good job" is not explicitly operationalized within the white
paper's synopsis, it presumably encompasses roles that align with students' fields of study,
leverage their academic skill sets, offer competitive compensation and benefits, and provide
opportunities for growth and advancement. The fact that nearly three-quarters of students fail to
achieve this benchmark before completing their degrees is a sobering indictment of the efficacy
of existing career preparation and placement initiatives.

2. An astonishing 50% of recent graduates find themselves underemployed upon entering the
workforce. Again, while the specific parameters of "underemployment" are not delineated, this
likely includes graduates working part-time when seeking full-time positions, those in roles that
do not require a college degree, and those earning wages significantly below the median for
their academic major and geographic location. That half of all graduates are languishing in
suboptimal employment situations is a clear signal that the return on investment for higher
education is falling short of students' expectations and societal needs.

3. The burdens of underemployment and lack of access to quality career opportunities fall
disproportionately on students of color and those from low-income backgrounds. While the white
paper does not provide the granular disaggregation of employment outcomes by race/ethnicity
and socioeconomic status contained in the full Strada report, it affirms the presence of dramatic
disparities that mirror and reinforce broader societal inequities. This troubling pattern suggests
that current career services paradigms are not only ineffective in aggregate, but are particularly
failing the very populations most in need of robust support.

These stark statistics underscore the profound misalignment between the promise of higher
education as a vehicle for socioeconomic mobility and the reality of graduates' lived experiences
in the labor market. They point to an urgent need for transformative interventions that extend far
beyond incremental tweaks to existing career services programming.

B. Identified Issues

Diving deeper, the Strada report surfaces a range of underlying factors contributing to anemic
graduate outcomes, clustered around four key themes:

1. Career services remain a low administrative priority for most higher education institutions.
Despite the centrality of employment outcomes to students' motivations for pursuing
postsecondary education and the increasing public scrutiny of graduate job placement rates,
university leaders continue to treat career development as a peripheral concern. Many
institutions relegate career services to a under-resourced and marginalized office, disconnected
from the core academic enterprise and lacking the stature to drive substantive change. Until
career readiness becomes a paramount strategic imperative championed at the highest levels
of institutional leadership, progress will remain limited.

2. Existing career services processes are often slow, cumbersome, and burdened by
redundancies that limit their impact. Many centers rely on antiquated technology platforms,
fragmented data systems, and manual workflows that create unnecessary friction for students
and employers alike. Lack of integration with academic departments, advising units, and alumni
networks leads to siloed programming and missed opportunities for collaboration. Bureaucratic
rigidities make it difficult to nimbly adapt offerings to shifting labor market demands and student
needs. Streamlining operations and leveraging cutting-edge technologies will be essential to
delivering career supports at scale.

3. Students' career exploration and employment prospects remain heavily dependent on the
social capital derived from their personal networks, reinforcing privilege and impeding equitable
access. Those from affluent, well-connected families benefit from a web of informal relationships
and insider knowledge that confers significant advantages in navigating opaque hiring
processes. First-generation students, low-income students, and students of color often lack
such support systems, placing them at a stark disadvantage. Career services must proactively
cultivate social capital for underserved populations through targeted networking opportunities,
mentoring programs, and employer partnerships that level the playing field.

4. Universities offer limited structured opportunities for students to meaningfully explore career
options, gain work-based learning experiences, and develop professional identities. Many
institutions confine career development to a scattering of optional workshops and job fairs,
disconnected from the core curriculum and lacking a coherent developmental framework.
Experiential learning opportunities like internships, co-ops, and research assistantships are
often under-promoted, inequitably accessed, and insufficiently integrated with academic content.
Infusing career exploration across the student lifecycle and embedding hands-on learning into
every major will be critical to fostering informed decision-making and competitive job
candidacies.

These issues highlight the multifaceted nature of the career readiness crisis, spanning
organizational structures, operational processes, programmatic design, and equity
considerations. Meaningful progress will require a holistic re-envisioning of the university's role
in workforce development, not just incremental enhancements to career services units.

C. Skills Recognition Challenge
Beyond the structural barriers impeding career services efficacy, the Strada report also grapples
with the complex question of skill alignment between higher education and the rapidly evolving
labor market. Its conclusions challenge some of the foundational assumptions underpinning
universities' value proposition to students and employers alike.

1. The notion that a liberal arts education imbues graduates with readily transferable skills that
are universally valued by employers comes under sharp scrutiny. While narrow technical and
vocational competencies have clear applicability to specific roles, the expansive
problem-solving, communication, and critical thinking abilities often touted by liberal arts
proponents prove more challenging to concretely demonstrate and reliably deploy in
professional contexts. This leaves many students struggling to articulate the relevance of their
college experiences to cynical hiring managers and floundering in the crucial task of "last mile"
translation between academic achievements and workplace readiness.

2. The report exposes a yawning chasm between the competencies cultivated in the classroom
and those demanded in the modern workplace. Many curricula remain rooted in narrow
disciplinary paradigms that bear little resemblance to the messy, multifaceted challenges
confronted by 21st century professionals. Coursework often prioritizes abstract theory over
practical application, individual achievement over collaborative problem-solving, and artificially
bounded subject matter expertise over the integrated perspectives required to grapple with
complex systems. Bridging this gap will require more than just bolted-on career programming; it
will demand a fundamental rethinking of instructional design and assessment practices to
ensure alignment with real-world demands.

3. University leaders' rhetorical invocations of skill development as a core institutional strength
come under withering critique. The report casts doubt on the empirical basis for administrators'
sweeping claims about the workforce applicability of competencies honed through curricular and
co-curricular experiences. In the absence of rigorous learning outcomes assessment, employer
skills validation, and long-term efficacy data, such assertions frequently appear as little more
than empty marketing slogans. Restoring confidence in higher education's skill-building value
proposition will require a renewed commitment to evidence-based practices, transparent
evaluation, and continuous improvement mechanisms that close the feedback loop between
classroom and career.

The skills recognition crisis speaks to a profound failure of academia to keep pace with the
breakneck velocity of economic transformation. It underscores the dangers of clinging to
anachronistic pedagogical models in the face of rapidly mutating labor market realities.
Institutions that hope to remain relevant and compelling destinations for career-minded students
must radically reimagine their approach to teaching and learning, anchoring it firmly in the
authentic challenges and skill demands of the professional world.

---

Takeaways
The Strada report's sobering findings constitute a resounding wake-up call for a higher
education system that has grown dangerously disconnected from the realities of the modern
labor market and the aspirations of the students it exists to serve. The yawning gap between
collegiate rhetoric and graduate employment realities, the stark inequities in career outcomes
across demographic lines, the structural barriers impeding institutional responsiveness, and the
widening chasm between academic skill development and professional readiness all point to a
system in dire need of transformative change.

Business-as-usual approaches to career preparation, rooted in outdated paradigms and siloed
support structures, are clearly falling short in the face of 21st century economic challenges.
Incremental tweaks and minor resource infusions will not suffice to address the profound
misalignments between higher education's value proposition and the lived experiences of
graduates navigating an unforgiving labor market.

What is needed is nothing short of a fundamental reimagining of the university's role in fostering
career readiness, grounded in a set of unifying principles:

• Elevating career development as a paramount institutional priority, championed at the highest
levels of leadership and woven into every facet of the student experience.

• Dismantling barriers to equitable access and outcomes, with a laser-like focus on expanding
opportunity for first-generation students, low-income students, and students of color.

• Streamlining and integrating career services operations to provide seamless, high-impact
supports that meet students where they are and respond nimbly to evolving needs.

• Cultivating a rich ecosystem of experiential learning, professional networking, and industry
engagement that empowers students to explore, refine, and articulate their unique value
propositions.

• Radically realigning curricular and pedagogical practices to close the gap between academic
skill-building and real-world readiness, ensuring that every graduate leaves with a robust suite
of validated and employer-recognized competencies.

To be sure, such sweeping reforms will not come easily. They will require courageous
leadership, disruptive innovation, and a willingness to challenge entrenched orthodoxies. But if
higher education is to make good on its promise as an engine of opportunity and social mobility,
it must confront the hard truths laid bare in the Strada report and summon the resolve to forge a
bold new vision for 21st century career readiness. The futures of millions of graduates, and the
fate of an equitable and prosperous society, hang in the balance.

Ryan Craig’s Proposal
