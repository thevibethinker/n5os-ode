Video 2: Introspection 101

Summary

  ●   Introspection is the basis of everything that you do during your job search
  ●   It helps you define what you want from your next opportunity + it helps you figure out
      what you have to offer your next employer
  ●   The reason people don’t do it enough is because it’s uncomfortable and it’s
      time-consuming
  ●   Even I don’t love digging into my past and I do weekly therapy
  ●   So what’s the quick and dirty way of getting started on some solid introspection?
  ●   Well, we at The ApplyAI think the easiest place to start is with anecdotes from your life

●   These are stories from your personal, academic, or professional life that are relevant to
      your job search - whether that’s because they’re the basis of a bullet point in your
      resume or a potential interview answer
  ●   These are stories where you’re the protagonist - the person solving the problem or
      achieving a particular goal
  ●   They’re also stories where there were clear challenges to overcome that you did by
      using your skills or knowledge in a smart, thoughtful way
  ●   Examples include everything from “Taking charge of a group project after a team
      member dropped out” to “leading a sales & marketing push for my division”
           ○ In other words, anecdotes can be about responding to a problem or achieving a
               particular objective
  ●   Making a list of such anecdotes and going through the following steps for each is a good
      way to start building your self-understanding without the pressure of open-ended
      self-questioning like “What am I good at?” or “what skills do I have?”
  ●   Once you have your list, there are two sets of questions to ask yourself dealing with the
      two levels of the anecdote - the surface level and the meta or underlying level
  ●   The six surface level questions you want to answer are:
           ○ (1) What was main problem or challenge I had to overcome?
           ○ (2) What were the consequences of this problem or challenging going
               unresolved?
           ○ (3) How did I figure out what to do in the situation?
           ○ (4) What specific actions did I take to address the situation?
           ○ (5) What was the result of my actions, both on the situation and the people
               involved?
           ○ (6) What lessons did I learn by going through this experience?
  ●   These questions will help you flesh out the anecdote so you have a complete story to tell
  ●   It also gives you an easy “first draft” of an interview answer using that anecdote

Video 3: Figuring out what to say about yourself for a particular job
application

  ●   During the job search, you’re presenting a composite of your best attributes - not one
      thing is going to do the job
  ●   The reality is no one thing about you is unique - we’re all just different combinations of
      traits, skills, and experiences.
  ●   It’s only when you bring it all together that you have something special - a unique
      individual that may be better suited for a particular role versus every other candidate
  ●   Your job is to figure out what’s the best composite of yourself to put forward for any given
      job
  ●   Hiring managers at different companies - sometimes even within a company - are going
      to be prioritizing slightly different things for the same role
  ●   Your job is to
           ○ (a) Figure out what they care about and in what order of priority
           ○ (b) Match what you have to offer to what they are looking for

○ (c) Communicate what you have to offer through the right channels
●   That’s why - at The ApplyAI - we think your materials and talking points have to be
    optimized for the job at hand
●   The first step in doing that is closely reading the job description and identifying the traits
    (personal attributes) and skills (capabilities - both hard and soft) that are being sought
    after. Pay particular attention to the stuff at the start of the list - the most important
    responsibilities tend to be the first ones listed
●   The next step is to figure out which of those elements you can justifiably claim to
    possess and list them in order of how confident you are. Remember, if you say you’re
    “empathetic” or have “strong written and verbal communication skills”, you’ll eventually
    have to support that in an interview answer.
●   Additionally, try and spend time thinking about qualities that you possess that aren’t
    explicitly referenced in the JD that you think would be valuable in that role. There’s
    always value in bringing something over and above what’s being asked!
●   Once you’ve made the master list of characteristics, narrow it down - focus on your
    strongest, most justifiable qualities. Somewhere between 5-10 will be sufficient. Avoid
    trying to overpack your final list because it’s unlikely you’ll touch on everything they’re
    looking for in a single-page resume anyway. It’s way better to prioritize showing the best
    of what you bring in spades rather than spreading yourself too thin
●   Also don’t worry if you’re missing a few things they’re looking for - an invaluable trait that
    almost everyone can and should convey in their profile is a “growth mindset”
         ○ Once you have this list of qualities, try to turn them into talking points. The
             formula’s simple: Trait/skill you have + how you’ve cultivated it + why it’ll help you
             deliver results at this new job. These talking points can be used flexibly to
             augment your submission in a variety of ways - during networking or recruiter
             calls, while preparing a cover letter, or as you tailor your resume for this role.
             Even if you never deliver them verbatim, developing them will refine your ability
             to talk about yourself in compelling ways
