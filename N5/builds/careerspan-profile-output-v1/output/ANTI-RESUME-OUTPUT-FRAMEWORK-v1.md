# The Anti-Resume Output Framework
## Careerspan Candidate Analysis — v1

---

## Philosophy

**Core Insight:** Resumes are positive-space documents. They tell you what a candidate *can* do. Careerspan's unique value is delivering the **negative space** — what they *can't* do, how they'll *fail*, and what remains *unknown*.

**The Question We Answer:** "Is this person worth 30 minutes of my time?"

**The Promise:** Even if the candidate isn't a fit, the employer will know fast. The analysis surfaces disqualifiers early. No ambiguous "maybe" meetings.

---

## Document Structure

### 1. Should You Take This Meeting?
**Purpose:** The 80/20 panel. Answer the core question in 3-4 sentences.

**Must include:**
- Clear yes/no recommendation
- One-sentence synthesis of who this person is
- What 30 minutes will reveal (the open question)
- Why the meeting won't be wasted even if they're not a fit

**Anti-patterns:**
- Hedging ("it depends...")
- Generic praise without specifics
- Burying the lead

---

### 2. What You're Buying
**Purpose:** The resume signal, synthesized. What a resume *would* tell you, but faster and with judgment applied.

**Format:** Table with columns: Asset | Evidence | Why It Matters for [Company]

**Must include:**
- 3-5 key assets (not a laundry list)
- Specific evidence for each (not generic claims)
- Company-specific relevance (not generic value)

**Anti-patterns:**
- Listing everything on the resume
- Generic soft skills ("team player", "hard worker")
- Evidence that doesn't support the claim

---

### 3. What You're NOT Buying
**Purpose:** The negative space. What a resume will NEVER tell you. This is Careerspan's unique value.

**Format:** Table with columns: Gap | Severity | What It Means

**Severity levels:**
- **High:** Likely dealbreaker unless specifically addressed
- **Medium-High:** Significant gap, requires mitigation plan
- **Medium:** Notable gap, manageable with awareness
- **Low:** Minor gap, unlikely to affect success

**Must include:**
- Specific, named gaps (not vague concerns)
- Severity assessment with reasoning
- Practical implications ("What this means for you")

**Anti-patterns:**
- Vague hedging ("may struggle with...")
- Gaps without severity assessment
- Gaps without actionable implications

---

### 4. Risk Profile
**Purpose:** How this person fails when they fail. Predictive, not evaluative.

**Format:** Table with columns: Risk | Assessment

**Risk categories (MECE):**
- **Ramp Time:** How long before productive output?
- **Likely Failure Mode:** When this person fails, how do they fail?
- **Management Overhead:** How much attention do they need?
- **Flight Risk:** How likely to leave, and why?

**Must include:**
- Time estimates where applicable (not just "some time")
- Specific failure patterns (not generic "might struggle")
- Assessment reasoning (not just verdicts)

**Anti-patterns:**
- Generic risks that apply to everyone
- Risks without assessment
- Missing the "how they fail" question

---

### 5. What The Meeting Will Reveal
**Purpose:** The unknown unknowns. What we genuinely couldn't assess. These become the interview agenda.

**Format:** Numbered list with question and specific ask

**Categories to cover (select relevant):**
- **Motivation alignment:** Why this job? Why now?
- **Operating model fit:** Can they thrive in your environment?
- **Trajectory alignment:** What do they want to become?
- **Self-awareness:** Do they know how they fail?

**Must include:**
- Specific, probeable questions (not generic interview questions)
- Why this question matters for THIS role
- What a good vs. bad answer sounds like (implicit)

**Anti-patterns:**
- Generic interview questions ("Tell me about yourself")
- Questions answerable from resume
- Too many questions (max 4-5)

---

### 6. Decision Framework
**Purpose:** Make the trade-off explicit. Force the reader to clarify their priorities.

**Format:** Table with columns: If you need... | [Candidate] is...

**Must include:**
- 5-7 specific trade-offs relevant to this role
- Clear verdicts for each scenario
- Both "hire" and "don't hire" scenarios

**Anti-patterns:**
- All positive scenarios
- Vague verdicts ("might work")
- Generic trade-offs not specific to candidate

---

### 7. The Bottom Line
**Purpose:** Final synthesis. The "so what."

**Must include:**
- Restate the recommendation (take meeting / don't take meeting)
- Why the signal is strong enough (or not)
- What makes this meeting low-risk (fast disqualification) or high-upside
- The one thing the meeting will resolve

**Anti-patterns:**
- Hedging the recommendation
- Repeating earlier content
- New information not previously covered

---

## Design Principles

### 1. Negative Space Over Positive Space
Resumes handle positive space. We handle negative space. Every section should include what's *missing*, *uncertain*, or *risky*.

### 2. Specificity Over Generality
Every claim has evidence. Every gap has severity. Every risk has a timeline. No generic assessments.

### 3. Trade-Offs Over Verdicts
We don't say "hire" or "don't hire." We say "hire IF you prioritize X, don't hire IF you prioritize Y." The reader decides.

### 4. Efficiency Over Completeness
The reader is a busy founder/CEO. Every word earns its place. Tables over paragraphs. Scannable over comprehensive.

### 5. Honesty Over Advocacy
We're not selling the candidate. We're giving the employer the information to make a fast, confident decision — including fast "no" decisions.

---

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any Anti-Resume output:

- [ ] Does the opening answer "should I take this meeting" in under 30 seconds of reading?
- [ ] Does every "What You're Buying" asset have specific evidence?
- [ ] Does every "What You're NOT Buying" gap have a severity rating and practical implication?
- [ ] Does the Risk Profile include a specific "likely failure mode"?
- [ ] Are the "What The Meeting Will Reveal" questions probeable in 30 minutes?
- [ ] Does the Decision Framework include at least one "don't hire if" scenario?
- [ ] Is the Bottom Line a clear yes/no with reasoning?
