   ✦ Design Director ✦ Currently rebranding LinkedIn from the inside out.

   4h

   Edited

   WTF people. Calm. The. Heck. Down.
   I swear I post one thing about the broken hiring system, and suddenly the entire recruiter-to-robot pipeline shows up like I set off a bat signal in LinkedIn Gotham.
   I’m not turning comments off. Go ahead. Go at each other’s throats. I’ve got snacks and a front-row seat to the chaos.
   But let’s get one thing straight. We’re not stupid. We know what’s happening. We know how ATS works. We know our resumes are being sorted, scored, filtered, and buried under a mountain of algorithms designed to “optimize efficiency” while dehumanizing actual people.
   So stop defending robots.
   Stop telling us the system is “fair” or that we “just need to tailor more keywords.” We’re not falling for the gaslight disguised as career advice anymore.
   We’ve rewritten, reformatted, and rebranded ourselves a hundred times over. We’ve followed every “insider tip” from every self-proclaimed “talent whisperer.” And guess what? The system is still rigged.
   The only ones winning are the consultants selling workshops on how to beat it. So yes, I’m loud. Yes, I’m feral. And yes, I will continue to scream about it until someone listens.
   If that makes you uncomfortable, maybe you’re too cozy with the machine.

   1st

   Founder @ Warp | Prev: Computer Science @ MIT

   7h

   Edited

   Most leaders refuse to mix relationships and work. I did and it's been one of the best decisions I've ever made.
   My wife [Gabriella G.](https://www.linkedin.com/in/gabriella-garcia-mit?trk=feed_main-feed-card-text) and I both have strong personalities - we both went to MIT, and we’ve been together 8 years.
   For a long time, I kept work completely separate. I wouldn't even talk about company problems at home.
   She'd get frustrated. "I can help with this."
   "Nope. Keeping it separate."
   Then I went to this founder retreat in Wyoming. Spent time with other founders at similar stages.
   We were all talking about the same problem: hiring people who can take something from zero to done. No hand-holding. Just make it happen.
   And I realized: Gabriella is EXCEPTIONAL at this:
   Product leadership at Google on fintech. Engineer @ Apple and Meta. Investor at @ Felicis & Two Sigma. She’s seen the pattern across dozens of startups.
   I came back and asked her to start on Monday.
   Week three just ended. She's already made one critical hire. Solved three problems I'd been stuck on.
   Here's what I got wrong:
   I thought keeping work and life separate was "professional."
   What it actually was: leaving one of the best operators I know on the sidelines while I struggled alone.
   The real life hack isn't that “work-life” balance everyone loves to talk about. It's having someone who's equally invested in the outcome sitting across from you when shit hits the fan.
   The only thing I regret is not doing it sooner.

   I recently found out that one of our competitors literally copied the [DOSS.COM](https://www.linkedin.com/company/doss-com?trk=feed_main-feed-card-text) Series A deck word-for-word to position themselves as "AI ERP" 😂
   Unfortunately - it gets SO MUCH WORSE!

   Of course I found out about this in the most entertaining manner -- I was talking with a GP at another fund and they stopped me as I was giving them updates... "Wait, I've seen this diagram before." They proceeded to pull up a carbon-copy of our hero slide.
   I was dumb-founded.
   I texted a few other folks and sure enough, this "vision" was circulated around all the top funds with their logo swapped out for ours... 🤡 they literally didn't even bother to change any of the words.
   So, I'm a bit curious now. If this company is willing to rip-off competitor materials -- who else are they willing to rip-off???
   Turns out they are:
   - Faking G2 reviews by the HUNDREDS via ChatGPT and contractors in the Philippines
   - Faking the scale of their business (and it's transaction volumes)
   In an attempt to build trust... they are lying at scale?
   And for those of you reading this feeling like I'm being bit extreme like "startups are hard, they're just bending the truth", I have to push-back. This isn't your run of the mill "growth hacking".
   I reached out to the founder months ago, flagging that there were a bunch of fake reviews and assumed the best when they said "Oops our growth marketing contractors must have done this, will get it fixed."
   We are in the TRUST business. Our platforms serves as the foundation for many of our customers to realize their life's work. When we make mistakes our customers businesses suffer. We have a moral obligation to do our best by them. I have had brands ask me to send our balance sheet to them. To pitch to their board of directors. I've been told "If DOSS went out of business and we couldn't switch to something new fast enough, we'd go out of business."
   This isn't "fluffing up some reviews on Amazon so your drop-shipping brand can get to the top of the buy-box"... this is "We are faking the quality of a mission-critical system that manages your operations, finance, accounting."
   Of course, every startup has its up and downs... with customers, technology partners, investors, media, your own team. This is an insanely hard industry that we're all trying improve.
   But to "Move fast and break things" and intentionally mislead + defraud the market won't work.
   I'm sure the people involved will be reading this. Do better next time.
