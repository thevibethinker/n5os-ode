Most of us suck at job hunting.

years.

Unlike daily skills we naturally improve through repetition, job hunting sits

dormant until desperation strikes.

right opportunity.

The feedback loop is broken too.

Employers rarely tell you why you didn't make the cut, so you're left

guessing what went wrong.

e@ Practice articulating your value regularly, not just when updating your

Getting ghosted on outreach? Even by people you know?

When someone helps you—makes an intro, gives a referral—they're not

spending social capital. They're accepting YOUR social currency in

exchange for theirs.

They trade their credibility today for your potential tomorrow. Every

introduction is them saying, “I'll trade my established reputation for this

person's future value.”

This is why people ghost you. Not because they're mean or busy, but

because you haven't shown them why your currency is a good trade.

So how do you signal your currency will appreciate?

Momentum proof: Not “I'm passionate about X" but “I published 3
analyses on X that industry leaders shared” or “I built a tool that 200
people now use weekly.”

Skin in the game: “I'm investing my evenings in this certification” hits
different than “I'm interested in this space.”

Follow-through evidence: “Here's exactly what I'll do with this intro
and by when" backed by “Here’s how | maximized the last connection
someone made for me."

Reciprocal value: Show you're already helping others in your network,
even with “lesser” currency. It signals you understand the exchange
economy.

Cold outreach works when you make the “currency exchange” attractive

—when you signal that your currently unproven dollars are bound to go

parabolic one day.

Ever notice how career guidance can feel like a maze of contradictions?
One expert says “job hop every two years,” another insists “show loyalty.”
Both can't be right... or can they?
Here's what I've learned after years of career coaching: Most folks
haven't cultivated “advice literacy" - the skill of understanding why
certain guidance exists and how to apply it to our unique situations.
All conventional wisdom has implicit assumptions baked into it.
For example, when someone says "follow your passion,” they're assuming
you have clarity about what that is, financial runway to pursue it, and that
you're capable of translating passion into career success.
But what if you're supporting a family, discovering your interests through
work, or building passion through mastery?
The advice isn't wrong - you need to understand it’s really about finding
work that energizes you, which can happen in many ways.
Developing advice literacy means:
@ Understanding the context behind guidance
e@ Recognizing what assumptions it makes about your situation
e@ Adapting the core insight to fit your actual constraints
This skill transforms how you level up. Instead of feeling confused by
contradictory advice, you start seeing patterns.
You realize that seemingly opposite guidance often targets different
contexts, career stages, or goals.
And once you develop this lens, every piece of career content becomes
more valuable--even when it isn't immediately relevant.
Moral of the story: Stop blindly collecting advice and start thoughtfully
incorporating it into a mental model.
