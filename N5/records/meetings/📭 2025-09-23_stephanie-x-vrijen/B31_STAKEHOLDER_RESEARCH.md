### Insight 1: Entry-level talent won't engage with "development" tools without tangible job placement promise

**Evidence:** "I feel like that might be the missing piece for us because, like, as you know, I've been struggling to get the students to actually come on engage. But I feel like that last piece of it of you'd be in a talent pool...that might be the piece that's missing."

### Insight 2: Vetted talent pipelines require relationship context to differentiate from generic applicant pools

**Evidence:** "Getting companies to actually recognize, holy shit, that's a great pipeline because they've got a much higher baseline...these are quality candidates...they show up to work, they've increased their professionalism, they've proven themselves...but [we struggle] getting them like getting companies to actually recognize...So I feel like there's something there if you can use that piece to sell because like we need that bridge."

**Why it matters:** Quality signals invisible in open market applications; employers need contextual vouching ("we can vouch for that") to recognize differentiated talent. Generic job scraping won't solve this - requires relationship-based curation.

### Insight 3: Early-career hiring budget follows perceived ease, not actual quality of candidate pool

**Evidence:** "Early career pipeline is not where people spend money first, in my experience. Because the, I guess the kindest way to put it is that people falsely equate having a lot of like, inbound applicants for it's going to be easy to find the right candidate. Where in fact, the counterintuitive reality is the more people that you have inbound, the harder it's going to be."

**Why it matters:** Employers deprioritize investing in early-career recruiting tools despite it being hardest problem (most noise, least signal). Explains why Career Span can't monetize early-career first - market psychology inverted from actual need.
