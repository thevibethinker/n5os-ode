## Strategic Messaging Blurbs

### 1. Value Prop (Partnership positioning)

**Usage context:** When explaining Careerspan's partnership strategy to potential distribution partners

"We're not trying to capture the entire recruiting value chain—we're exceptionally good at getting job seekers organized and engaged, and we partner with platforms that excel at employer relationships. Our candidates have retention rates 1.5-3x higher than typical career product users because we focus on deep reflection and skills evidence, not just resume uploading. If you're looking for quality candidate supply without the headache of cultivation and retention, we're the data layer you've been missing."

**What resonated:** Ash immediately recognized value of focusing on core strengths; clean division of labor between candidate cultivation (Careerspan) and employer sales (partners)

### 2. Technical Credibility (Differentiated data asset)

**Usage context:** When partners ask what makes Careerspan's candidates different from job board or LinkedIn sourcing

"Every candidate who meaningfully engages with Careerspan has shared 20 minutes to an hour of conversational reflection with our AI. We break down every job into concrete skills and match candidates based on three evidence levels: zero proof, resume proof, or reflected story proof. The more someone can demonstrate they've lived it—not just listed it on a resume—the higher they're weighted in our matching. We've also built capability to extract 'vibe' signals from interview transcripts, helping identify culture fit beyond credentials. Our head of AI is a genius, and we're sitting on a data asset that traditional ATS systems can't replicate."

**What resonated:** Ash acknowledged Careerspan's vetting quality might eliminate need for duplicate screening; recognized conversational interview data as differentiated (though flagged ethical concerns for their use case)

### 3. Mission Alignment (Refusing to charge job seekers)

**Usage context:** When discussing business model and target customer with aligned founders/investors

"We will never charge job seekers. Ever. These are fucking broke people who just lost their jobs—asking them to pay for career support is both morally bankrupt and strategically dumb. If you want network effects, you don't put up paywalls. We've proven the consumer product works (D30 retention 1.5-3x category average), but we're building this to be genuinely helpful, not to extract desperate people's last dollars. Our monetization comes from employers and partnerships where the value exchange actually makes sense."

**What resonated:** Ash had nearly identical philosophy ("No, we're not doing that shit"); bonded over shared frustration with VCs pushing job seeker monetization; values alignment created immediate trust

### 4. Partnership Economics (Success-based pricing affinity)

**Usage context:** When negotiating revenue share terms or explaining pricing philosophy

"We love success-based pricing. The 12-month retention kickback model takes stones, and we respect platforms that tie their revenue to actual outcomes. Our goal isn't to maximize per-candidate fees—it's to deliver candidates who actually succeed long-term. If we're getting a cut of placements that stick for a year, our incentives are perfectly aligned with yours and with the candidate's. That's the kind of partnership that scales because everyone wins when the match is genuinely good."

**What resonated:** Vrijen explicitly praised Ash's "stones" for 12-month retention pricing; recognized this creates quality-first incentive alignment vs. volume-based models

### 5. Community Distribution Strategy

**Usage context:** When explaining go-to-market approach and candidate acquisition to partners or investors

"We're building a decentralized network by partnering with high-quality communities—ex-Googlers, McKinsey alumni, Emory's network, and more. We give them the product for free as a member benefit, which solves their problem (providing career support to their community) while building our candidate pool with pre-vetted, high-quality talent. It's distribution through trust networks rather than paid acquisition. Every community partner becomes a channel where candidates come in warm and engaged."

**What resonated:** Ash asked "How are you guys getting volume on candidates?" signaling this is their core constraint; community partnership strategy offers differentiated answer to quality-at-scale problem

### 6. Product Completeness (Built, not building)

**Usage context:** When partners/investors question readiness or product-market fit

"The product is built. We have 3,000 signups, 800 deeply engaged users who've shared serious time with our AI, including the former chief of staff of Netflix. We have conversational AI that extracts meaningful insights, a matching algorithm that weights evidence levels, tailored interview question generation, and skill breakdown for every role. We've integrated with two job boards and a recruiting platform already. At this point, we're not in product development mode—we're in distribution mode. We just need partners who want access to pre-vetted candidates and communities who want to give their members a genuinely useful tool."

**What resonated:** Ash recognized Careerspan had solved the hard technical problems (vetting, matching, engagement); partnership conversation immediately shifted to integration logistics not product validation

### 7. Founder Positioning (Schmoozers, not salespeople)

**Usage context:** When explaining why partnership model fits Careerspan's strengths vs. direct B2B sales

"Logan and I are fucking schmoozers. We're great at building relationships, getting communities excited, and creating products people genuinely love. What we're terrible at is B2B sales cycles and convincing skeptical buyers to part with budget. Our ultimate superpower kicks in when people come to us saying 'give us what you have.' That's why the partnership model works—we cultivate candidates and build community relationships (what we're naturally good at), and partners handle the employer sales (what they're built for). We don't need to own every part of the value chain to build something valuable."

**What resonated:** Ash seemed to appreciate the self-awareness and honesty; partnership structure respects both parties' strengths rather than forcing square pegs into round holes

## What This Messaging Reveals

**Careerspan's strongest positioning:** Data layer / API-first candidate platform that partners distribute, not direct-to-consumer app competing for job seeker attention or direct B2B sales competing with recruiting agencies.

**Market opportunity:** Partners with employer relationships are talent-constrained; Careerspan has solved candidate cultivation and engagement (the hard, unglamorous work) and can plug into existing distribution.

**Founder-market fit:** Vrijen and Logan excel at community relationships and product craft, struggle with traditional sales. Partnership model lets them focus on strengths.

**Differentiation:** Conversational interview data creates unique candidate intelligence that job boards, LinkedIn, and resume-only platforms can't replicate. Whether this becomes mainstream or stays niche depends on ethical AI standards evolution.

**Strategic risk:** Relying on partners for monetization means Careerspan doesn't own customer relationship. If partner acquires or builds competing candidate cultivation, Careerspan loses leverage. Counter: Niche in community distribution and deep engagement moat creates defensibility.
