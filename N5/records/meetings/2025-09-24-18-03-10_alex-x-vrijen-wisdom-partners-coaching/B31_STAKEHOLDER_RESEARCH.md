### Insight 1: Hiring managers ignore traditional recruiters because relationship-building precedes value demonstration

**Evidence:** "They would want to establish a relationship before I saw their candidates. So it's like, I have no idea who the hell you are and I don't really want to start talking to you because once I start talking to you're never going to leave me alone ever again. But if it was just like, hey, here's my candidates. Do you want to pay for any of them? That's just sort of like, yeah, sure, I can take five minutes to look through your candidates."

**Why it matters:** Traditional recruiter outreach fails because it optimizes for relationship-building before proving value, creating perceived obligation and friction. Inverting this (candidates first, relationship later) removes the primary barrier to engagement and respects hiring managers' time constraints.

### Insight 2: HR departments and hiring managers have misaligned incentives regarding third-party hiring tools

**Evidence:** "I think HR likes their normal bullshit resume review process. Like that's their job security. That's what they do if there's an actual hiring department in the company. But if I'm like the busy engineering manager or team leader or whatever, giving you that digestible, very human centered approach, I think resonates a lot more because part of what that card is like it's a little more human centric than just your resume work history."

**Why it matters:** Selling to "companies" is too broad—HR departments may resist tools that streamline candidate review (threatens their role), while hiring managers desperately want shortcuts (outcomes over process). This clarifies ICP: target hiring managers directly, not HR gatekeepers.

### Insight 3: Resumes are exhausting and blend together; stories provide differentiation that hiring managers crave

**Evidence:** "The stories you're talking about are a little more interesting, and I think you can derive a more interesting blurb out of it. So if you have other ones that have some issues that don't quite fit... that's one of your strong suits and that's something that's not on a resume. Very often people put their resume bullet points of like, oh, I increased 20% this, and blah, blah. And it all kind of blends together. And those are boring."

**Why it matters:** Stories are proprietary moat—traditional recruiters and ATSs only have resumes, which are commoditized and boring. Careerspan's conversational coaching generates unique signal that hiring managers find compelling and memorable. This validates the core product bet.

### Insight 4: Algorithmic percentage scores feel impersonal and untrustworthy; human-style curation builds trust

**Evidence:** "If I see percentage fit scores, I'm like, an algorithm did this. It might just be bullshit, but if I see like, hey, these are really clear fits. Hey, here are some hidden gems. Don't discount them. Give them a second look. Then I really, I'm a lot more engaged with this list you gave me."

**Why it matters:** Presentation and framing matter as much as technical accuracy. Even if generated by AI, categorizing candidates into "Clear Fits" and "Hidden Gems" feels more trustworthy and human-centric than numeric match scores. This is a UX/positioning insight that affects conversion.

### Insight 5: Job posting age may correlate with hiring manager receptivity (hypothesis to test)

**Evidence:** "One question I would have is, would this approach be more effective for jobs that just got posted because the hiring manager is really engaged, or is it better for jobs that got posted a month ago because the hiring manager's burnt out and wants an easy solution?"

**Why it matters:** Timing of outreach could significantly impact conversion rates. Fresh postings = high attention but less desperation. Old postings = hiring manager fatigue and urgency for shortcuts. This suggests A/B testing outreach timing as key variable.
