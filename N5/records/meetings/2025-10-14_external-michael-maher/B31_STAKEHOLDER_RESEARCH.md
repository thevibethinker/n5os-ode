### Insight 1: Higher Ed institutions buy on risk mitigation and peer validation, not just product quality or price

**Evidence:** "Schools use the ones they've always used or what the other schools use all the time. And it's sort of the easy way out."

**Why it matters:** Even with budget pressure ($20K for "shitty" Vmock) and product dissatisfaction, schools default to familiar vendors because risk aversion dominates decision-making - switching vendors introduces operational risk, team training burden, and potential blame if it fails.

### Insight 3: Budget pressure in Higher Ed creates urgency to cut costs but not necessarily urgency to make decisions - distraction dominates execution

**Evidence:** "Everybody's a little bit distracted. Doesn't excuse [lack of engagement]... we're under a lot of pressure to cut costs" / "We haven't really started this little initiative [for] another few weeks until when the crunch is over."

**Why it matters:** "Budget crisis" doesn't translate to "fast sales cycle" in Higher Ed - institutional inertia, competing priorities, and cyclical workload (recruiting season crunch) mean that even when cost savings are available, decision timelines remain slow. The gap between pain (Vmock costs $20K, product is "shitty") and action (evaluation in "few weeks") can be months.
