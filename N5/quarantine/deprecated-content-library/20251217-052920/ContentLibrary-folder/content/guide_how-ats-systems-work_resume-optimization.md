# How ATS Systems Work - A Primer [Feb 2025]

## Introduction

Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) are the gatekeepers of modern hiring. Understanding how they work is critical for job seekers who want their resumes to reach human reviewers. This guide breaks down ATS functionality across key resume sections and provides actionable optimization strategies.

---

## 1. Resume Bullet Points

### What ATS Systems Look For

#### Keyword Relevance & Synonyms
- Beyond scanning for core terms (like "project management" or "Python"), ATS engines now benefit from natural language processing that recognizes synonyms and related phrases
- Example: "budget oversight" versus "financial planning" may be recognized as related
- **Best Practice:** Tailor each bullet point by mirroring the language in the job posting

#### Standard Formatting
- Use simple, standard bullet symbols (• or -) and avoid complex layouts (tables, columns) that might confuse parsing algorithms

#### Quantifiable Impact & Action Verbs
- Clearly state measurable achievements—like "Increased sales by 30%"
- Use strong action verbs such as "led," "optimized," or "designed"
- **Insight:** This approach improves both keyword matching and impact for human reviewers

### Rewarded Features

✅ **Action-Oriented Language**
- Begin with strong action verbs (e.g., "led," "developed," "optimized")
- Signals achievements and initiative to both ATS and human reviewers

✅ **Quantifiable Achievements**
- Specific metrics (e.g., "Increased revenue by 30%") are favored
- ATS algorithms look for numbers as evidence of impact

✅ **Clarity and Simplicity in Formatting**
- Standard bullet symbols and straightforward formatting (no nested bullets, complex tables)
- Clean, one- or two-line bullet points are more effective than long paragraphs

### Penalized Features

❌ **Vague or Generic Statements**
- "Responsible for managing projects" lacks impact and context
- Penalized by both ATS and human reviewers

❌ **Keyword Stuffing Without Context**
- Overloading a bullet with too many keywords unnaturally is counterproductive
- May trigger "keyword stuffing" filters, lowering your ranking

❌ **Complex or Non-Standard Formatting**
- Fancy symbols, unusual bullet markers, inconsistent punctuation
- Can confuse the ATS parser and result in misinterpretation

### How Keyword Matching Works

**Exact Matching**
- Many ATS systems scan for exact phrases
- If the job description calls for "project management" and your bullet reads "managed projects," there's a chance the system might not match (depending on ATS sophistication)

**Contextual Matching**
- More advanced ATS platforms employ natural language processing (NLP) to understand context
- Example: "optimized workflow efficiency" may be recognized as related to "process improvement"
- This depends on the system's sophistication

**Diminishing Returns**
- There's a sweet spot for keyword frequency
- Including a crucial keyword two to three times is ideal
- Additional instances don't boost your score and might appear forced

### How Synonyms Are Handled

**Exact vs. Semantic Matching**
- Some older/simpler ATS algorithms require exact word matches
- Modern systems with semantic analysis can recognize synonyms and related phrases
- **If uncertain:** Mirror the job description exactly

**Best Practice for Synonyms**
- Include both the exact term and a synonym if it reads naturally
- Example: "Managed a cross-functional team (i.e., led team collaboration)"

## 2. Executive Summaries

### What ATSs Look For

#### Keyword Integration
- Advanced ATS algorithms scan for exact phrases and job-specific keywords from the job description
- Give extra weight to keywords placed near the top of your resume

#### Conciseness and Clarity
- Must be succinct—ideally two to three sentences
- Simple, clear language helps both ATS and human reviewers understand your value

#### Relevance and Context
- ATS checks whether the summary aligns with the job's requirements
- They look for context demonstrating how you've applied relevant skills to drive results

### Do's for a Successful Executive Summary

✅ **Tailor It for Each Application**
- Customize keywords: Read the job description carefully and include exact terms that are repeated
- Highlight relevant achievements: Focus on accomplishments most relevant to the role with quantifiable outcomes
- Example: "Increased online engagement by 35% through targeted content strategies"

✅ **Keep It Concise and Impactful**
- Aim for 2–3 sentences
- Use strong action verbs: "led," "developed," "optimized"

✅ **Present a Clear Value Proposition**
- Explain in one or two lines why you are the ideal candidate for that specific role
- Example: "A seasoned marketing professional with over 8 years of experience in data-driven strategies, I have consistently delivered growth in competitive markets"

✅ **Maintain a Clean, Error-Free Format**
- Proofread for grammatical errors or typos—penalized by both ATS and human recruiters

### Don'ts for the Executive Summary

❌ **Avoid Generic or Overused Phrases**
- Steer clear of clichés like "results-driven professional" or "hardworking team player" without specifics

❌ **Don't Overstuff with Keywords**
- While keywords are important, overloading the summary makes text sound robotic
- Using a keyword two or three times is usually enough

❌ **Avoid Irrelevant Details**
- The summary should highlight only aspects of your background directly relevant to the target job
- Extraneous information lowers your ATS match score

❌ **Don't Copy-Paste the Job Description**
- While important to use the language of the job ad, your summary must tell your personal story

## 3. Education Sections

### What Increases Your ATS Score

✅ **Clear Degree and Field of Study**
- List your full degree name exactly as stated in the job posting
- Example: "Bachelor of Science in Computer Science" instead of "BS in Comp Sci"
- Precise, standardized language helps ATS systems match your qualifications

✅ **Institution Name and Location**
- Include the full name of the institution along with location
- Example: "Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Cambridge, MA"
- Avoid ambiguous abbreviations unless universally recognized

✅ **Graduation Dates (Month/Year)**
- Use consistent date format: either "MM/YYYY" or "Month YYYY"
- Example: "March 2025"
- Month and year are important; year-only can affect ATS calculations

✅ **Academic Honors and Achievements**
- List honors like "cum laude," "Dean's List," or high GPA (typically above 3.5)
- Act as proof of excellence and are key if the job emphasizes academic performance

✅ **Relevant Coursework or Projects (for Recent Graduates)**
- For those with limited work experience, mention relevant coursework or academic research
- Especially valuable if coursework mirrors skills and knowledge needed for the position

✅ **Certifications and Additional Training**
- Use exact titles required by the job description
- Example: "Certified Public Accountant (CPA)" or "Google Analytics Certified"
- Verify expertise and serve as keywords for ATS

### What Can Ding Your ATS Score

❌ **Non-Standard Abbreviations or Incomplete Terms**
- Using "BSc" when the posting specifies "Bachelor of Science" causes mismatches
- Inconsistent naming may cause ATS to overlook your qualification

❌ **Inconsistent or Incorrect Date Formatting**
- Mixing formats (e.g., "03/2023" in one entry and "March 2022" in another) confuses parsing software
- Omitting the month can cause misinterpretation

❌ **Placing Critical Information in Headers or Footers**
- Some ATS systems ignore header/footer text
- Keep education details in the main body of the resume

❌ **Extraneous or Irrelevant Details**
- Including outdated information (high school details when you have a college degree)
- Focus on most recent and relevant credentials for the target role

❌ **Complex Formatting or Graphics**
- Avoid tables, columns, or non-standard formats
- Can disrupt how ATS parses information

## 4. Skills Sections

### Best Practices & General Principles

#### Clarity and Organization
- **Clear Heading:** Use "Skills," "Core Skills," "Technical Skills," or "Soft Skills"
- **Structured Format:** Use a simple list rather than complex tables or graphics

#### Tailoring for the Job
- **Match the Job Description:** Pull exactly the keywords and phrases the employer emphasizes
- **Prioritization:** Place most important and relevant skills near the top—ATS may give more weight to early skills

#### Balance Hard and Soft Skills

**Hard Skills Dominate**
- For technical or specialized fields, hard skills (programming languages, software, certifications) are easier for ATS to recognize and quantify

**Soft Skills Inclusion**
- Include a few core soft skills (like "leadership," "communication") if explicitly mentioned in the job description
- Rather than a laundry list of generic soft skills, focus on those essential to the role and support them in job descriptions
- **Approach:** Weave soft skills into job descriptions for context and evidence

### Do's for a High-Scoring Skills Section

✅ **Be Precise and Relevant**
- Only list skills directly relevant to the target job
- Use exact wording from the job posting

✅ **Group Similar Skills**
- Example: "Programming Languages: Java, Python, C++"
- "Project Management Tools: Jira, Trello"
- Easier for ATS to parse and for recruiters to scan

✅ **Keep It Simple**
- Use bullet points or comma-separated list
- Avoid complex formatting, graphics, or non-standard symbols

✅ **Tailor Each Application**
- Customize your skills section for every job
- Remove irrelevant skills and emphasize matches to increase your "match" score

✅ **Highlight Proficiency Levels (if applicable)**
- Include "Advanced" or "Intermediate" if supported by evidence in your work history

### Don'ts for the Skills Section

❌ **Avoid Keyword Stuffing**
- Don't cram your skills list with every term you've ever encountered
- Looks spammy and may lower your ATS score

❌ **Don't List Irrelevant Skills**
- Resist listing every skill under the sun
- Focus on those directly applicable to the role

❌ **Avoid Vague or Overly Generic Terms**
- "Proficient in communication" without context is less effective
- If soft skills are required, list only those you can substantiate with examples

❌ **Don't Use Non-Standard Formatting**
- Avoid tables, columns, or creative designs that hinder ATS reading

❌ **Don't Repeat Without Purpose**
- Avoid repeating the same skill in different forms ("data analysis" and "analyzing data") unless the job description demands multiple variations

## General ATS Best Practices

### File Format
- Save your resume as **.docx** or **text-based PDF** (avoid image-based files)
- Ensures all content is machine-readable

### Standard Headings
- Use conventional titles: "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills"
- Helps ATS correctly identify and score each section

### Tailoring Your Resume
- Customize for each application by incorporating exact keywords from the job description
- Increases likelihood of matching ATS criteria without keyword stuffing

### Avoiding Complex Formatting
- Refrain from graphics, columns, or tables that disrupt parsing

---

## Conclusion

Applicant Tracking Systems rely on **structured, well-formatted resumes** to extract and evaluate critical information. By ensuring:

- ✅ Bullet points are concise, achievement-oriented, and keyword-rich
- ✅ Executive summary is targeted and succinct
- ✅ Education details are clearly formatted
- ✅ Skills are organized and specific

You increase your chances of **passing ATS filters AND making a strong impression on hiring managers**.

Customizing your resume for each role and adhering to these best practices ultimately **bridges the gap between automated screening and human evaluation**.
