# B31 - STAKEHOLDER RESEARCH

## Landscape Insights from Healthcare Founder Perspective

**Perspective**: Speaking as healthcare AI founder (surgical intelligence / cardiac diagnostics platform)

---

### Key Insight 1: FDA Strategy is Shifting Startup Playbook for Health Tech

**Observation**: "If it's an AI solution, then you should go meet the FDA... it's more of a software solution, which is building as opposed to [hardware devices]"

**Implication**: FDA SaMD (Software as a Medical Device) pathway is now the *preferred* route for health AI startups because: (a) regulatory clarity has improved, (b) approval timelines are faster than hardware (18-24 months vs. 3-5 years), (c) product iteration is faster post-approval

**Strategic Value**: This is a major shift. Health tech founders now have a viable pathway that doesn't require massive capital raise for hardware manufacturing/certification. Attracts more founders to healthcare problem-solving.

---

### Key Insight 2: Institutional Adoption Barriers Are Profession-Specific, Not Patient-Specific

**Observation**: "Radiologists will be replaced. Psychologists replaced. My fear is them. It's not the patients. Patients will have no choice. Decisions that hospitals will make and their investors will make."

**Implication**: AI adoption in healthcare is driven by *institutional economics* not patient outcomes. Hospitals/insurers will adopt AI that saves labor costs; threatened professions (radiologists, psychologists, certain physician types) will resist. But patient choice is largely irrelevant.

**Strategic Value**: Health tech go-to-market must be B2B2C, not B2C. Winner is whoever convinces hospitals/insurers first. Patients follow institutional decisions. This reshapes pricing power, partnership strategy, and sales motion.

---

### Key Insight 3: India as Healthcare AI Goldmine (Data + Market)

**Observation**: "In India... diabetes capital. The diabetes capital. Diabetes is rampant and undiagnosed. We'll have to probably build close to at least 5,000 to 10,000 patients on the database in six months."

**Implication**: India offers three advantages simultaneously: (a) high disease prevalence (unmet diagnostic need), (b) massive patient population available for research, (c) lower cost to operate + faster IRB/ethics approval vs. US

**Strategic Value**: Health AI startups that go India-first can: (a) train models on representative datasets faster, (b) validate product-market fit with lower cash burn, (c) establish distribution partnerships early, (d) then expand back to US/UK with traction. This is a underexploited playbook in health tech.

---

### Key Insight 4: Trust Barrier Remains the 50-100 Year Problem in Healthcare AI

**Observation**: "Unless we have more, you're not going to get better... How are you going to trust me?... I don't think for at least 50-100 years who can you see the manufacturer of the ai i don't just say no we have whatever 90 you know our sensitivity is 99"

**Implication**: Even with 99% sensitivity/specificity, physicians won't *immediately* shift behavior from human intuition to AI recommendation. This is a cultural/cognitive barrier, not a technical one. Adoption requires generational change OR regulatory mandate.

**Strategic Value**: Health tech founders need to price for "augmented intelligence" positioning (not automation replacement) in early markets. Revenue model should be lower initially; upside comes 15-20 years out when new generation of physicians trained on AI-assisted workflows enters workforce.

---

### Key Insight 5: Hospital/Pharma Partnerships Have Extreme Leverage Asymmetry

**Observation**: "My fear is that they will take a large share and you might end up giving away so much that you feel deprived... It's better to own 5% of something that's real than 90% of it."

**Implication**: Large distributors (Meril example), pharma partners, hospital networks have massive leverage in negotiations. Founders often give up outsized equity or IP rights to get market access. The counter-intuition: small ownership of scaled outcome beats large ownership of stalled outcome.

**Strategic Value**: Health tech founders must prioritize *optionality* over immediate revenue. Build defensible IP, prove traction independently, then negotiate from strength. Alternatives: build own distribution (hard), stay indie/regional (lower ceiling), seek smaller partnership partners.

---

### Key Insight 6: Product Sequencing Creates Non-Linear Scaling Opportunity

**Observation**: Discussion of multiple products in pipeline (QuadBio platform → Surgical Intelligence platform → Additional AI modules), each with distinct FDA/regulatory paths

**Implication**: Health tech founder can create a "core platform" with modular AI products. First product gets FDA approval (hard work, 18-24 months), establishing regulatory credibility. Subsequent products are faster to approve because infrastructure is proven.

**Strategic Value**: This creates moat + scale leverage. First product is expensive; later products benefit from same regulatory framework, same hospital relationships, same data pipeline. Founders who think about product sequencing early win faster.

---

### Key Insight 7: Talent/Team Scaling is Execution Bottleneck

**Observation**: "You are kind of the founder and the... the brain behind all of it and then the team of five or six into it [question of how to scale without founder bottleneck]"

**Implication**: Healthcare AI startups are founder-driven in early stage (one person holding the clinical knowledge + technical vision). Scaling requires either: (a) bringing in clinical co-founder, (b) hiring experienced healthcare operations person, (c) partnering with hospital/research institution that has institutional knowledge

**Strategic Value**: Health tech recruiting should prioritize: clinical advisors > engineers > business ops. Founders who solve team scaling early have exponential advantage on product-market fit timelines.
