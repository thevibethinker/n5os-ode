# B01 - DETAILED RECAP

## Key Decisions and Agreements

**1. Surgical Intelligence Platform as Secondary Product**
- Decision: Defer aggressive development of the new surgical intelligence platform until core QuadBio platform achieves traction
- Why it matters: Resource concentration and founder focus are critical constraints; parallel innovation risks both initiatives
- Rationale: "I really want to get the QuadBio platform working first. Once I get this working... then we move to..."
- Implication: 12-18 month runway on QuadBio before surgical intelligence becomes primary focus

**2. AI Remains Proprietary & Housed Internally**
- Decision: AI models and trained datasets will be kept proprietary and secured on company infrastructure (not via third-party vendors)
- Why it matters: Defensibility of intellectual property; prevents employee/partner leakage; maintains competitive moat
- Rationale: "Where does your AI stay? It stays within your drive or your cloud protected by your password. That's all because they are not going to have access to it."
- Implication: Build internal ops capability for AI governance and data security; potential HR/compliance burden

**3. India as Primary Market Expansion & Data Source**
- Decision: Focus initial pilot and data collection in India (10,000+ patient sample in 6 months) with Deewan & Shashir (hospital partners)
- Why it matters: India has massive addressable market (diabetes, undiagnosed cardiac disease); raw data availability; lower cost to operate
- Rationale: "We'll have to probably build close to at least 5,000 to 10,000 patients on the database... in six months. So India is a great challenge."
- Implication: Must establish legal, regulatory, and ethical framework for India deployment; hospital partnership execution will be critical

**4. FDA Strategy: Software as Medical Device (SaMD)**
- Decision: Position solution as FDA-regulated software (not hardware); pursue SaMD pathway for US regulatory approval
- Why it matters: Faster pathway than device hardware; enables feature iteration; more defensible positioning vs. device competitors
- Rationale: "If it's an AI solution, then you should go meet the FDA... it's more of a software solution, which is building as opposed to..."
- Implication: Requires regulatory expertise; timeline ~18-24 months to FDA clearance; must document clinical evidence now

## Strategic Context

### Positioning in Market

The founder is building a **predictive diagnostic AI platform** focused on cardiac and surgical intelligence. The core innovation appears to be: **non-invasive physiological monitoring (pulse wave analysis) + AI interpretation = early detection of vascular disease.**

Current stage: Concept validated, seed-funded or bootstrapped, now transitioning to early clinical data collection. The founder is speaking with confidence about the technology and has attracted early hospital partnerships (Deewan & Shashir's networks).

### Core Problem Being Solved

**Current workflow pain:** Physicians managing ICU/cardiac patients manually interpret multiple signals (blood pressure, a-line readings, biomarkers, echograms) with high cognitive load. Dosing decisions on vasopressors, inotropes, and other medications are subjective and error-prone.

**Proposed solution:** AI-powered vascular disease predictor + dosing recommendation engine, trained on raw physiological data. Reduces physician decision time and improves clinical outcomes.

**Why this matters to Vrijen context:** Potential partnership angle around **decision support in knowledge work** - the AI is essentially augmenting physician expertise the way Careerspan augments career decision-making.

### Key Competitive & Market Dynamics

1. **Regulatory dynamics shift AI incentives**: FDA is now actively encouraging SaMD pathways (regulatory clarity); makes software-first startups more viable vs. hardware-first
2. **Insurance + Hospital politics dominate**: "Radiologists will be replaced. Psychologists replaced. My fear is them. It's not the patients." - Healthcare adoption is institutional, not patient-driven
3. **Trust barrier is massive**: "How are you going to trust me?" - Even with 99% accuracy, physicians won't shift behavior without 50-100 years of cultural change; must position as "augmented intelligence" not replacement
4. **Data governance is existential**: Startups must choose: keep data proprietary (defensible but operationally harder) vs. license to partners (faster revenue, higher leakage risk)
