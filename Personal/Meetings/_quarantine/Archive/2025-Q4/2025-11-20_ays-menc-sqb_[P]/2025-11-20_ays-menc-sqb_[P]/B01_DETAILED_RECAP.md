The conversation opened with a little banter about Hugo-nominated power users while Ilya returned from a quick interruption, then dove into the meat of tomorrow’s demo: Ilse wanted clarity on how much of the conversation should stay in the app versus referring to a Google Doc, especially when prospective customers ask how the model works. The team agreed that the app should remain the star, with the doc reserved for explainability when someone specifically presses for details; Ilse can scroll through narratives, gaps, and stories to point toward explainability without abandoning the visual flow.

They rehearsed the three-act structure (problem → candidate story → payoff) and noted that Act 2 needs to be very brief—just enough to highlight the hoops candidates jump through (assessment criteria, story scores, deal-breakers)—before closing on Act 3, which celebrates the top candidates and the ease of plugging them into the role. Ilya reminded Ilse to keep an eye on the clock, end the demo early if possible, and drop quick diagnostics (e.g., “why does hiring suck for you?” or a battery of short questions) within the first four minutes so they can calibrate the act structure to the prospect’s appetite.

The team rehearsed likely curveball questions: (1) how exactly the assessment is accurate (answer: assessment criteria + documented reasoning + narrative stories), (2) send-me-a-one-pager (Ilya promised to deliver a PDF within an hour of the call), and (3) pricing (the high-level story is small/medium/large buckets, headlined by a generous first 100 candidates free). They also noted compliance sensitivity around showing numeric stack ranks, so any “fit” explanation should use labeled buckets instead of hard scores, while still allowing candidates to see their own numbers.

Near the end, Logan reflected on how the demo should feel like the recruiter doing the heavy lifting, noted that every extra minute of candidate stories transforms into actionable insight, and that the product saves teams from the framing of “cheap ATS nonsense.” Ilse will keep Act 2 tight, highlight deal-breakers, and show how candidate narratives map back to the rubric; Logan will ask diagnostic questions, keep the pace under 30 minutes, and end by saying “here are the top candidates, here’s the link you can publish.” Ilya will finalize the one-pager, keep compliance in mind, and coordinate resume copies and the legal-minded explanation of how the scoring works behind the scenes.
