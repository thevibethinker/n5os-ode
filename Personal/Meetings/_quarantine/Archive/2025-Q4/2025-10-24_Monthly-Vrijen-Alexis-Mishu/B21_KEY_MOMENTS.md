## Cultural/Structural Bias: Big Tech vs. Startup Experience

**Vrijen's Core Warning:**
> "Just know that like just keep an open mind because like frankly even you're not in a position to evaluate that. You haven't worked at a startup either, right? So be aware of that like bias... it's really something I've seen a lot and is why startup founders, when I speak to them, a lot of them refuse to Hire people from big tech. If they don't have at least some startup experience..."

**The Big Tech Trap:** Employees from large companies (Google, Facebook, big tech) bring "structured thinking" optimized for scale and reliability, not startup chaos:

> "They're like a goose that lays a golden egg, or not even. It's the opposite. They lay, like one very reliable bronze egg. And there's a million of them. Right. And they're like, standard is not I need to achieve product market fit. Their standard is I need to fit within the schema."

**Critical Nuance on Mishal's Hire:**
- Uber friend has both: big company structure (AWS/Salesforce experience) + startup-adjacent exposure (limited contracts)
- This hybrid experience is rare and valuable—but still carries big-company thinking patterns
- Vrijen explicitly praised Mishal for being aware of this bias and countering with Uber's "duct tape and glue" reality

## The Incentive Alignment Failure Pattern

**Vrijen's Core Principle:**
> "Generally the folks that know their worth are only committing what they are worth. You know what I mean?"

**Applied to Mishal's Adviser Model:**
> "I have a lot of concerns with that model. You're doing the same thing again... because you've done this with other guy in Canada... I would just be wary of like a hundred percent of nothing is nothing."

**The Reality:** Even brilliant advisers without equity or compensation will deprioritize a startup when their own job becomes more interesting or demanding. The incentive structure doesn't create accountability.

**Mishal's Risk:** The Uber engineer is self-admittedly bored with his day job and treating this as a "cool side project." This is the exact pattern Vrijen warns against—when primary incentive disappears (boredom factor), so does reliable participation.

**Mishal's Discovery:**

**Vrijen's Reframe:**
