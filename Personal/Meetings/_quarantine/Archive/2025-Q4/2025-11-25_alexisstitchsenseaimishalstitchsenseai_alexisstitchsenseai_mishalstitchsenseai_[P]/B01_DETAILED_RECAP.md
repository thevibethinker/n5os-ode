## Fundraising Instruments – Education & Tradeoffs

### SAFEs
- V explains **SAFEs (Simple Agreements for Future Equity)** as the default, founder-friendly instrument in the US:
  - Very lightweight (≈ five-page document), cheap to set up, and can have money in the account within a week.
  - Investor gives money now; equity is calculated and issued later at a **priced equity round or liquidity event**.
- **Key risk:** "stacking" SAFEs on varying terms without modeling the cumulative effect.
  - Dilution does not appear gradually; it hits all at once when SAFEs convert.
  - Founders can easily go from ~50% ownership to ~30% without realizing it if they keep signing SAFEs.
- V strongly recommends **basic cap table modeling** before each SAFE:
  - Make the eventual % ownership of each investor under different round valuations explicit.
  - Use the modeling as a discipline to avoid over-dilution.

### Priced Rounds (Equity Rounds)
- V describes a priced round as more **expensive, procedural, and legally heavy** than SAFEs.
- Example: taking money from Indian investors required a **full priced round** because of India’s overseas investment rules/FDI regime.
  - That drove legal costs to roughly **tens of thousands of dollars** instead of a few hundred for SAFEs.
- Takeaway: **priced rounds** are often unavoidable when dealing with certain jurisdictions but should not be a founder’s first choice if cheaper instruments are possible.

### Convertible Notes
- V briefly outlines **convertible notes**:
  - Structured initially as **debt that can convert into equity** later.
  - Investors may have the option to take repayment as a loan or as equity, depending on terms and outcomes.
- He notes that **he hasn’t used them extensively**, and that in India they likely weren’t practical or allowed in the way his lawyers advised.

### Geography & Jurisdiction

  - One structural advantage of being globally oriented is the ability to **raise from non‑US investors** who have less access to AI/factory optimization deal flow and may accept **higher valuations**.

## Timing and Strategy of the Raise

### Momentum-Driven Window
- V encourages them to **time the first serious raise** to a moment when they can show:
  - A **wave of new revenue** coming off the back of V1.
  - Clear evidence that the product is solving factories’ "most amazing problems" and converting existing LOIs.
  - Optionally a **press release** or other visible signal (even if a bit "BSE"/PR‑ish) to create a sense of momentum.
- He suggests **stacking multiple proof points in the same quarter**:
  - Revenue growth.
  - New contracts.
  - Strong pipeline metrics.
  - External validation (e.g., PR, case studies).

### Time Compression & FOMO
- V shares a specific playbook for creating investor urgency:
  1. **Now until target month (e.g., February):**
     - Have many light conversations with potential angels/VCs.
     - Consistently answer "Are you raising?" with **"No"** and "We’ll let you know when we are".
     - Build a list of **50–80 interested parties** who want to hear when a raise opens.
  2. **In the target month:**
     - Announce that the "gates are open" for a **short window (e.g., two weeks)**.
     - Share a concise data pack: revenue over last quarters, pipeline, factory case studies, V1 performance, PR links.
     - Specify:
       - Desired **round size**.
       - **Minimum ticket size**.
       - **Valuation (cap or price)**.
       - Exactly what data they’ll get (data room, metrics) and no more.
     - Make it crystal clear: **"In or out within the window"**.
- The intention is to:
  - **Compress time** so investors feel urgency instead of endless optionality.
  - Position Alexis & Misha as the **"hottest company at the party"** rather than supplicants.
  - Maintain a **psychological advantage**: investors interpret structure and confidence as competence.

## Valuation Philosophy
- V emphasizes that startup valuations, especially at pre‑seed/seed, are **forward-looking commitments**, not historical facts.
- He coaches Alexis to:
  - Start from a **credible revenue target** for the next 12 months and apply a multiple.
  - Recognize that early-stage multiples can be **very high** (far above public SaaS multiples) because risk is higher and markets are less liquid.
  - Understand that the valuation set in this round becomes the **bar they must clear** in the next round.
- Example logic he walks through:
  - If you expect to reach **$100k annualized revenue**, a notional **10x–50x multiple** can support valuation in the low‑ to mid‑seven figures.
  - But if the future priced round lands below that cap, founders experience both **lower cash** and **worse dilution** than expected.
- Key point: **Don’t choose a vanity valuation**; choose one you can plausibly justify and deliver against.

## Lessons from Careerspan’s Fundraising
- Before raising:
  - V wishes he had a **much clearer deployment plan** for the capital than "we will hire people".
- After taking a **$1M check from a family friend in India**:
  - Felt **intense stress** around cash burn and investor expectations.
  - Regrets not having **more optionality** (i.e., other term sheets or investors to compare) before accepting that capital.
  - Learned how important it is to choose **"non–pain-in-the-ass" investors** who will largely let founders execute.
  - Noted how much of fundraising is **investor psychology**: once money is in, investors feel entitled to look into the company at any time.
- Later attempts to raise:
  - Struggled because the pitch did not sufficiently explain **how investors would get their money back** (clear exit path and return profile).
  - Observed that founders often obsess about proving the **product and mission**, but investors mainly want to know **how capital compounds and returns**.

- Alexis has heard **mixed and often negative feedback** on YC and similar accelerators (e.g., Antler); perceives them as "you get out what you put in" and questions their practical value for factory owners in Southeast Asia.
- V’s nuanced view:
  - **Pros:**
    - Brand and clout: being YC‑backed can make them the **default "factory optimization" company** in the eyes of tech/investor ecosystems.
    - Strong network of founders and alumni; V notes that his **YC founder network** is now unexpectedly valuable for his own future options.
    - Potential to **de-risk careers** – even if the company fails, YC credentials and network maintain or increase founders’ long-term optionality.
  - **Cons / limitations for Stitch Sense AI:**
    - YC will not directly produce **factory-owner customers**; many in their target region may not know what YC is.
    - Dilution could be **largely for signaling rather than distribution**.
- They align roughly on a **"maybe later, if clearly strategic"** stance:
  - YC is not obviously necessary for customer acquisition.
  - It could be strategically useful for brand and optionality, but only if Alexis & Misha believe that benefit outweighs dilution and distraction.
