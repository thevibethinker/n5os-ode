# B01 – Detailed Recap

The meeting is an informal but focused co‑founder working session between Vrijen and Logan, centered on short‑term financial pressure, product monetization options, and relationship‑driven business development.

They open with light personal check‑in. Vrijen arrives slightly late while booking a TaskRabbit to install a bidet, explaining the risk calculus of avoiding a potentially expensive plumbing disaster by paying a small fixed fee. This sets a tone of pragmatic risk management that echoes later in the discussion about company finances.

Vrijen shares quick operational updates: a productive alignment call with Ilsa on the upcoming Sapphire demo, positive momentum around “the stuff tomorrow,” and follow‑up emails sent to Conrad and Marvin. Both acknowledge that around winter, prospects drag their feet and sales cycles slow down. They briefly entertain the possibility of a seasonal lull and the need to potentially tilt focus toward candidates rather than employers if client motion stalls.

Logan then raises the core strategic question of the session: whether to introduce some form of paywall for candidates to help cover costs. She notes that, while she would prefer to keep the product free, it may not be realistic given current burn. Vrijen acknowledges he’s amenable in principle but flags uncertainty about how much revenue it would actually generate and highlights that existing costs are not primarily driven by candidate usage.

They explore different monetization patterns. Logan suggests a more conventional paywall on the existing product—e.g., a model where applications remain free but stories or higher‑value usage is metered or paid after a few free actions. Vrijen pushes on implementation details, suggesting patterns like “three stories free” or usage‑based constraints, while noting that Careerspan has never yet implemented usage‑based pricing. Both are wary of spending limited engineering capacity on billing infrastructure that may not justify its cost.

This leads into a broader conversation about financial runway. Vrijen mentions recent payments to Ilya and Rahul and describes the current cash picture as “not looking so great” in the near term. He is, however, expecting additional money in the next few days, is exploring business loans, and is coordinating with his father to ensure there is a guaranteed solution by the new year. The challenge is bridging to that point without unnecessary risk. They also consider practical levers like asking Megan to accept payment in January under their existing contract to ease short‑term cash pressure.

Logan brings in context from a conversation with Alice. Alice’s view is that Careerspan will always have far more candidates than clients, and that job seekers will pay something for real value, even if not a large amount. Logan finds this persuasive and sees potential in leaning into candidate‑side monetization, especially during a period when companies pause hiring and decisions until January. She worries, though, about “death by a thousand cuts”—acquiring large numbers of free users without any monetization ballast.

From here, Vrijen sketches a more ambitious, subscription‑style offering: a “Careerspan jobs” or “Careerspan Select” concept where candidates pay a monthly fee to be auto‑applied to curated opportunities and moved to the top of the queue. The value proposition is a “set it and forget it” service—around $9/month as an illustrative price point—for people who are effectively always job‑seeking in today’s climate. He notes Alice’s observation that the old distinction between passive and active job seekers has collapsed; everyone is actively anxious and open to new roles, which makes a standing subscription particularly compelling.

Logan likes the business model but points out the operational load: they would need payments infrastructure, some mechanism (even if initially manual) to apply on candidates’ behalf, and a system to continuously check new jobs against subscribers. She argues that this stack is roughly three layers of product development and operational effort—more demanding than simply adding a basic paywall to the existing product. Vrijen agrees that the subscription model is strategically attractive but may need to be staged; they tentatively align on first understanding the quickest possible implementation of a simple paywall and then working toward the richer subscription model over time.

The conversation then shifts into relationship and distribution strategy. Logan mentions being tapped by Aisha to film a LinkedIn reel (a product demo), along with Meta ads already set up and sales collateral she has built in Canva. Vrijen sees an opportunity to link Aisha’s considerable online reach with Zo’s interest in AI‑friendly audiences. He asks whether Logan would be open to facilitating an introduction between Aisha and the Zo team, suggesting that her audience could be a strong match for “hot consumer AI” and that Zo might even host something like an AI‑influencer dinner in New York. Logan agrees to raise this on her upcoming call with Aisha and to float the idea.

They also briefly handle operational follow‑ups: Logan reminds Vrijen about “Future Fit Guy,” prompting Vrijen to take responsibility and commit to emailing him immediately and moving related scheduling. Logan outlines additional marketing tasks—moving other calls, filming for Instagram, pushing the Meta ads live, and following up on previous outreach.

The second major strategic thread is business development via VC‑adjacent platforms and relationships. They dwell on Getro, which Ilsa had mentioned—a VC‑focused job board rather than a generic platform. Vrijen explains why Getro is a particularly good fit: while a standalone VC job board is not obviously a VC‑backable business, a VC‑backed startup like Careerspan that adds a conversational sourcing layer could be. He sees Getro as structurally aligned with Careerspan’s model and a promising partner or customer.

They explore paths to Getro’s CEO, Evan Walden. Using LinkedIn and shared network knowledge, Vrijen notes he now has “shitloads” of credible connections compared with two years ago—people who will vouch for him rather than merely being loose acquaintances. Names like Jamie Farrell, Eric Lavin, Andy Moat, Jacob Asher, Chris Russell, and others surface as possible connectors. Logan also volunteers that a strong contact at Radancy is connected to Evan and offers to ping her, which would both push the Getro intro forward and serve as a good excuse to reconnect.

Along the way, they discuss other high‑value connectors, including Megan Curtis McKenna (FIF Collective) and Netta (an Israeli B2B advertising/VC‑connected figure). Vrijen is interested in deepening those relationships, possibly through targeted outreach that leverages prior positive interactions—such as Megan’s generosity at an event where she immediately introduced him to someone strong at Betaworks. There is a recurring theme of systematically converting a now robust personal network into structured, high‑leverage intros for Careerspan.

The tone throughout is informal, sardonic, and warm, with humor about Mormon suitors, “sister wife for the company,” and age cutoffs for the Sandbox community, but underneath there is serious work: triaging financial constraints, shaping monetization strategy, and architecting a BD pipeline around specific people and platforms.

They close without a formal summary but with clear directional alignment: in the near term, get clarity on a pragmatic candidate‑facing paywall, keep pushing active marketing experiments (Meta ads, LinkedIn content, Instagram), follow through on key relationship‑driven intros (Aisha ↔ Zo, Getro/Evan via multiple connectors, Megan/Netta and similar), and continue refining the longer‑term subscription model that could turn the anxiety of today’s job market into a sustainable, recurring‑revenue engine for Careerspan.
