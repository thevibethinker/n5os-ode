### Pattern 2: "Latent Signal" vs "Hidden" vs "Something That Can't Be Brute Forced"

**Instances**:
- "Something hidden that you're not going to get by brute forcing to understanding"
- "Ability to hold a conversation is actually pretty indicative"
- Referenced roles where "keywords don't surface the right people"

**Semantic meaning**: Edmund distinguishes between:
1. **Explicit signals**: Resume credentials, job titles (legible, searchable, already commoditized)
2. **Latent signals**: Capabilities revealed through engagement but not explicitly claimed (defensible moat)

His language suggests he sees Careerspan in category #2, which is why partnership interests him.

**Why it matters**: When pitching pilot roles, frame everything as "latent signal discovery" not "better sourcing." The value is revealing non-obvious candidates, not accessing larger databases.

**Messaging implication**: Don't compete on candidate volume ("we have access to 10,000 engineers"). Compete on signal quality ("we reveal capabilities invisible on LinkedIn").

### Pattern 4: "Traffic Light Not Thermometer" = Categorical Thinking Over Comparative

**Instance**: "Red, yellow, or green... let's not pretend we know"

**Semantic meaning**: Edmund rejects false precision in evaluation. This maps to broader epistemological humility - he's comfortable with uncertainty and prefers honest uncertainty to confident wrongness.

**Why it matters**: When proposing metrics for pilot success, avoid overly precise targets ("increase conversion by 23.7%"). Use categorical outcomes:

**Strategic implication**: This mindset reduces pressure on pilot to deliver perfect metrics. Edmund likely okay with "learned something valuable" even if commercial outcome unclear.
