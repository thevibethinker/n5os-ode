# B31: Stakeholder Research

**Perspective:** Speaking as a career tech founder with 10 years of coaching experience, running a founder community in the hiring space.

## Insight 1: Founders are abandoning inbound applications entirely

**Evidence:** "I know multiple very successful founders, big companies don't look at inbound anymore. You know, if they look at inbound, they'll look at the first hundred people, right? What's that old finance joke like, you take half the pile, put it in the trash can."

**Why it matters:** This reveals a hidden crisis in hiring that's not publicly discussed - high-quality applicants are being systematically ignored because the volume has completely overwhelmed traditional screening methods. The "throw half a pile in the trash" approach is becoming standard practice, meaning candidates are wasting massive effort on applications that are never seen.

**Signal Strength:** ● ● ● ● ○ (4/5)
- Rating rationale: High specificity (actual behavior observed among successful founders), not publicly known, actionable - indicates a fundamental market breakdown. Verifiable with further research.

## Insight 2: The resume paradigm is fundamentally adversarial and broken

**Evidence:** "I think the big fucking problem right now is that everyone is accepting the current paradigm, which is resume based and is essentially adversarial between the candidate and the employer... The conversion rate just from like applicants to interviewees can be under 1%."

**Why it matters:** This identifies the root cause of the hiring market paradox - both sides want the same outcome (hire/get hired) but the system creates an adversarial relationship where success requires gaming the system rather than authentic value exchange. Under 1% conversion is worse than getting into Harvard, indicating systemic failure.

**Signal Strength:** ● ● ● ○ ○ (3/5)
- Rating rationale: The adversarial frame is a powerful insight, but the "everyone accepts" claim is generalizable. The under 1% stat is specific and alarming.

## Insight 3: AI is creating 10x employees, not just 10x engineers

**Evidence:** "There are going to be more overpowered 10x employees of the future. It's not going to be 10x engineers, it's going to be more 10x employees. And like a 10 person billion dollar company is like how it's going to go... For every business there is a theoretical optimal team size... AI is basically compressing the size of that."

**Why it matters:** This reframes the hiring conversation from "finding engineers" to "finding AI-augmented employees." The fundamental constraint of team size (minimum people needed to coordinate and execute) is being broken by AI, enabling radically lean organizations. This changes who to hire, how many to hire, and what roles matter.

**Signal Strength:** ● ● ● ○ ○ (3/5)
- Rating rationale: Directional insight about future work structure, not yet verified but highly actionable for product thinking. The "optimal team size" concept is sound and the AI compression thesis is compelling.

## Insight 4: Values/mindset mismatch drives 90% of hiring failures

**Evidence:** "46% of hires fail by the 18 month mark. 90% of the reason they fail is a values mindset or soft skills mismatch, right? So like really we're fucking excellent fucking excellent at assessing whether someone can do the job... And we are horrible at assessing whether someone will actually do the job well at this company as part of this team."

**Why it matters:** This is a massive product opportunity gap - everyone is building tools to assess technical skills when 90% of failures are cultural fit. There's essentially no one working on the actual problem that causes most hiring failures.

**Signal Strength:** ● ● ● ● ● (5/5)
- Rating rationale: Specific data point (46% failure rate, 90% values mismatch), directly contradicts market behavior (everyone building technical assessment), identifies clear white space opportunity.
