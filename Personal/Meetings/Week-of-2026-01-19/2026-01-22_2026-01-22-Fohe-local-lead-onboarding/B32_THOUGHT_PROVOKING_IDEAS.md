# B32: Thought Provoking Ideas

**Meeting:** FOHE Local Lead Onboarding  
**Date:** 2026-01-22

## Theme 1: The Learning Organization Manifesto

**Core Idea:** Every rule in FOHE's playbook exists because someone broke something.

**Context:** V remarked, "Every rule is a fuck up, right?" during the onboarding. Ray confirmed: "That document is a bit scattershot bullet points, but those are all lessons we learned."

**Provocative Question:** How many organizations systematically encode their failures into repeatable success, versus hiding mistakes and repeating them?

**Deeper Implication:** FOHE's onboarding isn't just training — it's a distributed risk management system. Every new local lead receives not just instructions, but a catalog of problems already solved. This is institutional memory as competitive advantage.

**Paradox:** The more comprehensive the rulebook, the scarier it looks — but actually, it's safer because those mistakes won't happen to you. The thoroughness V observed isn't bureaucracy; it's pre-paid tuition.

## Theme 2: 80% Inactive as Strategic Asset

**Core Idea:** FOHE turns a major engagement problem (80% Slack users inactive) into a precise targeting opportunity.

**Context:** Ray explained: "Roughly 80% of people in Slack are not active — that's why email is important. If you can see who's active, it gives you a proxy for who's most engaged with FOHE. If you're trying to find a sponsor, figure out who has a decision-maker title at a company headquartered in your city and who's also active — they're likely to get the most value."

**Provocative Question:** What if low engagement isn't a failure, but a segmentation mechanism?

**Deeper Implication:** FOHE is building a three-tier engagement model:
- **Active 20%** → High-value targets for sponsorship, leadership, partnership
- **Passive 60%** → Email subscribers, occasional event attendees
- **Inactive 20%** → Dormant database requiring re-engagement or cleanup

Most organizations panic about low engagement and try to fix it. FOHE is systematically measuring it, segmenting by it, and monetizing it (via sponsorships). The proprietary database will automate this targeting.

**Insight:** Inactivity isn't a bug — it's a filter. The people who show up despite friction are your true community.

## Theme 3: Volunteer Compensation as Sustainability Engine

**Core Idea:** FOHE is inventing a new economic model that transforms volunteers from exploited labor to value-capturing stakeholders.

**Context:** Ray explained: "We're not paying you — but we're working with lawyers to figure out a way that if you raise sponsorship money (e.g., $10,000/year), the first portion goes toward your activities as needed, some portion becomes a stipend for you, and some portion goes back to FOHE global to support the program and staff."

**Provocative Question:** What happens when unpaid volunteers have financial upside tied to their impact?

**Deeper Implication:** Traditional nonprofits exploit volunteers through moral suasion ("you should do this for the cause"). FOHE is creating a hybrid model:
- **Volunteer commitment:** 30-60 minutes/week, 1-3 years
- **Entrepreneurial upside:** Raise sponsorship money, keep a portion
- **Career development:** Build leadership, event planning, community skills

This aligns incentives in a way pure altruism doesn't. A local lead who raises $10,000 doesn't just feel good — they might earn a stipend that makes their volunteer time sustainable.

**Risk/Opportunity:** This could transform local leads from passive managers to aggressive networkers and sponsors. It also introduces sales pressure into volunteer work. FOHE is walking a fine line between empowering volunteers and exploiting them for revenue.

**Broader Question:** Is this the future of distributed organizations? Small stakes, aligned incentives, but not full employment?
