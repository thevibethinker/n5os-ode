# B32: Thought-Provoking Ideas

## Idea #1: The Collapse of Proxy-Based Assessment

**The Insight:** Traditional systems for evaluating human potential - resumes, standardized tests, technical interviews, credit scores - are all proxies. They measure easily quantifiable things because that's what technology has historically allowed. But these proxies are increasingly becoming meaningless.

**Why It Matters Now:** AI can generate perfect resumes, ace algorithm memorization tests, and produce convincing but hollow outputs. The signal-to-noise ratio has collapsed. Keywords on a resume are no longer a proxy for skills; they're just text that AI can arrange in any order for free.

**The Question:** If all our proxy-based assessment systems are broken, what replaces them? The answer appears to be direct observation of authentic behavior and self-reflection - things that are harder to fake at scale but potentially more revealing when captured thoughtfully. But this raises a deeper question: Are we prepared for a world where assessment requires understanding someone's thinking process rather than their outputs?

**The Tension:** Authentic assessment requires more time, more intimacy, and more sophisticated interpretation. It's the opposite of scalable, efficient, automated systems. How do we build assessment infrastructure that scales without losing the authenticity that makes it valuable?

## Idea #2: Liquid UI and the End of Product Stability

**The Insight:** What if every application you use becomes a "starting point" rather than a finished product? What if the boundaries between "user" and "developer" blur to the point where every user has the power to reshape the tools they use daily?

**Why It Matters Now:** AI makes it possible to modify interfaces through natural language. You don't need to code; you just describe what you want to change. The technical barrier to customization drops from high to near-zero.

**The Question:** What happens to product design when users can modify everything? Designers craft experiences based on assumptions about user needs and mental models. But if users can and do modify those experiences, what does "good design" even mean anymore?

**The Tension:** This isn't just about personalization - it's about control. Who owns the product experience? The company that built it, or the user who uses it? If I modify Twitter to never show me tweets with the word "excited," am I still using Twitter? What are the implications for advertising, engagement metrics, and the fundamental economics of free software?

**The Deeper Question:** Maybe the future isn't liquid UI at all. Maybe it's about finding the right balance between stability and mutability. Some interfaces should change; others shouldn't. The challenge isn't making everything mutable - it's knowing what should stay fixed.

## Idea #3: Information Edges and Privacy as Competitive Advantage

**The Insight:** In a world where AI can train on everything, the only valuable knowledge is what you keep private. The moment your preferences, behaviors, or insights become training data, they become commodities available to everyone with access to that AI.

**Why It Matters Now:** Personalization systems increasingly rely on training models on user data. But paradoxically, the more personalized the system becomes, the more it dilutes the competitive advantage of that personalization - because the model learns patterns from everyone, making everyone's personalization less unique.

**The Question:** What does it mean for a product to both personalize deeply AND protect user data as a competitive advantage? These seem like opposing forces. Personalization requires learning about users; protection requires NOT learning (or not sharing what you've learned).

**The Tension:** The companies that win in personalization may be those who can say: "We know you deeply, we optimize for you specifically, but we never use your data to help anyone else." This is technically challenging but potentially valuable. It suggests a future where the best products are those with the most private, most proprietary understanding of their users.

**The Deeper Question:** Is it even possible to build AI systems that learn individual patterns without generalizing them? Can we create personalization that doesn't require training on aggregated data? If not, maybe the future of personalization isn't about AI at all - it's about deterministic rules, user-owned preferences, and systems that remember without learning.
