# B01 Detailed Recap

## Overview
Vrijen Attawar (V) met with Anthony DeLuise to discuss Anthony’s career transition and his background in EdTech. Anthony recently left his company, VisitDays, after 11 years to focus on fatherhood and is now exploring his "next play." The conversation touched on the challenges of the EdTech market, the limitations of current career tools, and the role of high agency in job hunting.

## Key Discussion Points
- **Introductions and Personal Connection**: The two connected through the "Next Play" and "Fohi" groups. They shared a moment discussing their cats (Anthony has Katniss and Keanu; V has a "standard issue" tabby).
- **VisitDays and EdTech Market**: Anthony described VisitDays as an "OS for campus recruiting," providing a full stack of events, tours, and communications that standard CRMs/ERPs lacked. They discussed the difficulty of selling to universities, noting the disconnect between high student UX expectations and low buyer standards.
- **Higher Education and ROI**: They explored the changing sentiment around higher education, with parents increasingly demanding ROI data. Anthony noted that while long-term ROI for a four-year degree is high, immediate ROI is harder to quantify via standard six-month surveys.
- **Systemic Career Issues**: V expressed his view that the current hiring system (the "application meat grinder") is fundamentally broken. He emphasized that networking is a short-term fix but that the system itself requires reform.
- **Agency and Job Hunting**: V highlighted "high agency" as a critical trait for job seekers. He described job hunting as a perishable skill that needs refreshing every few years.
- **Careerspan and Career Coaching**: V encouraged Anthony to use Career Span to record his achievements and conduct an "inventory" of his capabilities. This helps shift the search from a broad, undefined direction to one focused on achievable goals.
- **Critique of AI Career Tools**: Anthony shared his skepticism of apps that automate applications or cold DMs, arguing that for experienced professionals (5+ years out), these problems aren't "real" problems compared to the value of networking and personal research.
