# B01: Detailed Recap

## Context & Connection
Aniket and Vrijen connected via warm intro from Andy (mutual LinkedIn connection from commenting on same posts). Both founders in recruiting/talent acquisition space. Vrijen had just returned from Labor Day weekend in Monroe, NY; Aniket based in Seattle.

## Aniket's Business Overview
**Problem Space:** Recruiting and talent acquisition - specifically solving sourcing pain points for recruiters

**Solution:** Two-sided platform:
- **Recruiter side (monetized):** Sourcing tools that save hours/days by filtering out fake candidates and mass applications. Positioning as "buying back time" with quality guarantees.
- **Candidate side (free):** Profile creation using video to tell their story, single unified profile aggregating all experience (technical: GitHub, blogs, etc.; non-technical: other credentials). Goal is helping candidates "stand out from the crowd" and land interviews.

**Business Model:** Recruiters pay for time savings and vetted candidates. Candidates use platform free. Unclear on specific pricing but positioned as "cheap way to buy back time."

**Traction:** Achieved "organic traction" that led to doubling down on this idea over other concepts Aniket explored.

**Stage:** Pre-funding (no funding mentioned), testing monetization approach.

**Philosophy:** Critical of mass-apply tools (mentions apply.com specifically) as creating "big mess" in hiring ecosystem. Focused on quality over volume.

## Vrijen's Business Overview (Careerspan)
**Evolution:** 2.5 years in. Started as consumer coaching product focused on "self-reflection" to help job seekers. Pivoted recently (past 3 weeks) to B2B matching platform.

**Pivot Rationale:** Consumer willingness-to-pay problem - "self-reflection" is valuable but painful, hard to monetize unless immediate value is obvious (gave example: mass-apply tools can charge $50 because they provide instant dopamine hit of "I applied to 50 jobs").

**Current Approach:**
- Focus on candidate experience first (coaching-based data collection)
- Use expanded data set from coaching to build superior matching system
- Sell curated candidate lists directly to companies (not recruiters initially)
- Target ICPs: Founders looking for devs, hiring managers

**Technology Play:** Strength of matching system as differentiator - "technological play at the end of the day"

**Monetization:** Employer-side only, no candidate fees. Positioning as recruiter alternative by offering speed and quality.

**Early Traction:** 3 weeks into B2B approach:
- Multiple companies posting jobs exclusively on Careerspan
- 30-50% improvement in engagement from candidates receiving tailored recommendations
- "Just need to connect these little pieces and we're there"

**Funding:** Raised $1M pre-seed last year + $200K extension from existing investors. Goal: Get "sizable revenue through the door in next two months" to extend runway.

**Anti-spam Stance:** Strongly against mass-apply tools ("I think they suck" / "they're a menace"). Currently speaking with auto-apply companies to "keep lines of communication open" but maintains critical perspective.

## Market Context Discussion

**Recruiter Economics:**
- Traditional contingency fees: 25% → now 20% of first year base
- Still takes "multiple months" to place candidates
- Market experiencing layoffs in recruiting function

**Market Opportunity:**
- Hiring slowdown + smaller teams = recruiter starvation
- Creates openness to new tools and process changes
- "More openness to new shit" than in stable markets
- Pressure on contingency model creates opportunity for disintermediation

## Shared Perspectives

1. **Ecosystem Pollution:** Both see mass-apply tools as harmful to hiring ecosystem
2. **Monetization Strategy:** Both monetize on employer/recruiter side, not candidates
3. **Respect for Recruiter Time:** Both designing solutions that don't add to spam problem
4. **Quality over Volume:** Neither pursuing spray-and-pray approach to applications
5. **Market Timing:** Both see current conditions as opportunity for process innovation

## Strategic Differences

**Customer Target:**
- Aniket: Serves recruiters directly (makes their job easier)
- Vrijen: Targets hiring managers/founders (potential recruiter disintermediation)

**Market Entry:**
- Aniket: Recruiter-first, organic traction-driven
- Vrijen: Consumer product pivot to B2B after 2.5 years

**Stage:**
- Aniket: Earlier, pre-funding, exploring pricing
- Vrijen: Raised capital, in pivot phase with early validation

## Tone & Dynamics
Collegial, exploratory conversation between founders in adjacent/complementary spaces. No competitive tension. Both transparent about challenges and approaches. Aniket more inquisitive; Vrijen more explanatory about journey and strategic rationale. Mutual respect evident in discussion of each other's approaches.

## Technical/Business Insights Shared

**Vrijen on Consumer Monetization:** Clear articulation of why consumer products are hard in this space - unless value is immediate and measurable, people won't pay. "Small gap" between problem being painful enough to act on and immediate pain of solution is "where all the difference lies."

**Market Structure:** Acknowledgment that recruiters "in general are in a tough spot" with hiring decline and layoffs. Creates both opportunity and risk for recruiter-facing vs. recruiter-replacing solutions.

**Unit Economics:** Vrijen mentioned people "run away from" better matching because "unit economics of it are not enviable," but believes "that is what the market wants."
