# B13: Risks & Opportunities

## Strategic Opportunities

The primary opportunity lies in building an AI-powered learning coach and mentor for fitness professionals progressing through ACE's certification programs. This could take multiple forms: just-in-time learning support that provides specific content segments when users need them (such as explaining planes of movement or demonstrating exercises), role-playing scenarios where learners practice approaching different client situations, and adaptive learning that builds on previous knowledge over time. Vrijen's demonstration of Zo's capabilities revealed a compelling opportunity to integrate with external data sources—such as fitness trackers, wearable devices, or health platforms—to create highly contextualized learning experiences. The most significant strategic advantage, however, may come from shifting to a dynamic two-way relationship with learners rather than a one-way information delivery model. By maintaining learner profiles that compound knowledge over time and understanding individual needs through conversation, ACE could create a differentiated experience that adapts to each professional's journey. There's also an opportunity to leverage ACE's existing behavior coaching and fitness methodologies by combining these proven approaches with AI capabilities, potentially scaling human coaching principles to support learners at any time.

## Strategic Risks

A major risk is over-engineering the technical solution—Vrijen emphasized that LLMs are likely sufficient for most use cases and that custom models, fine-tuning, or PhD-level ML expertise may be unnecessary expenditures that could sink the project before it proves value. The hiring risk is substantial; Vrijen shared that they made a critical mistake selecting a co-founder who was managerially credentialed but far removed from hands-on building, and this is a common failure mode for non-technical founders. Without strong internal technical leadership, there's risk that external development partners could deliver what was requested rather than what was needed, with no accountability on ACE's side. There's also a financial risk around unit economics—Vrijen noted that 80% of Careerspan's costs went to salaries, but AI operational costs can escalate quickly. Many companies start with unlimited AI subscriptions, shift to usage-based pricing when costs spike, and then see adoption tank. Finally, there's a strategic risk in scope creep: building learner profiles and integrating external data sources adds significant complexity, and ACE should validate core value propositions before expanding into data-intensive features that may not drive retention.

## Risk-Opportunity Trade-offs

The most significant trade-off centers on technical talent sourcing. Vrijen advised finding a technical product leader in-house to corral development, but senior US talent commands salaries of $300k+, which may be excessive for an unproven initiative. Logan suggested an alternative: partnering with firms that source high-caliber developers from India at $100-150k with similar technical prowess. This presents a trade-off between accessibility, cultural alignment, and time zone considerations versus significant cost savings that could extend runway for experimentation. Another trade-off is between data integration capabilities and user privacy concerns—creating a comprehensive learner profile that pulls from external sources could deliver powerful personalization but raises questions about data consent, security, and user comfort. Finally, there's a trade-off between moving quickly with a minimal viable product versus waiting for more sophisticated AI capabilities. Vrijen emphasized that pedagogy and product intuition matter more than AI engineering, suggesting ACE should prioritize solving real learner problems with simple, reliable AI interactions rather than chasing advanced features that may not deliver proportional value. The opportunity lies in being first-to-market with a genuinely useful AI coach, but the risk is building something technologically impressive that doesn't actually improve learning outcomes or retention.
