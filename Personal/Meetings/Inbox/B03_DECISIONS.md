# B03: Decisions Made

## Decision 1: AI Strategy Direction for ACE Fitness

**DECISION:** ACE Fitness will focus AI investment on creating a "study coach or mentor" capability for learners, rather than exploring other AI applications.

**CONTEXT:** Merisenda described ACE's thinking around AI use cases - specifically leveraging AI as a study coach to support users at various stages of their career journey. The strategy centers on creating a content repository for just-in-time learning segments and progressive coaching through role-playing and case scenarios.

**DECIDED BY:** Merisenda Alatorre (representing ACE Fitness's internal strategy direction)

**IMPLICATIONS:** Product development will focus on content delivery, learner support, and personalized coaching experiences rather than other potential AI applications. This provides clear direction for MVP scoping.

**ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED:** None explicitly mentioned in the transcript.

## Decision 2: In-House Technical Leadership Requirement

**DECISION:** ACE Fitness needs to hire at least one strong technical product lead in-house to guide AI development, even if outsourcing engineering work.

**CONTEXT:** Vrijen and Logan shared their hard-learned experience from Careerspan, emphasizing that having an in-house technical person who can manage external developers, call out when they're not delivering, and provide product direction is critical. This is distinct from outsourcing everything or relying solely on a technical advisor.

**DECIDED BY:** Consensus between Vrijen and Logan (advice adopted by Merisenda for ACE's planning)

**IMPLICATIONS:** ACE will need to budget for at least one senior technical hire in-house. This person should have both technical building capability and management/product intuition.

**ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED:** Fully outsourcing development vs. building entirely in-house with a full team.

## Decision 3: Unit Economics Awareness Approach

**DECISION:** ACE should plan for AI cost structure and unit economics from the outset, not defer it as a "figure it out later" problem.

**CONTEXT:** Vrijen shared a common failure mode where companies start with unlimited AI subscriptions, realize usage costs are unsustainable, then shift to usage-based pricing and see engagement tank. The advice is to maintain awareness of AI compute costs while still prioritizing user experience in early stages.

**DECIDED BY:** Vrijen (based on Careerspan experience), accepted by Merisenda as applicable guidance

**IMPLICATIONS:** ACE should include AI cost modeling in their planning, implement usage tracking from the start, and consider pricing models that align with unit economics even for early iterations.

**ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED:** Optimizing purely for user experience and addressing costs later (explicitly identified as a risky approach).

## Decision 4: No Initial Investment in Custom AI Infrastructure

**DECISION (IMPLIED):** ACE should build initial AI product using existing LLM APIs and models without investing in custom model training or fine-tuning.

**CONTEXT:** Vrijen and Logan both emphasized that for a product at ACE's scale, existing large language models are "good enough" and investments in custom models or fine-tuning would require a "very compelling case." The consensus was that most companies don't need these capabilities initially.

**DECIDED BY:** Consensus between Vrijen and Logan, adopted as approach for ACE's planning

**IMPLICATIONS:** Faster time-to-market, lower initial technical debt, no need for ML PhD-level hires. Focus should be on product application rather than model engineering.

**ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED:** Building proprietary models, fine-tuning open models, or investing in custom ML infrastructure (dismissed as unnecessary for initial product).
