# Detailed Recap

1. **Opening and emotional check‑in**  
   - V and Holly reconnect informally; both acknowledge feeling exhausted by the last few months of founder life. Holly shares that this is the first time she has really smiled in three months and reflects on how hard the co‑founder breakup has been.
   - She summarises a core lesson: if she did it again, she might have tried to build solo first, raise later, and only then consider co‑founders instead of committing quickly because of accelerator pressure.

2. **Co‑founder fallout and mindset reset**  
   - Holly explains that one co‑founder relationship remains fine, but the other became toxic and is now "a bit of a twat" in her words. She is consciously trying not to internalise it and instead focus on "what’s next".
   - V responds with empathy and a pragmatic framing: we can’t control other people, only how we live around them. He reinforces his read of Holly as a high‑agency, "I’ll do it one way or another" founder and expresses confidence she will be okay.

3. **Current product: recruiting marketplace + ATS layer**  
   - Holly outlines the direction she is most excited about: a **recruiting marketplace and dashboard** for early‑stage startups to post high‑quality roles and get real help filling them. She has already collected ~713 startups in a database and a list of ~600 with live roles.
   - The product vision: become a **home base for early‑stage startups**, solving the visibility problem for good but lesser‑known companies that can’t rely on brand gravity like Cursor. Startups would be able to post roles and access a curated candidate network.

4. **Candidate network and data‑quality attempts**  
   - Holly has built a basic **candidate component** where candidates create private profiles (not public like LinkedIn), specify the kinds of startups they want, and then receive role matches. Startups can in principle tap into this candidate network.
   - She attempted to build an **AI data‑quality checker** to clean and classify her startup database, but the models mis‑labelled important entities (e.g., FIA showing up as a fintech). Given her constraints and lack of robust guardrails, she paused this path.

5. **Experiments building an ATS and pre‑screening system**  
   - Holly demos a fairly full‑featured **ATS interface** that she and a technical partner assembled, including dashboards, calendar integration, stages, interview rounds, and a video/voice pre‑screening agent that can send customised question sets.
   - She explains some of the go‑to‑market challenges: mid‑stage startups with 10–15+ hires see data migration as too painful; early‑stage companies are often hiring too infrequently to justify a full ATS switch. People are habituated to Ashby/Greenhouse despite hating them, and scraping CVs from those systems is brittle and labour‑intensive.
   - A seed of validation appears: the CIO of Seek (Australia) likes the tech and is considering an angel cheque, but Holly is unsure whether doubling down on ATS is the right strategic path.

6. **Market learnings: resumes, AI analysis, and buyer psychology**  
   - Holly reports that when they previously added rich AI analysis on candidates, many hiring managers did **not** read the essays; they found them "too chunky" and still anchored on CVs, preferring information expressed in their own language and formats.
   - She notes that Australian customers in particular often want to rapidly flip through CVs in bulk—something existing ATSs don’t do elegantly but which still dominates their workflow.
   - This leads to a broader recognition between both founders that: resumes are increasingly low‑signal, but the market is still psychologically attached to them, and any new tool has to respect that reality.

7. **V’s reframing: predictable revenue and candidate signal**  
   - V shares that Careerspan has been wrestling with a parallel problem: turning **patchy, unpredictable revenue** (per‑placement cuts, recruiter fees) into something more stable and subscription‑like.
   - He demos the **Careerspan candidate experience**: candidates tell structured stories via AI‑guided chat; the system breaks down responsibilities, hard skills, and soft skills; then produces a detailed role analysis and candidate profile that lets an employer quickly see whether someone is worth a deeper look.
   - On the employer side, he shows a portal view where hiring managers can see high‑signal narrative summaries and gaps instead of wading through unstructured resumes. The thesis: make inbound viable again by **separating “getting to know the human” from “evaluation"** and giving founders a much better filter on who is worth interviewing.

8. **Careerspan as filter, Matchbox as ATS/recruiter**  
   - V positions Careerspan not as an ATS replacement but as a **filter and signal amplifier** that could sit in front of Matchbox’s recruiting work.
   - The model he proposes:
     - Holly continues to own the **employer relationship, ATS, and recruiting workflows**.
     - Careerspan supplies **story‑driven candidate assessment** and can be the first stop for inbound: candidates go through the storytelling process; only high‑engagement, high‑signal candidates are surfaced.
     - Matchbox can then see those candidates in a format that makes it easy to decide who to advance, while still keeping CVs available for customers who insist on them.
   - V stresses that this may be more realistic than Holly independently building deep AI matching and assessment, which is compute‑heavy and expensive.

9. **State of the ATS/AI hiring market**  
   - Both agree the ATS space is already a **bloodbath**, with incumbents like Ashby and Greenhouse enjoying huge installed bases despite underwhelming intelligence.
   - V shares an anecdote: Zapier’s head of recruiting has made resumes optional because there is so little signal left; Zapier is now building its own agentic interviewer. He interprets moves by major players as a shift toward **identity and credibility verification**, leaving subjective evaluation to specialised tools.
   - They converge on a belief that the next wave of hiring will be **lower volume but deeper attention per candidate**, where tools like Careerspan (and potentially Matchbox’s dashboards) provide the informational edge.

10. **Matchbox distribution and pricing experiments**  
   - Holly describes a potential **self‑serve ATS pricing** at around $59/month (down from an earlier idea of ~$4k/year), mostly to let the existing tech earn its keep and see if there is organic uptake.
   - For now, however, she wants to prioritise **rebuilding revenue and reputation** by filling roles and re‑establishing Matchbox as a go‑to recruiter/marketplace, then layering in ATS subscriptions if/when demand proves itself.

11. **Concrete collaboration: roles, bounties, and job links**  
   - Holly shares that she has several **live roles** right now, highlighting especially two high‑priority ones (DeepTune and Yarn) and mentioning Agave as slower‑moving. Many of these are New York‑based senior engineering roles with compensation in the $175k–300k range.
   - V proposes to:
     - Set up a **Careerspan employer account** for Matchbox and generate job‑specific application links.
     - Distribute those links through Careerspan’s curated communities and mailing list, which regularly achieves ~70–80% open rates and ~15% click‑through.
     - Treat these as **shared bounty opportunities**, where Holly keeps her 5k referral fee model and V participates via commission or rental arrangements that align incentives.
   - Holly agrees this would help her both close current roles and rebuild Matchbox’s presence as a recruiter who can reliably fill high‑quality positions.

12. **Specific candidates and intros**  
   - V mentions two specific engineering candidates:
     - A strong computer‑vision‑oriented engineer V recently met and can route immediately to Holly.
     - Marianne Ray, a senior engineer at Ginkgo Bioworks who is open to moving to a smaller team; V sees strong alignment with some of Holly’s health‑tech‑adjacent roles.
   - They discuss sending CVs quickly and potentially framing some of these introductions as coming "through Careerspan/Matchbox" to reinforce each other’s brands. MG‑4 has already generated a draft connector email from V to Marianne on Holly’s behalf.

13. **Immigration, New York plans, and founder life**  
   - Holly shares that a visa agent has told her she qualifies to apply for an **O‑1 visa** and that she is planning to spend 2–3 months in New York next year to build relationships and deal flow.
   - V mentions a friend (Oas) who runs an O‑1‑focused product and has a small but successful track record; he offers this as an alternative resource when Holly is ready.
   - They reflect on the emotional and financial toll of operating in the talent/ATS space, agreeing it is tough, noisy, and exhausting—but still meaningful if they can make better matches for founders and candidates.

14. **Funding strategy and revenue focus**  
   - Holly explains that a few angels (including senior leaders who saw her tech via advisory roles and accelerators) have expressed interest, but she is leaning toward **not taking more capital immediately**.
   - Instead, she wants to focus on **closing 2–3 recruiting placements**, using those fees to rebuild cash reserves. She views this as more realistic than chasing pre‑seed/seed cheques in a brutal market, and more aligned with Matchbox’s strengths.
   - V affirms that "money is money"—there is no shame in revenue over equity—and that a cash‑flow‑positive recruiting practice paired with differentiated tooling could be both sustainable and eventually acquirable.

15. **Closing and next steps**  
   - They agree that Holly will **send across role details and her spreadsheet** of open positions and candidates, plus CVs for the specific profiles they discussed.
   - V will **set up the employer account, generate Careerspan job links, and deploy them** to his communities, then coordinate with his team to work these roles.
   - Both end on a warm note: Holly appreciates the emotional validation and tactical ideas; V reiterates his willingness to help and looks forward to catching up again—ideally in New York once Holly’s visa and travel plans line up.
