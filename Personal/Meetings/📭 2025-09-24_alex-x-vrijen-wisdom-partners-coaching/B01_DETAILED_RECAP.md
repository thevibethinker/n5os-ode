## Key Decisions and Agreements

• **Adopt "playing card" candidate presentation format**: Alex recommended creating digestible candidate summary cards (picture, name, 2-3 relevant experience points, 1-2 story highlights) packaged as URL bundles. This replaces traditional resume-first approach and makes candidate review dramatically faster for hiring managers. **Why it matters**: Differentiates Careerspan from traditional recruiters who just "filter and send resumes" - this is a distinct work product that's more human-centric and engaging.

• **Target hiring managers directly, not HR departments**: Go straight to team leads/engineering managers who have authority to skip ATS and bring candidates into pipeline. **Why it matters**: HR likes their existing process (it's job security); hiring managers are burnt out and want easy solutions. Alex believes hiring managers will be far more receptive to the card-based, human-centric approach.

• **Lead with candidates first, relationship second**: Send URL bundle with candidates upfront rather than trying to establish relationship before showing quality. **Why it matters**: Traditional recruiter approach (long emails, relationship building before candidate reveal) is exhausting and off-putting. "Here's cool candidates, do you want to pay for them?" is unprecedented and enticing.

• **Use candidate categorization over percentage fit scores**: Present candidates in themed groups like "Clear Fits" and "Hidden Gems" rather than algorithmic percentage scores. **Why it matters**: Feels curated and human rather than corporate/algorithmic; builds trust by acknowledging trade-offs explicitly.

• **Focus on product manager roles as initial wedge**: Continue targeting PM positions despite fewer openings because it's a "really interesting area" - highly chaotic role to hire for with insanely varied backgrounds, perfect for surfacing atypical profiles. **Why it matters**: PMs are harder to evaluate than engineers (15-year engineer = obvious skill; PM backgrounds are all over the map); Careerspan's story-based approach is uniquely suited to surface non-obvious fits.

## Strategic Context

Alex is advising Vrijen on GTM strategy during an intense execution phase where Careerspan has achieved early validation ($300-400/warm intro commitments from employers) but needs to prove candidate quality at scale. The core strategic tension is: Careerspan has a "fantastic" product with strong engagement signals, but struggles to convert that into employer revenue because they don't yet have enough quality candidates in target geographies (NY/Boston).

Alex's core insight is that Careerspan needs to fundamentally rethink the **employer-side experience**. Current recruiter approach is broken: they send long emails, try to build relationships before showing candidates, and ultimately just deliver filtered resumes. Hiring managers hate this. They want to see candidates immediately without commitment, and they want information presented in a way that respects their time.

The "playing card" concept is the breakthrough - it packages candidates as discrete, quickly-reviewable cards with just enough info to decide if they want to dig deeper. This can be sent as a URL to capture engagement metrics (click-through rate, profiles browsed, time spent). The URL can tell the entire Careerspan story: "Hey, we found your job listing. Here are candidates we think fit. Click to learn more. That's it."

Positioning insight: Careerspan should frame as a hedge on traditional sourcing. Hiring managers already have talent teams and recruiters - Careerspan is the low-risk, high-signal supplemental channel that requires zero process change.

The PM focus is strategic because it's a role where traditional hiring signals break down (varied backgrounds, career transitions common) and where Careerspan's story-based approach provides maximum differentiation. If they can prove value in the hardest-to-hire role, they can expand to easier roles later.

Pain points surfaced: Companies want subscription model but make it contingent on "show me one good candidate first." The "first one's on you" dynamic means Careerspan needs to nail candidate quality immediately and make the review process frictionless.
