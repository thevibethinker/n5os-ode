## Salient Questions

**1. "Would this approach be more effective for jobs that just got posted because the hiring manager is really engaged, or is it better for jobs that got posted a month ago because the hiring manager's burnt out and wants an easy solution?"**
- Who asked: Alex Caveny (~43:50)
- Why it matters: This is a testable hypothesis that could dramatically affect outreach strategy and conversion rates
- Action hint: Run A/B test on outreach timing - track conversion rates for freshly posted (<1 week) vs. stale (1+ month) job listings. Use engagement metrics from URL tracking to determine optimal timing.

**2. "What are you giving them? What are you showing them?" [on the employer side]**
- Who asked: Alex Caveny (~22:01)
- Why it matters: Exposed that Careerspan has no employer-facing UI - everything is manual/analog. This became the fulcrum for the entire "playing card" redesign discussion.
- Action hint: This question led directly to the playing card concept. The lack of a polished employer view became an opportunity to reimagine the entire presentation format.

**3. "Would you, as an employer, have been responsive to something that just sent you all the information up front or asked for your permission to send stuff?"**
- Who asked: Vrijen (~39:04)
- Why it matters: Tests assumption about opt-in vs. push approach for candidate delivery
- Action hint: Alex's answer was clear: "Just send the info." Don't ask permission, just provide value. Build URL tracking into delivery to measure actual engagement rather than relying on explicit opt-in.

**4. "Should we be even more explicit and just say product managers [instead of 'product']?"**
- Who asked: Vrijen (~06:07)
- Why it matters: Positioning specificity - narrow focus (PMs only) vs. inclusive (all product roles)
- Action hint: Based on later discussion about PM hiring chaos, stay with "product" framing to capture head of product, senior product leaders, and PM roles. The varied backgrounds are actually the strategic advantage.

**5. "Would it be more or less compelling to have you be pitched the candidate with an obvious advocate versus totally neutral description?"**
- Who asked: Vrijen (~34:21)
- Why it matters: Tone and trust-building in candidate presentation - advocacy vs. objectivity balance
- Action hint: Alex's guidance: "Give the impression that it's neutral while being a little bit salesy." Surface both strengths and gaps explicitly. Flag candidates that don't meet certain criteria but seem like good fits anyway. This builds trust by showing you're honest about shortcomings.
