## Landscape Insights

### Hackathons as Technical Education Infrastructure for Non-Technical Founders in SF (Alternative to Traditional Technical Co-Founder Search or Outsourcing)

**Evidence:** "I discovered no-code coding this past year... I've done probably like 30, maybe even up to 50 hackathons... they happen like every weekend... I've actually placed in 10 of them... I was able to meet my technical co-founder because she was a judge of one hackathon that I got third place and she approached me afterward... she was like 'I'm a software engineering consultant for multiple startups... see how I can help you with whatever you're currently working on.'"

**Why it matters:** SF's hackathon density creates alternative path for non-technical founders to (1) rapidly learn technical skills through repeated practice, (2) demonstrate execution ability to attract technical talent, (3) organically meet technical co-founders through demonstrated work rather than cold pitching. This suggests ecosystem infrastructure advantage that's hard to replicate remotely or in smaller markets. Geographic arbitrage matters for non-technical founders specifically.

### Low-Code Tools Enabling Non-Technical Founders to Build Credible MVPs That Attract Technical Talent (Lovable.ai as Signal of Maturing No-Code Ecosystem)

**Evidence:** "One coder friend who told me about a Lovable hackathon. They were giving out 100 free credits... it's supposedly just that you just prompt and you just make a website. It took me 70 prompts to get an MVP that technically worked... I was kind of forced to roll out and build in public because the way the hackathon worked was submit whatever you built and post on social media."

**Why it matters:** Modern low-code tools have crossed threshold where non-technical founders can build functional (if imperfect) products solo to demonstrate vision and momentum. This changes technical co-founder dynamics - instead of pitching an idea, non-technical founders can show working prototype and say "help me scale this." 70 prompts suggests learning curve still exists but it's compressed from months/years to days/weeks. Build-in-public mechanic (hackathon requirement to share on social) forces accountability and early user feedback.

### Newsletter-First GTM as Strategic Foundation for Product-Market Fit Validation Before Building Technical Product

**Evidence:** "I originally started it in 2023 as a Substack newsletter and this was really a choice I made because I am not technical. I always wanted to have a job board but didn't know where I was going to start... previously had roles where I was co editor in chief of a campus magazine at Cornell. So I'm very comfortable with [content]... I was able to get that to grow through word of mouth pretty, pretty widely. I'm at 3,000 subscribers and followers on Substack."

**Why it matters:** Starting with newsletter (low technical barrier) before building job board (high technical barrier) allowed Theresa to validate demand, build audience, and establish distribution before significant technical investment. 3,000 subscribers through organic word-of-mouth provides proof of concept that justifies technical build. This inverts typical startup sequence (build product → find users) to (find users → build product). De-risks technical investment and provides built-in launch audience.
