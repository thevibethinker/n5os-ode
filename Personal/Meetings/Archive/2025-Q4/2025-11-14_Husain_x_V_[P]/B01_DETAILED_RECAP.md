# Detailed Recap

This call is a strategic check‑in between Vrijen (Careerspan) and Husain (Aspire/external partner) about where Careerspan has moved since their last collaboration and how a new, deeper partnership might work.

Vrijen explains that Careerspan has consciously pivoted from a B2C, job‑seeker‑paid model to a B2B hiring tool. Charging candidates was both ethically uncomfortable and economically fragile, so the company has decided to bootstrap instead of raising VC, and to monetize by selling into employers. He outlines current traction: ~3,500 signups, ~200 monthly active users, and meaningful depth of use (stories, vibe checks, resumes) that demonstrates real engagement even though the product remains free to candidates.

The core of the conversation is about how Careerspan fixes the "broken hiring chain." Today, HR screens volumes of resumes they don’t really understand, passes inconsistent shortlists to hiring managers, and even referrals create noisy signals. Careerspan’s approach is to require candidates to complete at least three AI coaching conversations, use their stories plus resume for semantic evaluation, and then supply employers with analysis of strengths, gaps, and concrete development suggestions. Instead of acting as a gatekeeping ATS, Careerspan becomes a candidate advocate and a structured way to see who can succeed with the right coaching.

They then discuss the business model. For employers, Careerspan is priced per role: roughly $200–300 for a six‑week campaign, with the first 100 candidates free. Employers distribute a Careerspan link instead of a generic "Apply on LinkedIn" link, and Careerspan surfaces the candidates who have invested time in reflection and storytelling. This model sidesteps the "how many hundreds of thousands of users do you have" objection by solving a different problem: quality and signal, not raw volume.

Husain pushes on proof and outcomes. He asks about impact metrics (e.g., Mass Eisenberg study data), employer case studies, and before/after comparisons for HR or hiring managers. Vrijen shares candidate‑side evidence (92% improved narratives, 80% surfaced new experiences) and explains how this underpins employer value, but confirms that more explicit hiring case studies are still in progress.

The back half of the call explores a new collaboration idea. Husain suggests a six‑month alumni engagement program with a dedicated alumni community manager, a Discord space, regular challenges and AMAs, and structured measurement over time. The program would aim to keep 1,000 alumni actively using Careerspan, generate engagement metrics and success stories, and culminate in an impact report that both Aspire and Careerspan can share with employers. They also talk about layering in external partners like Ripen to provide projects or programming that give alumni a natural reason to use Careerspan.

Throughout, they return to practical constraints: OpenAI token costs for running large cohorts, what Aspire can cover on the ops side, and what would make the program sustainable for Careerspan. They end with shared enthusiasm for the pilot concept and an implicit next step for Husain to formalize the proposal (cohort size, programming plan, cost coverage) so they can decide whether and how to move forward.
