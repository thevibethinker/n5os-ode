## Strategic Context

**The Fundamental Alignment:** Both Careerspan and Women Back to Work are solving for the same core dysfunction—talent waste in the employment system. Careerspan focuses on narrative reframing (helping people tell their stories better), while Women Back to Work focuses on infrastructure and recruiting rigor to place workforce returners. The conversation revealed that these approaches could be complementary rather than competitive.

**The Employer Problem (Repeated Theme):** Tami consistently emphasized that while candidate supply is not the issue, employer adoption is. Two specific mechanisms create friction:
1. **Manager risk aversion:** Hiring managers perceive empty seats as safer than "bad hires," so they're naturally risk-averse to non-traditional candidates (returnees, narrative-based matching)
2. **System fragmentation:** Hiring decisions are made by individual people (engineers, lawyers, marketers) who have other jobs and weren't trained to hire. They're making binary go/no-go decisions based on resume matching, not contextual understanding

**Why This Matters for Careerspan:** Tami's analysis reframes the problem. Careerspan's core proposition—better narrative matching—only solves half the problem if the fundamental blocker is manager psychology. This suggests either: (a) need to work deeper with employer clients on manager training/psychology, or (b) focus on employers who are already incentivized to take higher-risk candidates (startups, specific roles, explicit diversity initiatives).

**The ATS Crisis:** Tami and Vrijen rapidly aligned on the dysfunction of keyword-based ATS filtering in an AI-augmented world. Tami's comment "it's like spy versus spy...the whole thing is going to fall apart" captures a key insight: as AI makes resume padding easier, ATS systems become less trustworthy. This creates an opening for narrative-based evaluation systems like Careerspan.

**Internal Mobility as Hidden Opportunity:** Tami raised internal mobility (helping employees find new roles within large enterprises) as an underexplored but significant market. Large companies like SAP hire 20,000+ people annually but can't efficiently match current employees to new openings. This is a concrete use case where Careerspan's narrative matching could unlock significant value.
