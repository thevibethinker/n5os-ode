**Perspective:** Speaking as higher ed career services practitioner (Director level, entrepreneurship center focus, 10+ years in university employer engagement)

### Key Insights

**1. Career Center Staff Selection Problem: "Moderately Functioning Introverts"**
- **Observation:** Career center staff are typically "moderately functioning introverts who think they're awesome, don't know much about the working world, not curious, don't talk to employers as much as they need to" - they're "petty tyrants of small kingdoms"
- **Implication:** Structural barrier to innovation isn't just budget or bureaucracy - it's personnel selection. Career services attracts people who are risk-averse, inward-focused, easily threatened by change.
- **Strategic Value:** Don't sell to career center directors - route around them through entrepreneurship centers, alumni engagement, AI centers. Lisa explicitly positioned herself this way: "I'm no longer allowed to play in the job space" but found workarounds.

**2. Handshake Lock-In Despite Universal Hatred**
- **Observation:** "I hate handshake with a hot hate. Students hate it. If colleges are smart, they hate it." But universities won't give it up because it's "all you can eat buffet - none of it very good but there are a lot of options."
- **Implication:** Universities optimize for *appearance* of opportunity (20K jobs posted) over actual outcomes (near-zero hit rate). This is risk mitigation behavior - can't be blamed if jobs are "available."
- **Strategic Value:** Don't compete on job volume. Compete on hit rate + quality curation. Position as complement to Handshake ("we handle the real opportunities"), not replacement (threatens the security blanket).

**3. Alumni Networks Are Undermonetized Infrastructure**
- **Observation:** "I actually think there's a massive opportunity with alumni networks... alumni offer jobs to alumni all the time, perhaps even more than to undergrads. Much less risky." Class of 2017-2022 all pivoting right now.
- **Implication:** Alumni engagement has budget, fewer gatekeepers, clearer ROI than career services. They're desperate for tools to maintain engagement and demonstrate value. Career pivots (not just new grad placement) are the real volume.
- **Strategic Value:** Beth Roan intro is more strategic than career services intro. Alumni networks need continuous engagement tools (career updates, job matching) to justify their existence to admin.

**4. Institutional Recording Restrictions Creating Personal Tool Market**
- **Observation:** Lisa got HR complaint about using AI meeting note-takers (Fathom, Fireflies, etc.) - policy violation despite being "business tools" and her having ADHD. She's forced to find workarounds.
- **Implication:** University compliance/privacy policies are blocking productivity tools that knowledge workers consider standard. This creates demand for "compliant" alternatives (device-based recording like Plaud + local processing).
- **Strategic Value:** There's a service business in helping higher ed professionals navigate institutional tool constraints while maintaining productivity. "Shadow IT" consulting for academics/staff.

**5. AI Center + Entrepreneurship Center = Innovation Workaround**
- **Observation:** Lisa operates at intersection of AI Center (David Watts, IBM exec) and Entrepreneurship Center. They're hosting hackathons, doing "open source innovation challenges" with employers to bypass recruiting ATS.
- **Implication:** These centers have budget flexibility, mission alignment with innovation, and less bureaucratic overhead than career services. They're explicitly set up as innovation sandboxes.
- **Strategic Value:** Zo hackathon is perfect wedge - serves AI Center mission (student AI fluency), Entrepreneurship Center mission (building things), and creates product-led growth through student adoption. Triple alignment.

**6. Referral Bar Has Risen Dramatically**
- **Observation:** "The bar there has also been raised. It can't just be transactional. You need 6 months, a year of having been in touch, 'How can I be helpful to your company? What can I do for you without asking for things?'"
- **Implication:** Students think "asking for referral = getting considered" but don't understand mechanics. Referrals now require sustained value-add relationship building. The informal job market has formalized.
- **Strategic Value:** This validates Careerspan's "build network proactively" positioning. Also suggests content opportunity: teach students referral mechanics, not just "network more."

**7. Gen Z Certainty Bias Hurting Them**
- **Observation:** "This is a generation that's not used to lots of failure. They like certainty more than almost anything else. They will put up with anything if they can just guarantee an outcome."
- **Implication:** Students are choosing suboptimal paths (investment banking, consulting) because they offer perceived certainty, even as those paths are closing. Risk aversion is making them less adaptive.
- **Strategic Value:** Messaging angle - "the certain path is now uncertain; develop adaptability" - positions career exploration as risk mitigation, not risk-taking. Flip the script on their certainty bias.

**8. Higher Ed Trapped Between Nonprofit DNA and For-Profit Economics**
- **Observation:** Lisa's arc (nonprofit → for-profit → back to higher ed for financial aid) reveals structural tension. She saw higher ed as "no endowment, no way to support students" at Bennington, but went back when kids needed college.
- **Implication:** Higher ed employees are often entrepreneurial operators temporarily in institutions for pragmatic reasons (benefits, financial aid, stability). They're receptive to for-profit thinking but navigate nonprofit constraints.
- **Strategic Value:** Partner with individuals who have for-profit DNA (like Lisa) who can champion internally. They understand value creation, not just budget protection.
