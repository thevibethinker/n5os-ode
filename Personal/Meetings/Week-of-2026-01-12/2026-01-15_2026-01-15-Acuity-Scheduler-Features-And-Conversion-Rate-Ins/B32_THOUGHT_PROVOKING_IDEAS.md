## TPI-1: The Curse of Tech Empathy Deficit

**The Idea:**
> "When tech people are developing tech products, that can have a tendency to happen. Because it comes so intuitively to you."
— Heather

Tech founders and engineers systematically underestimate product complexity because they experience their own tools through expert eyes. This isn't ignorance—it's a kind of empathy blindness built into the development process.

**Why It's Provocative:**
Most product failures attributed to "bad UX" or "poor market fit" may actually be empathy failures. The person who built the thing literally cannot perceive the difficulty others experience.

**Implications for Thought:**
- Non-technical co-founders may be undervalued for their empathy function
- User testing with truly naive users should be mandatory, not optional
- AI may help bridge this gap (or widen it if AI is also "expert-brained")

## TPI-2: Schedulers as Shadow CRMs

**The Idea:**
Conversion tracking traditionally lives in CRM. But for practitioners without robust CRM (many coaches, therapists), the scheduler is already the de facto relationship system.

**Why It's Provocative:**
Schedulers could absorb CRM functionality not by becoming complex, but by recognizing they already hold relationship data. The "client history view" Heather uses is proto-CRM. Acuity just doesn't frame it that way.

**Implications for Thought:**
- Product category boundaries may be more arbitrary than we think
- The data is already there—the question is framing
- This is the "jobs to be done" insight applied to scheduling

## TPI-3: AI Adoption as Psychological Safety Problem

**The Idea:**
> "Bringing people into the AI Revolution, but meeting them where they are right now... Psychological safety matters."
— V

AI adoption resistance isn't primarily about capability or even utility—it's about fear. Users who feel judged, overwhelmed, or uncertain won't adopt even beneficial tools.

**Why It's Provocative:**
The AI industry focuses on capabilities and features. But the adoption bottleneck may be emotional, not functional. The winning AI products may be those that make users feel safe and competent, not those with the most features.

**Implications for Thought:**
- AI product design should include emotional states as first-class concerns
- "Meeting users where they are" may be more valuable than leading-edge features
- Empathy in AI may mean empathy for the *user*, not just AI that seems empathetic
