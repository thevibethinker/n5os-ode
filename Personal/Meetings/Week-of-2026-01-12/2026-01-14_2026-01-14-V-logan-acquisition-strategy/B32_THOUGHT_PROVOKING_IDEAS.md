# B32: Thought-Provoking Ideas

> **Filter Note:** These are high-threshold, non-actionable provocations—strategic "weirdness" or original mental models worth holding onto.

## Provocations

### 1. The Visibility Precedes Legitimacy Principle
**Core Idea:** In acquisition dynamics, being *seen* actively by decision-makers creates a presumption of value that cold outreach cannot achieve. The strategy isn't "convince them we're good"—it's "make them wonder why everyone's talking about us."

**What's Interesting:** This inverts the typical B2B sales motion. Instead of proving value then seeking attention, they're manufacturing attention to create *assumed* value. The product is almost secondary to the social proof of visibility.

**Mental Model:** "Line of sight strategy"—presence creates plausibility.

---

### 2. The Multi-Dimensional Optionality Matrix
**Core Idea:** Exit opportunities aren't binary (sell/don't sell). They exist across three independent dimensions: how close are we (proximity), how warm are they (engagement), and what do we actually want from them (exit type). 

**What's Interesting:** Most founders think about acquirers as a ranked list. V and Logan model it as a 3D space where different regions serve different strategic purposes. A "PR exit" isn't failure—it's a legitimate category serving a different optimization function than "money in the bank."

**Mental Model:** Exit optionality as a portfolio, not a ladder.

---

### 3. The Artificial Urgency Paradox
**Core Idea:** "Classic time compression" is explicitly stated as the tactic. They're creating urgency that doesn't exist externally to generate real competitive pressure. But if both sides know this game is played, does it still work?

**What's Interesting:** The paradox is that manufactured urgency works *even when sophisticated players know it's manufactured*. The game theory holds because no acquirer wants to be the one who miscalculated while others moved. FOMO operates even when consciously identified.

**Mental Model:** Synthetic urgency as a coordination mechanism.
