## TPI-1: The Monetization of Friction

**The Idea:**
Suburban therapists charge premium rates partly *because* scheduling requires a phone call. The friction is a feature, not a bug.

**Why It's Provocative:**
SaaS and productivity tools assume friction is always bad. But in high-touch service industries, friction signals care. Removing it may actually erode perceived value and justify lower prices.

**Implications for Thought:**
- Some markets may resist efficiency improvements not from ignorance but from economic rationality
- "Premium" in service industries may partly be paying for intentional inefficiency
- Scheduling automation companies may inadvertently commoditize their customers' services

## TPI-2: Privacy as Workflow Tax

**The Idea:**
Jen maintains two non-syncing calendars specifically because privacy requirements prevent easy integration. This is a hidden operational cost of confidentiality.

**Why It's Provocative:**
We think of HIPAA compliance in terms of software features and security certifications. But the lived reality is daily workflow friction—double data entry, confusion from using initials, mental overhead tracking two systems.

**Implications for Thought:**
- Privacy requirements have hidden productivity costs rarely quantified
- Solutions that solve the *workflow* of privacy (not just the compliance) may be undervalued
- The therapy market may be more receptive to scheduling tools that understand this tax
