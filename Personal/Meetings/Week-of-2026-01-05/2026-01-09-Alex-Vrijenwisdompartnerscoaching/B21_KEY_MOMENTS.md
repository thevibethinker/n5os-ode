# B21: Key Quotes & Strategic Questions

## Key Quotes

**"This is just such a bad way to implement an AI inbox."**  
— Alex Caveny  
*Context:* Critiquing Google's new AI Gmail features, which created overly verbose summaries instead of actually helping users prioritize. Captures frustration with AI products that add more to read rather than less.

**"I am not a designer. I have actively been told that some of the designs that I've come up with border on war crimes."**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Context:* Making a fundamental point about information design: the replacement information cannot be greater than the original, or it fails to solve the problem. Using self-deprecating humor to underscore a key UX principle.

**"The whole point of it is to save me time if I have to watch the damn thing. Like, how does it save me time?"**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Context:* Discussing video interview tools that require watching recordings instead of providing summaries. Highlights the paradox of tools claiming to save time while actually adding work.

**"Let's just throw some AI at it without really thinking, like, how are we streamlining people's lives and days and making it better and not just being like, more stuff to read within the product."**  
— Alex Caveny  
*Context:* Criticizing the "sloppy corporate" approach to AI—adding AI features without genuine user benefit thinking. Calls out the common pattern of companies prioritizing AI presence over actual utility.

**"I think what's really missing is AI learning from you. Like they try to do it like ChatGPT and everything will store memory, but it's not very good. It learns its guess of what's important, not what you're actually saying."**  
— Alex Caveny  
*Context:* Identifying a core limitation of current AI systems—they infer preferences instead of learning them directly from user feedback. Captures the gap between what AI thinks users want and what users actually communicate.

**"You can artificially raise the quote unquote IQ of a system with appropriate like systems engineering."**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Context:* Drawing a parallel to human behavior: a disciplined, well-guardrailed system can outperform a brilliant but unreliable one. Making the case that agenticity and predictability matter more than raw intelligence.

**"One of my big, like product philosophies is just keeping things simple and small and I just, I want less."**  
— Alex Caveny  
*Context:* Articulating a counterintuitive product philosophy in an AI world where "more" is easy. Positioning Careerspan as aligned with this approach—precision over volume, curated insights over overwhelming data.

**"It's easy giving people a lot. It's much harder giving people a little and having them be very happy."**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Context:* Reflecting on Careerspan's own product journey and the difficulty of providing truly high-value, minimal information that actually moves the needle. Captures the core challenge of AI product design.

## Strategic Questions Raised

**"So what you're describing has this very interesting parallel problem to ours. Which is to do any level of prioritization, whether it's of tasks or candidates, you have to have an external context for what matters and what doesn't."**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Significance:* Connects Alex's notification prioritization challenge to Careerspan's candidate ranking problem. Raises the fundamental question of how to establish and maintain dynamic context for decision-making—a shared technical and product challenge.

**"Do you have any sense of, like, how you would build the base understanding to even, like, start prioritizing one thing versus the other?"**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Significance:* Directly probes the technical architecture challenge of building prioritization systems with constrained memory and context. Goes beyond the product philosophy to ask about implementation—how to actually make these systems work at scale.

**"How do I do better? Which is the main thing."**  
— Alex Caveny  
*Significance:* In the context of interview analytics that provide overwhelming metrics, this cuts to the core purpose of any feedback tool. Asks whether the output enables improvement or just creates anxiety—the ultimate test of utility.
