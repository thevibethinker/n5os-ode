## Strategic Context

Dylan is building anphoria as a challenge-based recruitment platform - companies post real problems, students submit solutions, winners get prize money. He's explicitly positioning AWAY from traditional CS hackathons (already crowded with Major League Hacking, etc.) and TOWARD business/creative challenges (marketing campaigns, financial modeling, strategy work).

His thesis: Business students need "passion projects" and portfolio pieces just as much as CS students, but lack structured opportunities. By creating a marketplace for these challenges, he solves two problems: (1) companies get crowdsourced solutions and can talent-scout, (2) students get resume-worthy projects and potential job pathways.

Key strategic insight from Vrijen: Marketing/creative challenges have higher viral potential than code challenges because the work product itself is shareable content. A great marketing campaign or social media strategy can be posted, generating organic reach for the platform.

Dylan's traction: ~100 users, 5 companies signed up for Jan relaunch, Instagram video went viral. He's in the critical "first few months post-launch" phase where execution and positioning will determine trajectory.

Market timing advantage: With major tech layoffs ongoing, Dylan sees opportunity to attract attention as job market becomes more competitive and students seek differentiation.
