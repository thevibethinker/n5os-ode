# B32: Thought-Provoking Ideas

## 1. The Engagement Cost Paradox

Careerspan inverts the traditional cost-per-user model: every dollar spent is not an expenditure on a tool, but an investment *in* engagement with the institution. When a user tells a story on Careerspan, they are paying—with time and attention—for the privilege of engaging with Emory's brand ecosystem.

This creates a bizarre economics where "usage" is actually "loyalty purchasing." The platform doesn't provide a service to users; users provide a service to the platform—by voluntarily creating data, narratives, and commitment signals that reinforce their relationship with the institution. The university isn't buying software; it's buying a mechanism where users *pay* to demonstrate their affiliation.

**The provocation:** What if we stopped thinking about "providing services to alumni" and started thinking about "designing systems where alumni pay to demonstrate they're still ours?"

---

## 2. The Self-Fulfilling Liquidity Trap

The proposed hiring model creates an artificial liquidity loop that doesn't rely on actual job market activity. Alumni post jobs because they can check against alums; alums maintain profiles because they expect jobs from alums. The system generates its own gravitational pull through the promise of *potential* connection, not actual connections.

This is not a network effect in the traditional sense—it's a participation effect. Value is created not by who's there, but by the act of *being* there. The mere existence of a closed loop creates the illusion of opportunity sufficient to drive participation, regardless of whether actual transactions occur.

**The provocation:** Are we building a hiring platform, or are we building a mechanism that makes alumni *feel* like they have better access to each other, regardless of whether they actually do?

---

## 3. The Free Sample Irony

The "first 500 free" model deliberately gives away the most valuable segment of the user base to secure institutional commitment. In traditional sales, early adopters are your best customers—enthusiastic, forgiving, and willing to pay a premium. Here, they become the loss leader.

The university's most engaged alumni (the ones who will click first, use it most, and create the best proof points) are exactly the ones Careerspan gives away for free. The logic is sound: convert them into data points to unlock a larger, less engaged cohort who will pay. But it creates an inverted incentive structure where the best users subsidize the conversion of the worst users.

**The provocation:** When you monetize your power users by giving them away, are you building sustainable value, or are you just accelerating the commoditization of your own best customers?
