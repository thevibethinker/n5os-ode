# Detailed Recap

1. **Opening and reconnection**  
   - You and Jonathan reconnect after a long gap as former peers who have both stayed in the tech / startup / investing ecosystem.  
   - The tone is immediately friendly and informal; you joke about how long it has taken to have this conversation and about both of you "being in the same industry for a minute."

2. **Jonathan’s path from operator to fund manager**  
   - Jonathan summarizes his journey after operating roles: working at Zumba, helping spin out an early‑childhood company (Zoombini), and then doing a series of opportunistic SPVs that performed well.  
   - Those wins gave him enough of a story to go back to the same LPs and raise a dedicated fund of roughly **$20M**, and to build a small team around that vehicle.  
   - The fund’s strategy is to invest **post–product‑market‑fit** (typically around **$3–4M ARR** and ~30–40 employees), usually alongside a larger lead fund.

3. **State of the venture market and emerging manager reality**  
   - Jonathan contrasts the 2021 environment—rapid markups, easy follow‑on capital, and on‑paper gains—with the current, much tighter market.  
   - He notes that for emerging managers today the real question is: **can you show enough repeatable traction** to justify another fund when feedback loops are long and liquidity is far off.  
   - Even good portfolio companies are facing a brutal funding environment; winners are struggling to raise subsequent rounds, which slows down the visible "up and to the right" story.

4. **Round sizing, valuation, and fund math**  
   - Jonathan shares how large funds (e.g., QED) think: they will not do a deal unless they can plausibly see **$500M+** in return from their stake.  
   - That logic pushes founders toward **large, high‑valuation rounds** where expectations are extreme and the preference stack can become dangerous.  
   - You both reflect on how many founders mis‑calibrate round size and valuation relative to traction and market size, potentially working for years under a cap table that leaves them with little in realistic exit scenarios.

5. **Your journey and the origin of Careerspan**  
   - You recap your path: Singapore test‑prep and admissions consulting, a small exit, MBA, time at McKinsey, and then founding **Careerspan**.  
   - Careerspan began as a **coaching product**: helping individuals build self‑knowledge and coherent personal narratives, not just polishing résumés.  
   - You realized that 90%+ of people struggle to talk about themselves and that strong stories are the real bottleneck to compelling applications and interviews.

6. **Careerspan’s current product and thesis**  
   - You describe Careerspan as a **story‑driven screening and matching layer**: in a short conversation, it captures ~200 structured data points about skills, responsibilities, impact, and preferences.  
   - That data can be used to decide who should move into a screening process with much higher precision than résumé parsing alone.  
   - You argue that traditional ATS products are structurally constrained: if 99% of incoming résumés are low‑signal, it is extremely hard for any AI pipeline on top of them to be economically rational without asking candidates for *more and better data*.

7. **From job‑seeker monetization to employer / ecosystem customers**  
   - You share that trying to monetize job‑seekers directly is both hard (they are often cash‑constrained) and emotionally misaligned.  
   - Over the last several months you have shifted emphasis to **employers and ecosystems**: founders, VCs, and talent partners who are drowning in low‑signal inbound and would pay for a much cleaner, smaller funnel of high‑quality candidates.  
   - You outline how Careerspan’s tech can cut screening from "100+ resumes for one good person" down to a much smaller set of serious, pre‑qualified candidates.

8. **VC‑centric use cases and talent leverage**  
   - You and Jonathan explore a **fund‑level** use case: a Careerspan link that lives on the fund’s site / talent resources, where strong candidates can tell their story once and be matched into roles across the portfolio.  
   - Jonathan shares his own experience sourcing hires from LinkedIn: both of his current team members came from inbound, but only after ~1,500 applications per role and a huge amount of manual triage.  
   - He observes that, for many portfolio founders, inbound is effectively ignored because the cost of screening is so high; they default to retained search or warm introductions.

9. **Strategic realism about ATS and competitors**  
   - You argue that there is **no conceivable path** for traditional ATS vendors to layer real AI reasoning on résumés alone, because the underlying data stream is too weak.  
   - You mention UK companies with similar "richer candidate data" approaches that have already raised meaningful capital (e.g., ~$20M, ~$5M), suggesting the category is real even if crowded.  
   - You are candid that Careerspan’s most likely scale path may be as an **acquisition target** for a larger platform that needs your tech to truly modernize screening.

10. **Next steps and relationship commitments**  
   - Jonathan is supportive but capacity‑constrained; he is not in a position to engage deeply right now but wants to stay close.  
   - He offers two clear commitments:
     - When you are actively fundraising, send him your materials so he can share them with relevant pre‑seed / seed funds in his network.  
     - If you send him a short note / materials, he is happy to forward an intro to **Faison Body** (Halo Cars founder) and potentially other high‑leverage contacts.  
   - You agree to follow up with materials when the time is right and to ping him when you are in New York so you can meet in person.
