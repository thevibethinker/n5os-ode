# Key moments

## 1. Naming burnout and the shift away from fundraising
> "We just are too tired to do any more fundraising... after three years and a lot of effort, I think all three of us are pretty drained."

- **Why it matters:** This is the clearest articulation of founder burnout and the core reason for pivoting away from continued fundraising.
- **Impact:** Frames all subsequent strategic decisions (shrinking the team, exploring acqui‑hire, pausing external work) as responses to human constraints, not just financial ones.

## 2. Committing to a lean founders‑only core
> "We've kind of shaved off quite a few team members at this point. So it's essentially just going to be me, Ilsa and Logan from the start of the year."

- **Why it matters:** Marks a decisive move into **hibernation mode** with only the founders remaining.
- **Impact:** Sets expectations about capacity, signals that external spend (including Megan’s engagement) will necessarily be limited, and clarifies who will be in the room for any acquisition discussions.

## 3. Implication for the engagement with Megan
> "I think that also probably means that we have to, like, at minimum pause if not end working with you... which also sucks because, yeah, I mean, for obvious reasons we love working with you."

- **Why it matters:** This is the **explicit statement** that the consulting relationship will be paused or ended.
- **Impact:** Provides closure and context for Megan, and preserves the relationship by pairing honesty with genuine appreciation.

## 4. Articulating the exit goal
> "We're basically just trying to shrink operations to the point where we can get away with our salaries being somewhat maintained, you know, and just go into like absolute hibernation mode and see if we can sell the company out by like March."

- **Why it matters:** Captures the **operating plan and timeline**: minimal burn, founders’ salaries sustained, and a target window for a transaction.
- **Impact:** Useful anchor quote for any internal or external narrative about the wind‑down and sale process.

## 5. Framing the emotional debt and desire for a reset
> "You bring with you all of the baggage... in the interpersonal and the technical and like every form of figurative and emotional debt you think you can carry after three years, you do."

- **Why it matters:** Distills the emotional reality of a multi‑year startup grind.
- **Impact:** Clarifies why a **clean reset** after an exit is important and why V is unlikely to simply continue in the same structure.

## 6. Mutual commitment to future collaboration
> V: "I would love to work with you again... as long as you're open to working with me, I'd love to stay in touch."  
> Megan: "Yeah, of course... I'm always around so we can always brainstorm stuff like that."

- **Why it matters:** Confirms that this is a **relationship‑preserving transition**, not a rupture.
- **Impact:** Creates a clear relational bridge into V’s next venture and any future group/community experiments they might explore together.
