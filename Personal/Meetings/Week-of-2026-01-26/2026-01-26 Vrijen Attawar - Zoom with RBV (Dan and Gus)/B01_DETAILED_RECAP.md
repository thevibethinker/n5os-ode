# B01: Detailed Recap

## Meeting Overview
A strategic discussion between Vrijen Attawar (Careerspan) and Red Bear Ventures (Dan Stoller, Gus) covering V's startup exit plans and RBV's vision for community-driven expert-weighted capital allocation. The conversation revealed strong conceptual alignment between Careerspan's first-party data capture methodology and RBV's need to extract expert insight at scale.

## Chronological Discussion

### Careerspan Status Update (0:00-15:00)
V shared that Careerspan is actively pursuing a sale/exit. The product is in strong shape and currently deployed placing Indian engineers with US companies. However, the 3-person team (down from earlier scale) is fatigued by startup uncertainty and united in finding a strategic home for the technology. V noted 4-5 serious conversations underway, including a workforce development organization and several recruitment/talent pipeline use cases. He's preparing a 1-2 minute demo video to share with potential buyers and his network. Dan and Gus emphasized their empathy, noting they'd navigated similar team ramp-down and sale dynamics with their previous company.

### Tech Demonstration & Market Positioning (15:00-20:00)
V showcased the Careerspan employer portal, which presents anonymized candidate data derived from structured interactions (STAR rubric-style). He positioned Careerspan's core innovation as "capturing human data" through a guided interview modality—creating first-party insights directly from candidates. This contrasted sharply with companies like Eightfold, which face class-action lawsuits over scraped social media data. V argued the market will shift toward first-party data as the Eightfold "emperor has no clothes" reality sets in. Dan noted this approach could also be interesting for the 360/assessment space.

### Red Bear Ventures Vision (20:00-35:00)
Dan and Gus outlined RBV's evolution from a Cornell alumni-focused fund to a community-driven investment platform. Starting with 350,000 Cornell alumni, they envision enabling community members to serve as investors, advisors, and scouts. The core concept is "expert-weighted capital allocation"—leveraging industry insiders' deep expertise to vet deals rather than traditional VC networks. Currently operating manually ("air traffic control" mode), they need automation and AI to scale the expert connection process. The value proposition to entrepreneurs: easy capital allocation plus access to credible industry experts. They aim to eventually expand beyond Cornell into broader expert networks.

### Expert Network & Signal Propagation (35:00-45:00)
Gus detailed the mechanics: identify 3-5 industry experts per deal, have them review/interact with founders, then broadcast their endorsement as an investment signal to the broader community. The Biosha deal served as a concrete example—an orthopedic surgeon (Dan's contact) endorsed the orthopedic infection startup without deep DD, signaling "game changer" status. Incentive structures include carry allocation for anchor investors who vouch for deals. This creates a flywheel where expert endorsement attracts community capital. V drew parallels to "expertise capital" as distinct from social capital—aligning with Cornell's research-forward reputation.

### Synthesis & Technical Exploration (45:00-60:00)
V drew explicit parallels between Careerspan's data capture challenge and RBV's expert extraction problem. Both need mechanisms to capture structured insight beyond LinkedIn-style profiles. He noted unit economics challenges at scale (most data is noise, can't burn tokens filtering it). V introduced Zo (his advisor role with Zo Computer) as a potential MVP platform for prototyping—spinning up quick databases, agents, and research workflows. The group discussed guiding experts through AI-assisted questions, training on their scholarship/work, and reducing friction for experts like V's father (a high-volume transplant surgeon who performs 60% of India's heart transplants but has zero time for sourcing deals). Dan noted the target expert profile: "more money than time" with unique industry vantage points.

## Key Takeaways
- Careerspan is actively selling; V has 4-5 serious conversations and a demo video in progress
- RBV is building toward automated expert-weighted capital allocation, starting with Cornell's 350k alumni network
- Both companies face the same core challenge: extracting high-signal expert insight at scale with sustainable unit economics
- V sees Zo as a viable prototyping platform for RBV's expert interaction workflows and offered to demo capabilities
- Expert endorsement carries strong signal value; incentive structures (carry) can create anchor investor flywheels
- The conversation evolved from career update to strategic advisory—V offered to think through RBV's approach and share Zo demos
