## Meeting Overview

A peer mentorship conversation between Vrijen Attawar (Careerspan founder receiving executive function coaching) and Chris Hanson (special education teacher and edtech founder). The discussion centered on Chris's journey building an executive function support tool, sharing experiences as non-technical founders in the edtech space, with strategic advice on product development, enterprise sales challenges, and finding product-market fit.

## Chronological Discussion

### Opening & Coaching Context (0:00-4:06)
The conversation opened with casual discussion about Vrijen's vaping habits and his positive experience working with Heather, Chris's executive function coach. Vrijen praised Heather's ability to help him stay grounded and break down tasks, describing the coaching relationship as providing necessary guardrails for someone who mostly fixes themselves but needs focus and structure. Chris reflected on the language of "bad habits" and whether framing behaviors as good or bad is psychologically useful or merely a defense mechanism. Both acknowledged the ADHD tendency to thrive in chaos and the comfort of familiar patterns—Chris described leaning on patterns as a "source of comfort" in uncertain times.

### Founder Journey & Technical Challenges (4:06-8:16)
Chris revealed his background as a special education teacher who built an edtech product over the past year without technical expertise. He worked with Joe Coleman, described as a "tech handyman" rather than a specialist—"kind of like good at a lot of things but not like he's not like an expert in any one thing." Vrijen related strongly to this experience, sharing his own journey as a non-technical founder who faced a difficult first year of "planting on my face" and "trusting people I shouldn't have trusted." Vrijen praised Chris for successfully articulating product requirements and navigating the technical divide, something he found challenging initially. Chris admitted he only recently realized he could have used a product manager between himself and the developer but "there's no investors, they're just, it's just us" and lacked capital for additional hires.

### Monetization Strategy & Business Model (8:16-10:54)
Chris outlined his monetization approach: targeting B2B/B2G markets including school districts, clinics, and supportive organizations rather than individual consumers. He expressed a "moral obligation to not put more on supporters, on teachers" preferring employer-paid subscriptions that create organizational cohesion—"it can ultimately like bring cohesiveness into an organization if everyone is using this versus like one teacher here in this department." The current landing page offers individual trials at $10/month, supporter accounts with 10 profiles at $20/month, and a "request quote" option for organizations that routes to a form and Chris personally. Chris expressed concern about the "chicken or egg" problem—large organizations dangling six-figure contracts ("a shit ton of money in front of us, like a hundred thousand dollars") in exchange for custom features he lacks bandwidth to build: "that's a huge risk."

### Strategic Advice on Sales & Pilots (10:54-15:51)
Vrijen advised Chris on the dangers of enterprise feature creep and the risk of "over optimizing for one stakeholder and essentially building out from others." He recommended pursuing a "linchpin deal"—a paid pilot with a design partner willing to work with the product as-is rather than demanding extensive customizations. Vrijen warned against the "death by a thousand cuts" of endless unpaid pilots and suggested prioritizing partners who offer strategic alignment over those with deeper pockets but feature-heavy demands. He strongly advised avoiding venture capital, describing investors as "not worth your time, my friend—they're not, they're not" and instead recommended hiring an SDR (sales development representative) to handle outbound prospecting while Chris closes deals to maintain customer research, messaging refinement, and network building. Chris noted his branding ("inspired by people like you," "created by people like you") required his continued involvement in sales to maintain authenticity.

### Product-Market Fit & Narrowing ICP (16:06-19:12)
Chris disclosed a 65,000-person email list built over years and indirect connections to school administrators—"I can sort of tap into." Vrijen highlighted this as a strong position but emphasized the need for a structured sales process and clear boundaries on what he will and won't build. Drawing from Careerspan's experience, Vrijen explained the painful lesson of starting too broad—assuming everyone could benefit from self-reflection before realizing the true ICP was people willing to engage in it for career advancement. He described product-market fit as something "you know it when you see it" (referencing the Supreme Court's obscenity test) and "when you have it, you don't think about it. And when you don't have it, you fucking thinking about it all the time."

### Community Building & Authentic Branding (19:12-24:33)
Chris expressed concern about backlash if features that users love don't align with a narrower ICP—"what do I handle the, like, any backlash maybe that I get from that? Or like, if I have start subtracting features and dialing in on others." Vrijen recommended transparent communication with users and building in public, leveraging the emotive nature of executive function coaching to authentically connect with audiences. Chris proposed creating a Facebook group for users, including trial participants, to gather feedback on new features—"I can update them throughout the week as I'm. Yeah. As I'm considering new features or having interview." Vrijen described this as a "user interest group" halfway between one-to-many communication and true community, noting the spectrum from newsletter to TikTok with comments to private sub-communities to full communities. He emphasized the need for time efficiency given the small team: "it's just you and Joe and you're not trying to raise like, silly money, you will have to be extremely efficient with your time."

### Social Media Presence & Personal Values (23:25-25:20)
Chris revealed his reluctance to engage on social media due to discomfort with perceived disingenuousness—"Feels like I'd have to be disingenuous and I just, I'm not comfortable typically like doing the social media thing." Vrijen argued that authenticity is key—founders should post in their own voice: "If you're the kind of person that curses. Fucking curse." Chris countered that his product serves children, and parents seeing him curse on Instagram would be "not a good look"—a "mismatch of values I think with. Between some of the parents that come to us for support for their 14 year old." Vrijen suggested separating personas across different channels and recommended joining FOHI (Future of Higher Education), the largest higher education Slack community with 6,000 members globally (1,000 in New York, 1,000 in Boston), where Vrijen serves as New York co-lead.

### Closing & Personal Connection (25:20-28:24)
Vrijen offered to make introductions within the FOHI community once Chris secures a "linchpin school" deal. Both reflected on the positive aspects of edtech as a generally lovely industry compared to other tech sectors—"even though people can still be douchey because it's tech, people are still overall very lovely because it's ed." They exchanged location details—Chris lives in Kent, Washington between Seattle and Tacoma; Vrijen is in Brooklyn. Vrijen explained he found Chris's organization through his wife's research, initially speaking with Amy, the client onboarding specialist ("Asian lady?"—"Yeah, yeah, she's our, our client onboarding specialist"). They closed with shared appreciation for building teams of people they genuinely like working with—I feel like you get to hire the people you really like...it's like the best gig job I've ever had"—and the stress-reward balance of entrepreneurship.
