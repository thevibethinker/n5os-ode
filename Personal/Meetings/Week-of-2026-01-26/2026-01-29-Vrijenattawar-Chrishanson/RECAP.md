## Meeting Overview

A peer-to-peer strategic conversation between Vrijen Attawar (founder of Careerspan) and Chris Hanson (special education teacher turned edtech founder). Vrijen, currently receiving executive function coaching from Chris's colleague Heather, discovered Chris is building an ADHD support product called "360." The discussion evolved into a candid exchange about product strategy, go-to-market challenges, and shared experiences as non-technical founders in the education space.

## Chronological Discussion

### Opening & Relationship Context (0:00-1:00)

The conversation opened with casual small talk about Vrijen's voice quality (vaping), which Vrijen attributed to working with Heather on habit management. Chris is Heather's colleague at their coaching practice, and this connection established the context for their meeting.

### Habit Psychology & Self-Perception (0:57-2:08)

Chris asked about Vrijen's use of "bad habits" language, probing whether this framing had softened through coaching. Vrijen explained the language is mostly humor but acknowledged it might serve as a defense mechanism. He described oscillating between viewing behaviors neutrally ("behaviors to change") versus labeling them as "bad" on less disciplined days. Both related to the tension between adult self-regulation and reverting to childlike patterns.

### ADHD, Chaos, and Comfort in Patterns (2:08-3:11)

The discussion shifted to how ADHD minds find comfort in patterns despite thriving in chaos. Vrijen shared his personal interest in building AI tools as "brain crutches" for ADHD—filling gaps between therapy, medication, and coaching. He expressed excitement to learn from actual practitioners in a space he had only theorized about.

### Product Development Journey (4:06-6:56)

Chris revealed he's been building his edtech product for almost a year, leveraging AI despite being a former special education teacher rather than a developer. He emphasized coaching's high cost and limited accessibility, driving him to create different product offerings. Chris acknowledged struggling with feature creep but expressed gratification at executing an idea rather than letting it remain abstract.

Vrijen validated Chris's accomplishment, noting the product appeared well-built despite Chris's non-technical background. Chris credited Joe Coleman, described as a "tech handyman" and jack-of-all-trades, for development support.

Vrijen shared his own similar path—non-technical background, brief management consulting experience, affinity but not expertise in tech—and described the first year as characterized by face-plants and misplaced trust in technical partners.

### Monetization Strategy & Institutional Focus (8:14-11:46)

Chris outlined his monetization approach: targeting organizations, school districts, and clinics rather than individual consumers. He cited a moral obligation not to burden teachers/coaches with costs when employers could pay. The strategy emphasizes organizational adoption for cohesion—getting everyone aligned on executive function support rather than siloed usage.

Current pricing: $10/month for individuals (launch price), $20/month for supporters (up to 10 profiles), and custom quotes for organizations via a request form.

### The Pilot Trap & Custom Feature Requests (10:23-14:01)

Chris expressed concern about organizational sales conversations dangling large contracts in exchange for custom feature development. Vrijen warned against the "chicken or egg" problem and the risk of over-optimizing for a single stakeholder at the expense of broader product fit.

Vrijen shared advice from his edtech experience: avoid the "death by a thousand cuts" of endless unpaid pilots. Instead, seek paid pilots with scoped changes and identify design partners willing to work with the product as-is. A cornerstone customer providing reliable revenue creates options—whether to scale, attract others, or seek investment.

### Fundraising Advice & Sales Resource Allocation (14:17-16:34)

Vrijen strongly advised against seeking VC funding, describing investors unfavorably and noting the difficulty non-technical founders face in being taken seriously. He suggested prioritizing sales execution: either hiring an SDR for outbound prospecting or handling outreach personally as founder.

Vrijen emphasized that founder-led sales provides customer research, network building, and messaging refinement—critical activities for early-stage companies.

Chris noted his brand's tagline "inspired by people like you, created by people like you" and his desire to stay involved in sales. He revealed having 65,000 active email subscribers built over years—a significant distribution asset.

### Product-Market Fit & Niche Definition (16:34-19:12)

Vrijen shared Careerspan's hard-learned lesson about narrowing focus: despite having a general-purpose self-reflection tool applicable to everyone, they discovered not everyone was receptive to the message. They eventually identified their ideal customer as job-seekers willing to engage in self-reflection.

He defined product-market fit using the Supreme Court's obscenity standard: "you know it when you see it"—when you have it, you don't think about it; when you don't, you obsess over it constantly.

Chris expressed concern about backlash from existing users if the product narrows further or features are removed.

### Community, Building in Public, and Authenticity (19:12-22:21)

Vrijen recommended building in public given the emotive nature of executive function coaching. He suggested leveraging Chris's 65,000-email list for distribution.

Chris proposed creating a Facebook group for users to receive updates and provide feedback on feature considerations—potentially aligning with his brand of "for people like you."

Vrijen outlined a spectrum of community approaches: one-to-many broadcasting → platforms with comment sections → private user research groups → full communities. He cautioned that with limited resources (Chris and Joe only), time allocation requires careful prioritization.

### Authenticity, Personal Connection, and Networking (22:40-27:03)

Chris emphasized the advantage of building for his own community—having ADHD himself and being a former teacher/coach provides validation and trust with users. He expressed reluctance around social media due to authenticity concerns and discomfort with inauthentic performance, especially given his work with children and parents.

Vrijen countered that social media only works long-term when done authentically—owning one's style rather than performing. He acknowledged Chris's challenge: wanting to serve children while also maintaining personal authenticity. He suggested channel separation for different audiences.

Vrijen invited Chris to join the Future of Higher Education group (6,000 members globally, ~1,000 in NYC, ~1,000 in Boston), where Vrijen serves as NYC co-lead. He offered to make introductions once Chris locks down a linchpin school deal. Both agreed edtech attracts genuinely kind people compared to other tech sectors.

### Closing & Discovery Story (26:35-28:24)

Chris asked how Vrijen found their coaching practice. Vrijen revealed his wife discovered them during her search for executive function coaching. Vrijen had initially spoken to Amy (Korean, client onboarding specialist) before connecting with Heather.

Both expressed appreciation for their teams and the entrepreneurial lifestyle—being able to work with people they like, stay true to themselves, and work from home. They acknowledged the trade-offs, including the stress of being responsible for everything.
