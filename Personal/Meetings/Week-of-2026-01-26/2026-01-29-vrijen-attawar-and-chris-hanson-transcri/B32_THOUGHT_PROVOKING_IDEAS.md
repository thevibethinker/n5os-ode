# B32_THOUGHT_PROVOKING_IDEAS

### Narrowness Always Seems Too Broad

**Speaker**: Vrijen
**Classification**: V_POSITION
**Domain**: product-market-fit, strategy, careerspan

**The Spark**:
"One of my big learnings in retrospect is every time I thought we were being too narrow, we were still being too broad."

**The Insight**:
Founders systematically underestimate how much they need to narrow their focus. The instinct is to believe you've gone narrow enough, but this is almost always false because your brain is optimized for breadth—you see connections and possibilities everywhere. What feels like a laser-focused niche to you is still a meandering river to the market. The pattern repeats: you think you're at the point, but you're still at the set; you think you're at the set, but you're still at the category.

**The Principle**:
Human perception of focus is relative, not absolute. What founders experience as "narrowing down" is actually moving from "everything" to "a lot of things." The market experiences this as "still no signal." This is the same principle behind why engineers estimate projects at 2x and it takes 4x—optimism bias operates at every level of abstraction. The marketplace requires an order of magnitude more specificity than founders intuitively believe necessary. Product-market fit is not reached by shaving percentage points off the addressable market; it's reached by slicing to the point where your offering becomes the obvious, only choice for a specific person in a specific context.

**The Stakes**:
Early-stage strategy should default to aggressive narrowing, not careful calibration. The cost of being too narrow is finding out quickly that the slice is too small—you can always broaden later. The cost of being too broad is years of unfocused development, feature creep, and pilot purgatory where you're building everything for nobody. When in doubt, cut.

**Boundary Conditions**:
Applies when you have a generalizable capability that could serve many audiences. Less relevant when you're truly constrained to a narrow vertical by regulation or physics (e.g., medical devices). Also breaks down when the founder has genuine domain expertise and customer intimacy—in those cases, the bias can flip toward being too narrow too early.

### Product-Market Fit: The Obsession Test

**Speaker**: Vrijen
**Classification**: V_POSITION
**Domain**: product-market-fit, founder-psychology

**The Spark**:
"It's like the same way the Supreme Court defines porn. It's like if you see it, you know, if you see it, you know it... Product market fit is when you have it, you don't think about it. And when you don't have it, you fucking thinking about it all the time."

**The Insight**:
Product-market fit is not a metric you measure—it's an absence of a certain type of thinking. When founders have PMF, they stop obsessing over whether they have it. The question dissolves. The absence of the question is the signal. This reframes PMF from a destination you reach to a state of anxiety that lifts. The "obsession" is the tell.

**The Principle**:
Human attention tracks information deficits. We obsess over what we don't have, what's uncertain, what's unresolved. When the uncertainty resolves, the mental energy allocated to it evaporates. This is why newly diagnosed conditions dominate our consciousness until we understand them—and then they recede into background knowledge. PMF operates on the same psychological mechanism: it's the resolution of a persistent "do people want this" uncertainty loop. The loop's silence is the success condition.

**The Stakes**:
Founders should monitor their own psychological state as a leading indicator. If you're still asking "do we have PMF?" you don't. The question itself is evidence of the negative. This shifts focus from vanity metrics (signups, engagement, revenue) to internal state: are you still anxious about whether people want this? The answer is binary and you already know it.

**Boundary Conditions**:
Applies when you're genuinely uncertain about market demand. Can be masked by delusion—founders can convince themselves they're not obsessing while ignoring reality. Also breaks down in businesses where PMF is a continuous, rolling optimization rather than a one-time event (e.g., marketplace businesses, platform plays). In those cases, the obsession transforms but doesn't disappear—it just shifts to new questions.

### Authenticity As a Strategy, Not a Virtue

**Speaker**: Vrijen
**Classification**: V_POSITION
**Domain**: branding, social-media, founder-psychology

**The Insight**:
"The only way the social media thing works for folks in the long run is when they do it their way."

**The Spark**:
Authenticity is sustainable; performance is not. The founders who succeed long-term on social media aren't the ones who master the algorithm or adopt the polished influencer playbook—they're the ones who are themselves in a way that doesn't require ongoing willpower. If you have to perform, you will eventually burn out. If you are, you can persist.

**The Principle**:
Sustained behavior requires alignment with identity, not just alignment with incentives. This is why New Year's resolutions fail but identity shifts stick. When you act against your nature, you're spending a finite resource (willpower) every time you post. When you act from your nature, you're expending almost nothing. The compound effect over months and years is dramatic. The algorithm optimizes for engagement, but human endurance optimizes for alignment.

**The Stakes**:
Social media strategy should start from "who am I?" not "what works?" The second question is irrelevant without the first. If you curse, curse. If you're wholesome, be wholesome. If you're quiet, say less. The founder who tries to fake it will eventually quit—the founder who is it will still be there when the algorithm favors their style. This flips the typical advice: don't study the platform, study yourself.

**Boundary Conditions**:
Applies to founder-led brands where personal identity is core to the value proposition. Breaks down when the brand must serve audiences with conflicting values (e.g., kids vs. adults, parents vs. teens). In those cases, channel segmentation becomes necessary—not to fake authenticity across all channels, but to create authentic silos that don't bleed into each other.
