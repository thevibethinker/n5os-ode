## Meeting Overview

A strategic advisory conversation between Vrijen Attawar (founder of Careerspan, currently working with executive function coach Heather) and Chris Hanson (special education teacher turned edtech founder building an ADHD/executive function coaching product). Vrijen provided startup, product development, and sales guidance to Chris, who is navigating B2B/B2C edtech monetization strategies after nearly a year of product development.

## Chronological Discussion

### Opening & Personal Context (0:00-3:00)

The conversation opened with light personal exchange about Vrijen's voice quality and vaping habits, which segued into a discussion about working with Heather for executive function coaching. Chris expressed curiosity about the language around "bad habits" and whether that framing had softened. Vrijen noted that while he typically views habits as behaviors rather than morally good or bad, on less disciplined days the "bad habits" framing resurfaces. This established a rapport around shared ADHD experiences and the psychological complexity of behavior change.

### Product Building Journey (3:00-8:30)

Chris revealed his background as a special education teacher turned small business owner who is not a developer but has built an AI-powered executive function coaching product over nearly a year. He acknowledged the challenges of feature creep and the gratification of turning an idea into reality rather than leaving it in his head. Vrijen, drawing from his own non-technical founder journey at Careerspan, praised Chris for building a well-executed product without a technical background. Chris explained he had help from Joe Coleman, described as a "tech handyman" rather than an expert developer. Vrijen validated the difficulty of articulating product methodology and domain expertise to technical partners, noting this is often the hardest part of the process.

### Monetization & Sales Strategy (8:30-14:30)

Chris outlined his monetization strategy: B2B/B2G sales to organizations, school districts, and clinics rather than individual consumers. The current landing page offers individual trials at $10/month and supporter accounts (up to 10 profiles) at $20/month, with a request-for-quote option for organizations. Chris expressed concern about organizations demanding new features as a condition of purchase, creating a chicken-or-egg problem where significant feature development might be required for large contracts he cannot afford to deliver.

Vrijen advised seeking "linchpin deals" or design partners—organizations willing to pay something for a pilot with limited custom feature requests rather than death by a thousand cuts through endless pilots. He emphasized finding a cornerstone customer who provides reliable revenue and proof points for fundraising or further sales. Vrijen strongly advised against pursuing VC funding, suggesting instead that Chris hire a sales development representative (SDR) to handle outbound prospecting while Chris retains closing duties for customer research and messaging refinement.

### Product-Market Fit & Niche Refinement (14:30-22:40)

Vrijen shared his Careerspan journey of starting with a general-purpose self-reflection tool and eventually narrowing to job seekers who would pay for self-insight when it served a clear outcome. He explained product-market fit as something visceral—when you have it, you don't think about it; when you don't, it's constantly on your mind. Chris expressed concern about further narrowing the niche and potential backlash from users if features are removed. Vrijen recommended transparent communication with users, building in public, and leveraging emotional authenticity as a brand advantage, especially given the emotive nature of executive function coaching.

Vrijen suggested Chris leverage his 65,000 email subscribers and consider creating a user research group or community to engage committed users on new features and direction. He outlined a spectrum of community options from one-to-many communication (email) to private user groups to full community ecosystems, advising Chris to choose based on time efficiency given his small team.

### Social Media, Authenticity & Networking (22:40-28:24)

Chris expressed reluctance toward social media, citing discomfort with inauthenticity and concern about cursing conflicting with his work with children and parents. Vrijen encouraged authenticity—doing social media "your way"—and suggested separating personal and professional personas across different channels. He recommended Chris apply to FOHi (Future of Higher Education), a 6,000-member Slack community where Vrijen is New York co-lead, noting it's a high-quality source for K-12 and higher education leads. The conversation concluded with personal exchange about locations (Chris in Kent, Washington between Seattle and Tacoma; Vrijen in Brooklyn) and how Vrijen discovered Chris's business through his wife finding executive function coaching, connecting with Amy for onboarding, and then Heather.

## Key Takeaways

- Chris Hanson, a special education teacher turned edtech founder, has built an AI-powered executive function coaching product over ~1 year with help from technical advisor Joe Coleman, operating without external funding or investors
- Primary monetization strategy targets B2B/B2G (school districts, clinics, organizations) with individual trials at $10/month and supporter accounts at $20/month, but faces the classic enterprise sales challenge of feature demand in exchange for contracts
- Vrijen advised seeking "linchpin" design partner customers who pay for pilots with limited custom requirements rather than endless feature creep, and strongly recommended avoiding VC funding in favor of hiring an SDR for outbound prospecting
- Careerspan's journey from general-purpose self-reflection to job-seeker niche was offered as a model for product-market fit learning—starting broad then narrowing to outcomes people will pay for
- Chris has strong assets to leverage: 65,000 email subscribers, domain expertise as someone with ADHD who was a teacher, and authentic brand positioning; Vrijen recommended building in public, creating a user research group, and joining FOHi (Future of Higher Education) for networking leads
- Both founders share non-technical backgrounds and emphasized the difficulty of translating domain expertise into technical product specifications with development partners
