# B21: Key Quotes & Strategic Questions

## Key Quotes

**"Parents obsess over the SAT, not because they believe the SAT is the best measure of human capability, but it's because it's numbers. Numbers are concrete. They're discrete. They're relative. Someone's better, someone's worse."**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Context:* Explaining why the resume persists despite being inadequate—we crave concrete, comparable metrics to evaluate people, even when those metrics flatten multidimensional individuals into two axes.

**"Whatever you do in your work has to be some sort of visible link between you and the service or product that's being sold. Because otherwise, what differentiates an AI, a kind of smarter person with an AI that helps them do a lot of these tasks that you are just solely responsible for?"**  
— Maximilian Kwok  
*Context:* Articulating a fundamental shift in what makes someone valuable in an AI world—direct, visible connection to product/revenue is now the differentiator. Abstract coordination roles without product ownership are being automated away.

**"It's in no one's interest to create the infra for transmitting the signal because there's actually no, there's like, there's no real economic reward for acquiring and processing the signal to the extent and to the standard that I think you and I would agree needs to be had to properly evaluate human beings at scale."**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Context:* Identifying the tragedy of the commons in hiring infrastructure—no one has incentive to build better signal processing, so we're stuck with systems like Workday that are "dial up" when we need "broadband."

**"We learned this early on in our journey—not early enough because it took some years—is like, you know, the closest thing, the only thing that businesses care about is if you can add value to whatever they're making. And that unfortunately moves the goalpost when it comes to assessing whatever that result is."**  
— Maximilian Kwok  
*Context:* Hard-won lesson about assessment: you can't assess in isolation—value only exists in context of what the business sells, and that context shifts constantly. This is why assessments fail—they're disconnected from actual business value.

**"More they do that, more it's a hassle. Like it's just a perfect storm. And the problem is that it's not just that people are hitting themselves, it's that they don't know that they're hitting themselves."**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Context:* Describing the self-destructive cycle of modern job search—hassle → cutting corners → hurting chances → more hassle—and how people can't see they're sabotaging themselves. This creates demand for solutions, but also reflects broken systems.

**"The closest thing that the only thing that businesses care about is if you can add value to whatever they're making."**  
— Maximilian Kwok  
*Context:* Crystallizing the brutal truth of hiring: everything else is proxy. Skills, experience, pedigree—all must map back to adding value to what the company actually sells.

## Strategic Questions Raised

**"What do you think is the biggest obstacle to revenue generation right now?"**  
— Maximilian Kwok  
*Significance:* Cut directly to the core challenge Vrijen faces in a compressed timeframe. Revealed the marketplace chicken-and-egg problem: need both talent supply and founder demand bootstrapped simultaneously.

**"Are you envisioning this to be an assessment in the sense that you are like evaluating them or is it meant to be a means for them to create this portfolio that is then the thing that they are evaluated on?"**  
— Vrijen Attawar  
*Significance:* Distinguished two fundamentally different product directions—assessment (hard to legitimize) vs portfolio creation (more authentic). Maximilian's choice of the latter reflects learning about assessment legitimacy challenges.

**"Does having a really good HackerRank assessment actually say that you're a good engineer and can provide value to the company or the team or hiring manager?"**  
— Maximilian Kwok  
*Significance:* Challenging the core premise of assessment tools. The answer is no—technical proficiency ≠ business value. This is why assessments struggle: they're measuring the wrong thing.

**"Why does one vet charge \$300 for an annual checkup in San Francisco while another charges \$120 20 miles away in San Bruno for the same service?"**  
— Implicit in the conversation  
*Significance:* Revealed the dramatic economics of urban vs suburban pricing—wealth concentration in the 7x7 mile square of SF creates massive price premiums that drop sharply at the city limit. Highlights power of location economics and time premium of urban professionals.

**"What differentiates an AI, a kind of smarter person with an AI that helps them do a lot of these tasks that you are just solely responsible for?"**  
— Maximilian Kwok  
*Significance:* The existential question for many white-collar roles in the AI era. The answer emerging is: direct, visible link to product/service value. Without that connection, roles are vulnerable to automation.

**"Why are resume generation companies still being made in a post-AI era?"**  
— Implicit in Vrijen's observation  
*Significance:* Points to a painful reality: the resume succeeds because it serves the biggest market, even if it's inadequate. Novel hiring signals are small because the market has been burned by snake oil assessments. Reveals the tension between quality and market size.
