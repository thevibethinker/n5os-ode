# Detailed Recap (B01)

1. **Personal check‑in and health/family context**
   - Kamina and Vrijen open with informal catching up: hair, life, and the emotional weight of recent bad news and illness in Kamina’s family.
   - They briefly reflect on mortality and how quickly life can change, using the death of Rob Reiner as a touchpoint.

2. **Debrief on Kamina’s recent event**
   - Kamina reports her latest multi‑school networking event as the biggest so far (~260–270 students) with exceptionally strong feedback (“off the freaking charts”).
   - High‑profile speakers included Jim Malatras (Chief Strategy Officer at FedCap, a ~$1B org) and Peabody/Emmy‑winning journalist Tiwa Chang.
   - Lawrence (SideHustles.com founder who sold a prior company for ~$150M) partially funded the event and has committed to funding three more in the spring; he trusts Kamina’s work enough to send checks without even attending.

3. **Careerspan status: momentum plus acquisition exploration**
   - Vrijen shares that Careerspan has pivoted more towards hiring and is now in active conversations about purchase/acqui‑hire scenarios.
   - He is candid that as first‑time founders they likely “missed the boat” on the original wedge and moved too slowly, but remains passionate about the underlying problem.
   - There is concern about a “PR exit” where nobody makes meaningful money; the goal is a real outcome that rewards the team (Logan, Ilsa), advisors, and supporters like Kamina.

4. **University pipeline: UMass proof and re‑engaging schools**
   - Vrijen walks through strong UMass Amherst results from an undergrad business cohort: ~92% reported Careerspan strengthened their experiences, ~80% discovered additional experiences/angles, and confidence lift was material.
   - He notes interest from Rice (conversations with Felix and Jim Pike), ongoing ties to Columbia (via Diane), and a revived conversation with Cornell Johnson (MBA program) now considering adopting Careerspan as a tool two years after initial outreach.
   - Kamina challenges the idea that it is “too late” for schools: most institutions still “have nothing,” and in the college space “the game hasn’t even started.”

5. **Structural frustration with career centers and school behavior**
   - Kamina vents at length about institutional inertia and excuses: schools balk at $20 tickets while charging students $100k+, unions object to event promotion, and administrators fail to prioritize students’ actual outcomes.
   - She contrasts this with the students’ feedback (“this is the only event where people actually cared about me”) and her own willingness to put skin in the game (personally buying tickets if needed).
   - The subtext: demand from students is strong, but institutional gatekeepers are slow, cautious, and sometimes complacent.

6. **Business model and pricing discussion**
   - Vrijen outlines two models for schools: (a) sell to schools directly as a VMock‑replacement at ~$10–15k, or (b) let employers buy access to Careerspan portfolios and give schools a near‑cost license, with fees tied to student usage.
   - Kamina encourages charging something even for resource‑constrained institutions (e.g., University of Mount Saint Vincent) to avoid “free shelfware,” with possible discounts for smaller/HBCU or “Brown” schools.

7. **Partnership and event pipeline**
   - Kamina invites Vrijen to upcoming events at:
     - University of Mount Saint Vincent (where Lawrence now works and where unsupportive staff were recently fired).
     - BMCC (a program with ~1,000 students and a strong director).
     - St. Peter’s University.
   - Her framing: these events put Careerspan directly in front of decision‑makers and highly motivated students; simply “showing up” has asymmetric upside for pipeline.

8. **Strategic exit pathways: ASA, Strada, and national workforce orgs**
   - They discuss ASA (recent acquirer of Clarity) as a strong potential acquirer for Careerspan; Logan has already spoken to Steph at ASA about deeper integration.
   - Kamina offers to “put a bee in Jane’s ear” at ASA and to re‑engage Darren at Strada, positioning Careerspan as a solution for HBCUs and other institutions they fund.
   - They frame a notional deal structure where, for example, a $1M outcome would include a ~$300k success fee/commission to Kamina.
   - Additional national programs in scope for intros: Per Scholas, COOP, Per Scholas’ new executive director, and other large workforce initiatives.

9. **Concrete go‑to‑market experiment: email blast to schools**
   - Kamina advises Vrijen to send a direct email blast to prior and current campus contacts summarizing 2025 success and positioning 2026 as “10x bigger,” with subject lines like:
     - “Rice, Columbia, UMass increasing usage in 2026,” or
     - “We killed it in 2025; 2026 is going to be 10x better.”
   - She recommends naming marquee schools (Rice, Columbia, Cornell, UMass) in the subject and *not* including those institutions on the actual recipient list to create quiet shame and FOMO.
   - She urges including specific proof points (UMass study metrics, logos of Rice/Columbia/Cornell) and suggests sending the campaign from a personal sender (e.g., “Bridget” style sender, not a generic contact@ address).

10. **Division of labor and immediate next steps**
    - Vrijen commits to:
      - Drafting and sending the email blast to school partners and prospects.
      - Following up with Rice (Felix & Jim Pike) and Cornell Johnson.
      - Sharing the UMass results and a tight metrics summary with Kamina to arm her for ASA/Strada conversations.
      - Talking with Logan about whether and how Kamina should approach Jane at ASA about acquisition.
    - Kamina commits to:
      - Reaching out to Strada and ASA once she has a solid metrics pack from Careerspan.
      - Exploring Strada funding or acquisition fit for Careerspan, especially in support of HBCUs.
      - Using her relationships at Per Scholas, COOP, and other national programs to seed additional pilots or acquisition interest once proof is in hand.
    - They close on a collaborative tone: Kamina wants the outcome to be both financially meaningful and genuinely useful to students; Vrijen reiterates that he does not intend to “make any money before she does.”
