# Key Moments (B21)

> **Note:** Timestamps are approximate and based on transcript ordering rather than precise playback.

1. **Kamina’s event validation and sponsorship momentum**
   - Kamina describes her latest event as the biggest and best yet, with ~260–270 students and 200+ reviews posted the same day.
   - She reveals that Lawrence (SideHustles) has funded the event and committed to three more in the spring without ever attending in person—an unusually high‑trust sponsorship signal.

2. **Vrijen’s candid admission about execution and timing**
   - Vrijen acknowledges that as first‑time founders they “may have missed the boat” on the original wedge and moved too slowly.
   - He still expresses strong conviction about the underlying problem and a desire to create a real exit that treats employees and advisors fairly.

3. **UMass proof points as an inflection in credibility**
   - The discussion of UMass Eisenberg results (92% reporting stronger experiences, 80% discovering new angles, confidence gains) marks a shift from “intuition” to **evidence‑backed** claims.
   - Kamina seizes on this as ammunition that schools “have nothing” today and cannot dismiss Careerspan as speculative.

4. **“The game hasn’t even started” — reframing market timing**
   - Kamina pushes back hard on the idea that Careerspan is “too late,” arguing that in the college market the game has barely begun.
   - This reframing moves the conversation from regret about the past to opportunity in 2026.

5. **Institutional indictment: excuses vs. student demand**
   - Kamina details how CUNY, Columbia, NYU, and others refused to pay nominal costs or promote events even when students clearly valued them.
   - Her monologue about unions, finals‑week excuses, and six‑figure salaries funded by tuition surfaces a powerful narrative: institutions, not students, are the bottleneck.

6. **Acquisition vector crystallizes: ASA and Strada**
   - They explicitly identify ASA and Strada as prime candidates to fund or acquire Careerspan, with Kamina volunteering to reach out to Jane (ASA) and Darren (Strada).
   - The proposed structure (e.g., $1M outcome with a meaningful commission to Kamina) clarifies that there is a serious intent to pursue this path, not just idle brainstorming.

7. **Go‑to‑market “email blast” play is agreed**
   - Kamina and Vrijen converge on a concrete move: an email blast naming Rice, Columbia, UMass, and Cornell and highlighting UMass results.
   - Kamina’s advice to omit certain schools from the recipient list to induce quiet shame/FOMO is a notable tactical insight.

8. **Commitment to concrete reciprocity**
   - Vrijen explicitly tells Kamina he does not intend to make money before she does and is willing to structure a commission for deals she catalyzes.
   - This cements her role not only as a moral supporter but as a financially aligned partner.

9. **National program opportunity (HBCUs, Per Scholas, COOP)**
   - Kamina emphasizes that historically Black colleges and national workforce programs “need this stuff,” and that she has direct lines into key decision‑makers.
   - This expands the perceived addressable impact from elite MBAs to a broader, more mission‑aligned ecosystem.

Together, these moments move the relationship from general mutual support to a concrete, shared plan: run a data‑backed GTM push, then leverage Kamina’s network (ASA, Strada, national programs) to explore a high‑leverage outcome for Careerspan.
