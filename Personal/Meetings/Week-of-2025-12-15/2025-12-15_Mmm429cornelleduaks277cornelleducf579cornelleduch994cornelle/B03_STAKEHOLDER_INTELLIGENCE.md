# Stakeholder Intelligence (B03)

## 1. Michael Maher — Senior CMC leader, primary evaluator

**Role / Authority**
- Appears to be a senior decision‑maker in the Johnson Career Management Center (CMC), likely director‑level.
- Leads the review of the “toolbox” of career resources, starting with resume tools.

**Interests & Goals**
- Reduce **manual, repetitive resume work** for staff while improving outcomes for students.
- Bring AI into the process in a *meaningful* way, not as superficial ATS scoring.
- Maintain Johnson’s brand standards: consistent templates, quality, and fairness across students.

**Attitude Toward Careerspan**
- Warm and open; has known about Careerspan for some time and has been “intrigued” by its different approach.
- Positions Careerspan as the *first* vendor in the new evaluation wave—an implicit sign of trust.
- Emphasizes that they are still early and cannot commit yet; wants references and data to de‑risk the choice.

**Leverage Points**
- If convinced, he can likely champion Careerspan as the default reflection/resume tool for incoming classes.
- Values proof over rhetoric—UMass results, Aspire data, and peer‑school references will matter more than philosophy alone.

---

## 2. Amanda Moser — Coach/advisor with strong student empathy

**Role / Focus**
- Works directly with students on resumes and career prep; highly tuned to student experience and staff workload.

**Interests & Concerns**
- Wants resumes tailored to specific roles while still fitting Johnson’s template.
- Worries about **generic AI output**: she notes current tools can generate nearly identical bullets for very different jobs.
- Sensitive to the risks of poorly written professional summaries and how they interact with ATS.

**Signals from the call**
- Appreciates the idea of professional summaries *when specific*, but usually tells students to remove them if vague.
- Likes Careerspan’s referral‑tagging and “BFF Chad”‑style features (they feel human and helpful).

**Leverage Points**
- If Amanda sees Careerspan consistently producing distinctive, high‑quality bullets that reduce her editing burden, she will become a powerful internal advocate.
- She will likely insist on clear student‑facing guidance, short how‑tos, and examples to lower activation energy.

---

## 3. Victoria Preble — Operations and quality control

**Role / Focus**
- Appears to handle operational and quality aspects of resumes (formatting, consistency, error‑checking).

**Primary Concerns**
- **Human error risk** when students copy/paste content from Careerspan into Johnson templates.
- Need for flexibility: wants the ability to adjust flows (e.g., how content is exported) if they see recurring student issues.
- Visibility into **usage and adoption metrics** to report up to leadership.

**Leverage Points**
- Clear, robust usage exports (stories per student, resumes generated, vibe checks) will help her justify the tool.
- Simple student workflows (e.g., “copy as plain text, paste here”) plus micro‑copy and screenshots can address her formatting worries.

---

## 4. Chase Hudson — Admin/process lens

**Role / Focus**
- Leans into questions about provisioning, reminders, and analytics.

**Interests & Concerns**
- Wants low‑friction account setup: prefers an org code model over bulk CSV uploads.
- Asks explicitly about **automated reminders** to nudge students who haven’t engaged.
- Interested in granular adoption data and the ability to share summary stats with leadership on a recurring basis.

**Leverage Points**
- Automated SendGrid‑style nudges tied to usage events (no story yet, no resume yet) will speak directly to Chase’s priorities.
- Clean CSV exports and recurring “health” summaries from Careerspan can make the tool feel like a managed system, not just another app.

## 5. Vrijen Attawar — Founder / alumni partner

**Role / Position**
- Johnson alumnus and co‑founder of Careerspan.
- Functions as both vendor and internal ally: understands CMC constraints from his own MBA experience.

**Approach & Attitude**
- Transparent about product evolution and limitations; does not oversell.
- Frames Careerspan as a way to move CMC staff up the value chain, not as a replacement for their judgment.
- Willing to build **Johnson‑specific** content (demos, 101s, office hours) and to connect staff with other institutional users.

**Leverage Points**
- Alumni status plus lived experience of Johnson job search gives him credibility and empathy.
- If he can deliver clear UMass/peer proof and bespoke Johnson onboarding, he can lock in trust and accelerate internal consensus.

---

## 6. Overall power and risk map

- **Decision nucleus:** Michael (strategy) + Amanda/Victoria/Chase (UX, quality, operations).
- **Biggest concerns:**
  - Student cognitive load and getting started.
  - Quality and distinctiveness of AI‑generated content.
  - Data privacy and student control.
- **Most promising levers:**
  - Evidence from similar schools (UMass, Columbia SPS) and alumni case studies.
  - Simple, high‑touch onboarding (videos + office hours) during admit/pre‑MBA phases.
  - Strong admin tools: exports, reminders, and coach dashboards that feel like a system, not a toy.

If Careerspan can reduce resume friction while preserving (or improving) quality and giving the CMC better visibility, this group is well‑positioned to champion it internally.
