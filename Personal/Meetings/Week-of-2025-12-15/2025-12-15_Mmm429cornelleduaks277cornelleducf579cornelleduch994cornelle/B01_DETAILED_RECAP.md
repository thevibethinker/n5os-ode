# Detailed Recap (B01)

1. **Introductions and context**
   - Participants include Michael Maher, Amanda Moser, Victoria Preble, Chase Hudson (Johnson Career Management Center), and Vrijen.
   - Michael frames the meeting: Johnson is reassessing its “toolbox” of career resources, starting with resume tools. Careerspan is the **first** resource they are evaluating in this cycle.
   - He is particularly interested in using AI in a “new and different way” to reduce resume work for both students and staff.

2. **Vrijen’s background and problem framing**
   - Vrijen briefly recounts his path: Johnson MBA ’22, prior work in college admissions and career coaching (largely in Singapore), and learning what it means to be a successful US white‑collar professional through Johnson.
   - He highlights unmet needs around **self‑understanding** (what a student brings to the table) that sit one layer below mechanical resume generation.

3. **Candidate experience demo (Donna persona)**
   - Vrijen demos a simulated candidate account for “Donna,” a product manager.
   - Flow:
     - Student submits a resume and a few basic preferences, then lands on a dashboard.
     - They can paste job descriptions and run a “vibe check” to see alignment, backed by underlying stories.
     - They can enter stories via typed conversation or voice (click‑to‑record “rambling” that the AI cleans up and structures).
   - The AI coach:
     - Asks adaptive, follow‑up questions rather than a fixed script.
     - Produces STAR‑formatted interview answers and multiple resume bullets per story.
     - Surfaces strengths and gaps per role.

4. **Coach / admin view demo**
   - Vrijen switches to the counselor dashboard:
     - Shows how staff can see which students have completed stories, generated resumes, and run vibe checks.
     - Demonstrates per‑student insights: story summaries, competency coverage, and alignment to job descriptions.
   - Emphasis: the coach view allows CMC to operate at a **higher level of abstraction** (“goals, objectives, strategy”), not just edit bullets line‑by‑line.

5. **Discussion of story depth, bullet mapping, and ATS**
   - Michael and team probe how many stories are needed and how they map to resume bullets.
   - Vrijen uses the “sphere and slice” analogy: each rich story can yield multiple bullets, but you don’t want to repeat the same competency endlessly.
   - He argues ATS‑only optimization is increasingly obsolete: inbound funnels are clogged, smaller companies ignore generic inbound and rely on referrals/warm outreach, so **meaningful storytelling and self‑advocacy** matter more than gaming keyword filters.

6. **Template and formatting concerns**
   - Amanda asks if Johnson’s standard resume template can be enforced inside Careerspan.
   - Vrijen explains the deliberate choice:
     - Careerspan outputs clean text (bullets, summary) that students **copy into** the Johnson template.
     - This avoids fighting with Word table logic and lets CMC maintain its exact formatting standards.
   - Victoria raises concerns about human error when students copy/paste; Vrijen acknowledges the risk but notes that:
     - Students typically converge on a stable “base resume,” then swap in tailored bullets/summary for specific roles.
     - Careerspan can teach a simple “paste as plain text” workflow to preserve formatting.

7. **Adoption, timing, and student workload**
   - Michael outlines current timelines:
     - Early recruiting (primarily consulting/diversity) starts around April, even before students arrive on campus.
     - Students are asked to have a resume draft by August 1; some comply early, others wait until the first October deadline.
   - Amanda notes that many students *want* to start working on resumes pre‑matriculation and would benefit from a guided tool beyond JTS.
   - Vrijen suggests giving incoming students access to Careerspan as soon as they are admitted, framing it as an early‑reflection tool that also produces ready‑to‑use bullets and interview answers.

8. **Usage behavior and story completion rates**
   - Chase asks about how many stories students typically complete and how long it takes.
   - Vrijen shares data:
     - Cold web users: ~10–15% go deep enough to tell stories.
     - In a course‑integrated context (UMass Eisenberg), ~90% of students completed at least three stories when it was assigned work.
   - He reiterates that early MBA timing (just after admission / early summer) is ideal: students are highly motivated and uncertain, so they welcome structured reflection.

9. **Admin, data, and privacy questions**
   - Chase asks about account provisioning; Vrijen explains:
     - Students join via an org code; no CSV upload is required.
     - Admin overhead is limited to communicating the link/code and nudging students.
   - They explore automated reminders: Careerspan can use SendGrid to send periodic nudges to students who haven’t created stories yet.
   - On privacy and AI usage:
     - Requests to external LLMs are flagged “do not train”; student data is not used to train external models.
     - Within Careerspan, detailed conversation data is not shown to employers—only high‑level summaries.
     - Students can request data deletion; Careerspan will purge their data within ~48 hours.
     - Accounts belong to students; they can disconnect from Johnson while retaining their personal data and stories.

10. **Evaluation next steps and references**
    - Michael stresses they are early in the evaluation and won’t decide immediately; Careerspan is first in a series of tools to assess.
    - He asks to speak with other institutional users; Vrijen commits to:
      - Connecting them to Diane (Columbia SPS) and leaders at UMass Eisenberg.
      - Sharing survey results and proof‑point summaries from UMass and Aspire.
    - There is interest in Johnson‑specific onboarding materials (short demos, 101s) and possibly office hours to help students get started.

Overall, the meeting positions Careerspan as a serious candidate to replace or complement current resume tooling, with clear follow‑ups around references, data sharing, and onboarding support—but **no formal pilot or purchasing decision** is made yet.
