# B32: Thought-Provoking Ideas

### 1. The Carcinization of HR Tech (Convergent Evolution of Mediocrity)
Vrijen posits a "crabs of HR tech" theory—the idea that regardless of the starting point, all bad HR technology eventually evolves into the same failed design patterns: the resume, the static profile, and the basic ATS. This suggests a structural gravity in the industry that forces innovation back into legacy containers, implying that to truly escape the "crab" state, one must break the underlying biological (structural) constraints of the talent market, not just the interface.

### 2. Acquisitions as "Funny Churn"
Alec notes that SourceStack is seeing customers churn not because they are unhappy, but because they are being acquired—and then the *acquirers* become the customers. This re-contextualizes "churn" from a signal of product failure to a signal of "ecosystem success." It suggests a meta-strategy where a startup's value is validated by its customers' liquidity events, creating a "nice rollover" effect that bypasses traditional sales cycles.

### 3. The "Resume Builder" Existential Crisis
The realization that LLMs have turned "resume building" from a high-friction WYSIWYG service into a "one-shot" commodity creates a specific flavor of fear in the market. The idea here isn't just about automation; it's about the total collapse of a software category's primary value prop. If a resume can be "spun up" instantly, the builder isn't a tool anymore—it's just a skin on a prompt. This identifies a "lagging generation" of tools that are structurally incapable of surviving the shift from "creation" to "generation."
