## Insights from Dylan Lieberman

### INSIGHT 1: AI Resume Tools Creating Adverse Selection in College Hiring

**Core Claim:** AI-powered resume builders and auto-apply tools are flooding company inboxes with low-quality applications, making it harder for genuinely qualified students to stand out, which is creating a backlash against hiring college students generally.

**Evidence Provided:**
- Dylan's friends who are "overqualified for some roles" getting turned down
- Companies receiving hundreds of generic applications per posting
- Rise of AI application tools (mentioned as context for why anphoria exists)

**Dylan's Interpretation:** The arms race between AI applicant tools and AI screening tools isn't helping anyone get hired - it's just creating noise that benefits tool vendors while making hiring worse for both students and companies.

**Validation Status:** PENDING
- [ ] Check with recruiters/hiring managers: Are they seeing application volume spike from AI tools?
- [ ] Research: Is there data showing college internship acceptance rates dropping?
- [ ] Cross-reference: Do other hiring platform founders report similar observations?

**Strategic Implications if True:**
- Work-product based evaluation (like anphoria challenges or Careerspan assessments) becomes MORE valuable as resume screening becomes less reliable
- Companies may pay premium for pre-vetted talent pools that demonstrably can't be gamed by AI tools
- Timing advantage for platforms that solve "signal vs noise" problem

**Confidence Level:** MEDIUM-HIGH (anecdotal but aligns with known trends in AI tool proliferation)

### INSIGHT 2: Business/Creative Students Lack "Passion Project" Infrastructure

**Core Claim:** Business, marketing, and creative students don't have equivalent infrastructure to CS hackathons for building portfolio-worthy projects, which puts them at a disadvantage in internship/job applications.

**Evidence Provided:**
- Major League Hacking exists for CS students but no equivalent for business students
- Business students asking "what do I do for a passion project?"
- No regular financial modeling, marketing challenge, or strategy competitions at scale

**Dylan's Interpretation:** The gap isn't in student desire or capability - it's in infrastructure. CS students have hackathons almost every weekend; business students have to invent their own projects with no structure or validation.

**Validation Status:** PENDING
- [ ] Survey business students: Do they feel disadvantaged vs CS students in internship applications?
- [ ] Check with business school career centers: Is "lack of projects" a common complaint?
- [ ] Research: Are there existing business challenge platforms at scale (Vrijen mentioned case competitions exist but may be different)

**Strategic Implications if True:**
- Large addressable market: Business/creative students outnumber CS students at most schools
- Lower competition: Fewer established players in this space vs tech hackathons
- Viral potential: Creative work is inherently shareable (vs code which is less visual/social)

**Confidence Level:** MEDIUM (plausible but needs validation from business students and career centers)

### INSIGHT 3: Micro-Influencer Seeding Can Solve Two-Sided Marketplace Cold Start

**Core Claim:** For creative/marketing challenges specifically, seeding competitions with 1-2 micro-influencers (who participate AND promote) creates authentic buzz that attracts both students and companies.

**Evidence Provided:**
- Dylan's personal experience: Instagram video went viral
- Understanding of how creator economy works
- Recognition that creative output is inherently shareable content

**Dylan's Interpretation:** Unlike code (which is abstract), marketing campaigns and creative work are visual/social, so participants naturally want to share. Adding 1-2 influencers creates template for what "good" looks like and signals quality to both companies and students.

**Validation Status:** PENDING
- [ ] Research: Have other platforms successfully used micro-influencer seeding?
- [ ] Test hypothesis: Would creators actually participate in anphoria challenges?
- [ ] Check economics: Can platform afford influencer fees at early stage, or does it need barter deals?

**Strategic Implications if True:**
- Distribution strategy: Budget for micro-influencer partnerships rather than paid ads
- Content strategy: Showcase winner submissions as social content
- Positioning: Market anphoria as a way for students to "build portfolio and get discovered"

**Confidence Level:** LOW-MEDIUM (interesting hypothesis but unproven; needs testing)
