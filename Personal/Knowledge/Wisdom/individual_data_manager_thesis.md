# The Individual Data Manager (IDM) Thesis

## Core Claim

The next Salesforce is not a CRM — it's an **Individual Data Manager**.

Salesforce became the system of record for customer relationships. The equivalent opportunity in the AI age is becoming the system of record for an individual's total data profile — their productivity patterns, preferences, communication style, decision history, and working rhythms across every tool and context they operate in.

## Why Now

### The Cenotes Shift

Data is no longer something you move between containers (the Pools era) or pipe between systems via elaborate but brittle integrations (the Waterparks era). Data has become a substrate — an underground system that tools merely provide access points into.

In this world, the strategic question changes from "where do I store my data?" to "who sits at the most valuable checkpoint into my data?"

### The Profile That Doesn't Exist Yet

Every individual has a hypothetical **data profile** — the totality of who they are as a working person:
- How they think and decide
- How they communicate across contexts
- What their natural working rhythms look like
- What knock-on tasks their primary tasks generate
- How they sequence and batch work
- What their attention patterns reveal about priorities

No system currently captures this. Email knows your communications. Calendar knows your meetings. Task managers know your to-dos. But nothing holds the **unified view** of who you are as a productive agent in the world.

## The Strategic Landscape

### Why This Is Bigger Than Productivity

The IDM isn't just a productivity play. It's an identity play.

| Domain | Current System of Record | IDM Opportunity |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Customer relationships | Salesforce (CRM) | — |
| Individual work patterns | *None* | IDM |
| Career/professional identity | LinkedIn (static) | IDM (dynamic, behavioral) |
| Meeting relationships | Calendly (transactional) | IDM (relational, contextual) |
| Knowledge & thinking | Notion/Obsidian (manual) | IDM (auto-accumulated) |

### Who Could Win

**Zo** is reaching for this — the orchestration layer that, over time, accumulates the richest understanding of how an individual thinks and works. Its advantage: platform-agnostic, self-aware, learns from everything.

**Calendly** sits at a high-value checkpoint (the meeting moment) but currently treats it as transactional. If Calendly understood not just *when* you meet but *how* meetings cascade into work, they'd own a dimension of the IDM.

**No one else** is positioned for the full profile. Email clients, task managers, and note-taking apps each hold fragments. The IDM integrates the fragments into a living profile.

## The GDPR Connection

Giving individuals governance over their data profile isn't just ethical — it's a competitive moat.

The IDM that says "we know you deeply but we never use your data to help anyone else" wins. In a world where AI training on user data commoditizes everyone's patterns, the company that keeps your profile private and proprietary to you creates a form of value that cannot be replicated.

This connects to Tope Awotona's deep personal conviction about data privacy — he has never connected his email to a third-party app. The IDM must be built with this level of respect for data sovereignty.

## Relationship to Other Beliefs

- **Cenotes metaphor** (Principle 1): The IDM is the map of the underground water system. Tools are cenotes; the IDM knows which ones you use and how.
- **Instruction collapsing** (Principle 2): The IDM improves instruction quality over time because it knows your patterns — it can fill in context you'd otherwise have to specify.
- **Knock-on task decomposition**: The IDM learns your cascades and can eventually predict what you'll need next.
- **Careerspan thesis**: Careerspan was trying to be the IDM for career identity — helping people articulate who they are as workers. The IDM generalizes this beyond hiring into all of work.

## Open Questions

1. **Bootstrapping problem**: How do you accumulate an individual data profile without requiring massive setup effort? (Answer: minimal touch — the system learns passively from orchestration.)
2. **Portability**: Can individuals take their profile between IDM providers? Should they be able to?
3. **Granularity**: How deep does the profile go before it becomes creepy? Where's the line between "helpful" and "surveillance"?
4. **Monetization**: Does the individual pay for their IDM, or does the value flow to employers/platforms who want access? (V's position: the individual pays. Anything else corrupts the incentive.)
