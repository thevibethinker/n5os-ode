## Origins & Philosophy

### Historical Context (1727)
Benjamin Franklin founded the original Junto Club in Philadelphia in 1727 as a "club for mutual improvement" [^5]. The group consisted of 12 artisans and tradesmen who met weekly to discuss:
- Morals
- Politics
- Natural Philosophy (science)
- Business improvement
- Community betterment

Key principles:
- Mutual improvement over self-interest
- Open intellectual exchange
- Practical betterment of society
- Curated membership of diverse, thoughtful individuals

### Modern Interpretation (Andrew Yeung)
Andrew revived this concept for the modern NYC tech ecosystem, maintaining the core philosophy while adapting it for today's founders, investors, and creatives [^3]. The modern Junto Club is **NOT** [^6]:

**NOT a Networking Event**
- No business card exchanges
- No "working the room"
- Not transactional relationship building

**NOT Purely a Party**
- More substance than social events
- Curated conversations and connections
- Intentional relationship-building

**WHAT IT IS:**
> "A monthly gathering of the most interesting people in NYC" - bringing together tech leaders, founders, cultural innovators, artists, athletes, and academics.

## Event Format & Structure

### Pre-Event Curation
1. **Application**: 5,000+ person waitlist, selective approval
2. **Group Assignment**: Attendees assigned to groups of 10
3. **Pre-Introductions**: Email intros sent beforehand so people arrive knowing names
4. **Theme**: Each event has a thematic focus (tonight: "The Art of Connection")

### During Event
- **Venue**: Rotating private spaces (galleries, rooftops, dinner clubs)
- **Partnership with Dion**: App-enabled social layer allowing you to:
  - Send real drinks to people in the room via app
  - Discover others' backgrounds and interests
  - Break the ice digitally before approaching in person
- **No forced networking**: Organic conversations in curated setting
- **Free admission**: Events are complimentary to remove financial barriers

## Andrew Yeung's Philosophy

**On Networking vs. Community:**
> "People shouldn't have to buy their way into building meaningful relationships."

**On Curation:**
> "I don't quite know how to define these events. They're not 'networking' events. People aren't dishing out business cards. Yet it's not purely social either."

**Vision:**
Build infrastructure for human connection in an era of digital isolation. Events serve as a "taste of community" - some attend once, others become regulars, some form lasting relationships.
