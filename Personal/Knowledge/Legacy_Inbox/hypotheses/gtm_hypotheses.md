## H-GTM-008: Hiring Managers Prefer "Goods Upfront" Over Relationship Building

**Stakeholders:** `[employers - hiring_managers]` `[recruiters]`

**Statement:**
Hiring managers prefer recruiters who show candidate profiles immediately ("goods upfront") over those who try to establish a relationship or schedule discovery calls first. Trust is built through candidate quality, not relationship building.

**Why it matters:**
- If true: Restructure outreach to lead with candidate bundles, not intro calls
- If false: Traditional recruiter relationship-building approach is correct
- Affects: Sales motion, email templates, conversion funnel design

**How to test:**
- A/B test: "Goods upfront" email (candidate URL) vs. "Let's talk" email (meeting request)
- Measure: Response rate, engagement rate, conversion to paid intro
- Track: Qualitative feedback from hiring managers
- Timeline: 20+ outreach attempts per approach

**Status:** `VALIDATED` (High confidence from hiring manager direct feedback)

**Evidence:**

*Supporting:*
- **[Sep 24, 2025]** Alex Caveny (former hiring manager): Direct quote "I don't want to start a conversation with every single recruiter that reaches out. I want to see your candidates." Behavioral validation: ignored all recruiters who tried relationship-building first, only engaged with those showing candidates. "I have no idea who you are and once I start talking you'll never leave me alone." Market gap: "I've never experienced that. Literally no one has ever been like, here's cool candidates I have, do you want to pay for them?" (Meeting: alex-coaching-2025-09-24) [APPROVED]

*Contradicting:*
- None yet

**Implications:**
- Change outreach approach: Send candidate URL in first contact
- Email structure: Minimal copy + link to bundle
- Differentiation: This alone stands out in crowded recruiter market
- Conversion funnel: Bundle engagement drives follow-up, not upfront commitment

**Last updated:** 2025-10-09

## H-GTM-009: HR Resists Disruption Due to Job Security, Target Hiring Managers Instead

**Stakeholders:** `[employers - HR]` `[employers - hiring_managers]`

**Statement:**
HR/talent departments resist new recruiting approaches because their job security depends on maintaining the existing ATS/resume review process. Hiring managers (engineering managers, team leads) are more receptive because they're burned out from resume review and want better solutions.

**Why it matters:**
- If true: Target hiring managers directly, not HR departments
- If false: Continue targeting HR/talent acquisition leads
- Affects: ICP definition, outreach targeting, positioning/messaging

**How to test:**
- Compare: Response rates HR vs. hiring managers
- Track: Objections raised by each persona
- Measure: Deal velocity HR vs. hiring manager initiated
- Timeline: 30+ outreach attempts (15 HR, 15 hiring managers)

**Status:** `VALIDATED` (High confidence from hiring manager insight)

**Evidence:**

*Supporting:*
- **[Sep 24, 2025]** Alex Caveny insight: "HR likes their normal bullshit resume review process. That's their job security." Hiring managers are burned out and want solutions, have authority to skip people from ATS directly. "The main purpose of HR/hiring department is to take the fire hose and give filtered candidates. You're doing that [so go around them]." Incentive misalignment: HR sees disruption as competition to their role. (Meeting: alex-coaching-2025-09-24) [APPROVED]

*Contradicting:*
- None yet

**Implications:**
- **Target change**: Shift ICP from HR/talent teams to hiring managers (engineering managers, team leads)
- **Channel strategy**: Find job postings with hiring manager contact info listed
- **Positioning**: Frame as "hedge on sourcing" not replacement for talent team
- **Messaging**: Emphasize making hiring manager's life easier, not disrupting HR

**Last updated:** 2025-10-09

## November 2024 Update: Mid-Market "Boring Sectors" Hypothesis

**Added:** November 4, 2025  
**Source:** Strategic Intelligence Report (con_kSq36OtO8rmECYtW), Section 5

### H-GTM-010: Mid-Market "Boring Sectors" Are Underserved Opportunity

**Stakeholders:**   

**Statement:**
Mid-market companies in "boring sectors" (healthcare, education, non-profits, government, utilities, manufacturing) face acute talent challenges that Careerspan is uniquely positioned to solve. These sectors cannot compete on compensation with tech giants, but CAN compete on mission, culture, and career development — dimensions Careerspan's behavioral matching captures.

**Why it matters:**
- If true: Massive TAM in overlooked market segment
- If false: Need to focus on tech/high-growth sectors
- Strategic: SHRM partnership unlocks this segment at scale

**Market Sizing:**

**Addressable Market:**
- SHRM represents 340,000+ HR professionals
- 95% are mid-market (not Fortune 500)
- These sectors employ 60M+ US workers
- Chronic talent shortages due to compensation disadvantages

**Pain Points:**
- Cannot outbid tech companies on salary
- Talent gravitates to "sexy" industries (tech, finance, media)
- Résumé-based hiring misses mission-driven candidates
- High turnover when hires are purely compensation-motivated

**Careerspan's Unique Fit:**

**Why behavioral matching matters more here:**

| Traditional Hiring | Careerspan Advantage |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Filters for credentials → Misses non-traditional talent | Surfaces mission-aligned candidates with growth potential |
| Compensation-based competition → Loses to tech | Values-based matching → Finds candidates who prioritize mission |
| High turnover from poor culture fit | Soft skill signals predict cultural alignment |
| Spray-and-pray job boards → Low signal/noise | Engagement-screened candidates → Higher seriousness |

**Value Proposition to Mid-Market:**

**For employers in "boring sectors":**
- "Compete for talent without competing on compensation"
- "Find mission-driven candidates who value your work"
- "Hire for potential and cultural fit, not just credentials"
- "Reduce turnover with better behavioral prediction"

**For candidates:**
- "Discover meaningful careers in sectors you've overlooked"
- "Be evaluated on your potential, not where you went to school"
- "Find roles that match your values, not just your résumé"

**SHRM Strategic Fit:**

**Why SHRM Foundation partnership is perfect:**
- SHRM members overwhelmingly mid-market HR professionals
- Foundation mission = expanding opportunity for underserved workers
- Workforce development focus aligns with Careerspan's candidate-first model
- Distribution channel into exactly the right employer segment

**See:** Knowledge/stakeholder_research/shrm_application_2024.md for full SHRM partnership strategy

**How to test:**

**Phase 1: Partnership validation**
- Track: SHRM Foundation engagement with proposal
- Measure: Interest signals from SHRM member network
- Timeline: Q1 2025 (application response)

**Phase 2: Pilot outcomes**
- Track: Placement rates in mid-market vs. tech sectors
- Measure: Employer satisfaction and repeat hiring
- Compare: Retention rates vs. traditional hiring methods
- Timeline: 6 months post-partnership launch

**Phase 3: Economics validation**
- Track: Revenue per mid-market employer vs. tech employers
- Measure: CAC and LTV by sector
- Compare: Margins and scalability
- Timeline: 12 months of operations

**Status:**  (not yet tested, pending SHRM application outcome)

**Evidence:**

*Supporting:*
- **[Nov 2024]** SHRM Foundation mission aligns with serving underserved markets. Their network is overwhelmingly mid-market. Strategic fit is clear. (Source: Strategic Intelligence Report)
- **[Nov 2024]** Mid-market sectors show chronic talent shortages despite large employment base. Compensation disadvantage creates opening for alternative matching approaches. (Source: Market analysis)
- **[Nov 2024]** Behavioral matching creates differentiation in sectors where culture/mission matter more than credentials. Value prop resonates in theory. (Source: Positioning analysis)

*Contradicting:*
- None yet (hypothesis formulated but not tested)
- Risks: Mid-market may lack budget for premium talent solutions
- Risks: HR decision-makers may be risk-averse (prefer established vendors)
- Risks: Sales cycle may be longer than tech startups
