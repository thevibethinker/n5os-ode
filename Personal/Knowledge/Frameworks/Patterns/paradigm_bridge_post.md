# Reasoning Pattern: Paradigm Bridge Post

## Pattern Overview

A content structure for the middle post in a multi-part series that bridges from problem identification to solution architecture by introducing a fundamental conceptual reframe.

## When to Use

- Multi-post series (3+ posts) where Post 1 establishes crisis/problem
- Need to shift reader's mental model before presenting solution
- Audience may have default assumptions that block solution comprehension
- Metaphor/analogy can illuminate the paradigm shift

## Structure

```
1. Acknowledge Crisis (from Post 1)
   ↓
2. Name the Deeper Problem (conceptual, not just implementation)
   ↓
3. Introduce Paradigm Shift (clear before/after contrast)
   ↓
4. Ground the New Metaphor (concrete examples, physics)
   ↓
5. Explain Why It Matters (consequences of old vs new thinking)
   ↓
6. Define Required Shift (explicit contrast questions)
   ↓
7. Setup Next Post (what architecture/solution enables this?)
```

## Key Elements

### 1. **Continuity Opening**
Reference Post 1's crisis without rehashing it. Use 1-2 sentences to establish "we know the problem, now here's the insight."

*Example:* "We've established the crisis: our productivity systems are drowning us. But the problem runs deeper than implementation—it's conceptual."

### 2. **Name the Default Assumption**
Identify the mental model readers currently hold that needs challenging.

*Example:* "The dominant metaphor in productivity has always been **containers**."

### 3. **Paradigm Shift Statement**
One clear sentence that states the conceptual reframe.

*Example:* "The paradigm shift isn't from bad organization to good organization. It's from organizing pools to orchestrating flow."

### 4. **Grounded Metaphor**
If using metaphor (water, physics, biology), ground it explicitly to prevent florid language.

*Example:* "A river flows, but it follows physics. Water moves through channels carved by geology..."

### 5. **Concrete Before/After**
Show the practical difference between old and new thinking with parallel structure.

*Example:*  
Pool thinking asks: "Where should I put this?"  
Flow thinking asks: "Where should this go automatically?"

### 6. **Setup Question**
End with the question that Post 3 will answer.

*Example:* "The obvious question: what does a flow-based productivity system actually look like?"

## Quality Checks

- [ ] Paradigm shift is conceptual, not just tactical
- [ ] Metaphors are grounded with concrete examples
- [ ] Before/after contrast is crystal clear
- [ ] Continuity with previous post maintained
- [ ] Setup for next post is explicit
- [ ] Tone consistent across series
- [ ] Word count appropriate for series rhythm

## Anti-Patterns

❌ **Rehashing Post 1** - Don't re-establish the crisis, acknowledge and move forward  
❌ **Florid metaphors** - Ground with physics/mechanics/concrete examples  
❌ **Semantic wordplay** - Paradigm must be substantive, not just relabeling  
❌ **Missing bridge** - Must explicitly connect to Post 1 and setup Post 3  
❌ **Tactical instead of conceptual** - Shift must be mental model, not just technique

## Success Metrics

**Depth:** Goes beyond surface to fundamental reframe  
**Originality:** New lens on familiar problem  
**Clarity:** Reader can articulate the paradigm shift  
**Continuity:** Natural progression in series arc  
**Setup:** Post 3 question is obvious and compelling
