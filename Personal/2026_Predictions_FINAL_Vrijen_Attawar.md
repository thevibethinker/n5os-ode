# 2026 Predictions - Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan

## Trend Predictions

### AI Skills Gap, Reskilling/Upskilling and Workforce Transformation

**RESPONSE:** 2026 won't solve the AI skills gap—it will widen it. We lack the pedagogical infrastructure to teach AI at scale. While AI enables hyper-personalized learning, there's a critical difference between delivering knowledge and building skills. LLMs can tutor you on concepts, but they lack the world models needed to teach complex human capabilities like professional judgment or empathy. Meanwhile, employers say they want to hire for potential and trainability, but when push comes to shove, they retreat to the same ATS-driven, credential-focused systems they've always used. The result? A bifurcated workforce: those with compute access and the foresight to invest early will pull ahead, while everyone else falls further behind unless they commit an extraordinary amount of time and energy to catch up. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

---

### AI-Powered Recruitment and Hiring Tools

**RESPONSE:** AI-powered recruiting tools won't meaningfully advance in 2026—they'll still be fundamentally broken. The core problem isn't the AI; it's that no one is willing to reject the inadequate data structure of the resume. Most companies are they're pursuing procedural innovations—like video interviewing or candidate sourcing at scale—without questioning whether meaningful signal is being acquired in the first place. The reason 46% of hires fail by the 18-month mark is that we don't truly know who we're hiring, and AI is making all that worse. Bias isn't even our biggest problem anymore—frankly. Meanwhile, expect more legislation, more fake candidate scandals, and ATS vendors frantically backpedaling from screening responsibility because they know their systems are not set up to do that at scale with LLMs without burning cash. The hiring system is built on a convenient lie of meritocracy. In 2026, the Piper's bill will start coming due. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

---

### DEI, AI Ethics & Regulation

**RESPONSE:** No, models won't correct hiring inequities in 2026. For one, the baseline work that model makers do gets filtered through many layers before it impacts the candidate. Bias stems from knowing too little about someone and over-indexing on what you do know (race, gender, credentials). AI will proliferate in hiring, mostly for document validation and communication management. But candidates aren't crying out for better bulk emails—they want to be understood as whole people, not resume bullets. Unless we build corresponding systems for truly learning about people, we're just automating the same broken processes faster. Expect legislation within 1-2 years, possibly sooner. The trigger won't be "AI is taking our jobs"—it will be "AI is making it exponentially harder to GET a job." When the candidate experience degrades to the point of public outcry, regulators will respond. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

---

### Job Market

**RESPONSE:** The 2026 job market will be worse for everyone—candidates and employers alike. Signal loss is complete: performative startups meet desperate candidates, and mutual trust collapses. Inbound (post & pray) is already obsolete, though people haven't fully internalized it yet. Within a year, that reckoning will hit hard. Outbound and networking will become tactical necessities in the short term. But here's the uncomfortable truth: we need to ask ourselves if we really want hiring to become just another marketing exercise. The ability to advocate for yourself is NOT equally distributed—it aligns with protected classes, socioeconomic factors, and plain bad luck (bad parents, poor schools, trauma). There are countless reasons someone might be terrible at self-promotion that have nothing to do with their ability to do the job. If we're serious about reducing bias in hiring, fixating on networking as THE solution is fighting the wrong battle. The correct answer will be a combination of technology that captures who people actually are and human connection that validates it. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

---

### Higher & Continued Education

**RESPONSE:** Universities will do what they've always done: try out different AI-powered career planning tools and inevitably fail to get good ones. This isn't because career services professionals don't care—they do. It's because the bar for innovation in this sector is on the floor. The fundamental problem? Every tool focuses on deliverables—resumes, cover letters—completely missing the forest for the trees. Great resumes are a byproduct of self-understanding, and self-advocacy emerges from building foundational subskills. The resume is a container of information, but it's a fundamentally shit container—a bad data structure from first principles. This flawed thinking leads universities to conclude "we need something that helps with the resume," creating a market for products like VMock. Whether these companies genuinely don't understand the real problem or exist in blissful ignorance with zero user research is unclear. What IS clear: students hate these products, yet universities are paying $20,000+ per year for tools, because admitting that there are no good solutions or easy answers is worse than burning money to pretend like you do. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

---

### AI & The Multi-Career Era

**RESPONSE:** AI CAN make mid-career switching more realistic—but only conditionally. IF we build systems that actually learn about people as whole humans (not just resumes), THEN AI can process information at scale and do the "thought work" of translating someone's background into a new context. It could show employers how a non-traditional candidate brings unique value, potentially at lower cost than conventional industry hires. But I reject any one-size-fits-all framing. More accurate: we'll see concurrent effects hitting different population segments differently—some thriving, others struggling. Some of them will be held to higher standards, others will be able to meet lower standards than ever. What's certain? AI will put enormous pressure on people who just want to live their lives. Now everyone needs to be an AI enthusiast and tinkerer. This is yet another obligation the tech world is imposing on the rest of society. A few companies besides Careerspan (or Jack & Jill and Dex in the UK) seem to understand the underlying problem, but my prediction is conditional: career mobility through AI only works if we fix the foundational issue of how we learn about people. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

### 2026: The Year of ?? for Job Seekers

**RESPONSE: 2026: The Year Inbound Died.** This will be the year society wakes up to what AI-flooded pipelines have done to job seekers' prospects. Right now, people see inbound (post and pray) as the default and networking as something you do to "get ahead." In 2026, job seekers will realize networking isn't about getting ahead anymore—it's about staying afloat. The death of inbound as a viable strategy will spark societal-level discourse and likely trigger legislation. Paradoxically, it will also be a bumper year for recruiters—incomes will skyrocket as companies desperate for signal turn to human intermediaries. We'll see a flood of matching companies claiming proprietary methods for connecting employers to candidates. But here's the underlying problem nobody wants to address: we all say we want to help people with data, but we only want to sneak it or surreptitiously steal it out of their back pockets. Nobody wants to make the honest exchange: "We'll treat you fairly, you own your data, and when we can, we'll give you a slice of the upside." Until we're willing to have that conversation, this is all just rearranging deck chairs. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

---

### 2026: The Year of ?? for Employers

**RESPONSE: 2026: The Year of the Hiring Payout.** Employers will be bled dry by hiring costs in 2026. Flooded with AI-generated applications and unable to find signal through traditional channels, they'll abandon inbound hiring and retreat to what they know: referral networks. This retreat will accelerate the ossification of existing class structures in tech. Only employers with money can afford top recruiters. Only those with influence have robust referral networks to tap. Those with advantages succeed harder, compounding their momentum while everyone else falls further behind. It's a tale as old as time. The consequence? Tech becomes even more insular, even more exclusive. We desperately need to fix the job market—not just for efficiency, but to prevent the complete entrenchment of power and privilege in our industry. What good is all the talent in the world if you can't find it in a timely fashion? (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)

### Other

**RESPONSE: The Data Structure Crisis.** If there's one thread connecting all my predictions, it's this: we're trying to solve 2026 problems with 1970s data structures. The resume was designed for a world of stable careers, predictable role progressions, and human screeners with time to read. That world is gone. Every prediction I've made—from AI tools that still suck to universities buying terrible software to the death of inbound—traces back to our collective refusal to acknowledge that the resume is fundamentally inadequate for modern hiring. It's bad data: low quality, questionable provenance, easily gamed, and optimized for a screening process that no longer exists. What resilient advantage does a point-in-time assessment provide in a world where skills evolve constantly and AI makes credentials easy to fabricate? The companies that will win in 2026 and beyond are those willing to ask: What if we started from scratch? What data structure would we design if we actually wanted to understand people as whole humans—their growth trajectory, their learning capacity, their values, their potential—not just their past titles? Until we're willing to reject the resume and build something better, we'll keep automating broken processes and wondering why everything still sucks. The market is screaming for a better way. The question is: will enough people have the courage to choose that path. (Vrijen Attawar, Founder and CEO of Careerspan)
