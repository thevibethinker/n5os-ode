### The Synthesis

#### The Insight

This reflection connects multiple domains into a coherent theory:

**Ethics + Psychology:** Traditional moral philosophy asks "what should people do?" This framework asks "what CAN people do?" — and recognizes that capability constraints shape moral behavior. The under-talented person isn't necessarily evil; they're cornered.

**Game Theory + Career:** In competitive environments, antisocial tactics are a form of "buff" — they increase win probability. For the under-talented, they're necessary to compete. For the talented, they're an optional advantage. This explains why high-achievers can be both prosocial AND antisocial — talent doesn't determine morality, it determines the necessity of shortcuts.

**Psychology + Career Development:** The emotional state of "encountering the world unafraid" versus compensatory desperation. Talent isn't just skill — it's a psychological posture that shapes behavioral choices.

#### Why This Matters

This framework inverts the typical moral conversation. Instead of judging antisocial behavior, it asks: "What capability gap drove this?" This has practical implications:

1. **Hiring:** Look for capability-ambition alignment, not just credentials
2. **Management:** Reduce desperation by building genuine capability
3. **Self-development:** Honest assessment of talent vs. ambition gap
4. **Society:** Antisocial behavior as symptom of structural capability gaps
