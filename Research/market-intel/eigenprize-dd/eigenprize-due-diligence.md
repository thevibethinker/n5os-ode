## Assessment

### Legitimacy Indicators ✅
1. **Predecessor track record** — Duct Tape Prize successfully ran and distributed $25K+ to builders with announced winners
2. **Identifiable, credentialed team** — Thiel Fellow, Harvard, MIT, Wharton, Rhodes Scholar
3. **VC-backed parent company** — Orbit has $10.35M in institutional funding, not fly-by-night
4. **No equity, no strings** — Genuinely philanthropic structure
5. **Contact point** — Named contact (steven@orbit.engineering) for questions
6. **Consistent narrative** — "When we were younger, we scavenged for parts" matches the team's actual builder backgrounds

### Things to Note ⚠️
1. **Young team** — All founders appear to be in their early-to-mid 20s. Not inherently a risk, but the prize is run out of a startup, not an established foundation.
2. **Orbit is funding source** — The prize is funded by a VC-backed startup that hasn't shipped a consumer product yet. If Orbit hits financial trouble, future prize rounds could be affected.
3. **Mene Capital opacity** — No public fund structure or AUM data. Appears personal capital.
4. **Scale-up risk** — Going from $25K (Duct Tape) to $250K (Eigenprize) is a 10x jump. Execution at this scale is untested.
5. **Aviato shows Mene's current employer as Google (stale data)** — His actual current role is at Anon. Aviato enrichment can lag on role changes.

### Bottom Line
The Eigenprize appears to be a legitimate, well-intentioned initiative run by a tight-knit group of young technical founders at Orbit, supplemented by Mene Mazarakis's capital and connections. The team's credentials are strong (Thiel, Harvard, MIT, Wharton, Rhodes). The no-strings-attached model mirrors the Duct Tape Prize which did pay out. Low risk of scam; moderate risk of operational growing pains at the new scale.
