# Builder Pathway

**Trigger:** Caller names a specific task: "I want to automate X," "Can Zo do Y?" or describes a workflow they already do manually.

**These are the best calls.** Caller has intent — your job is to design a solution and make it feel achievable.

## The Layering Pattern (Proven in 5 of 8 Substantive Calls)

Always offer two levels. Simple first, then advanced. Never lead with the complex version.

### Example: Real Estate Lead Qualification (Call 4)
- **Simple:** "You could paste your leads into a spreadsheet and have Zo research each one — company, role, LinkedIn. Done by morning."
- **Advanced:** "The next level: Zo monitors your inbox for new leads, auto-enriches them, scores them, and drafts personalized follow-up emails for the hot ones."

### Example: Meeting → Content (Call 6)
- **Simple:** "Connect your Fathom account, and Zo pulls the transcript after each meeting and writes you a summary with action items."
- **Advanced:** "Now add a scheduled task that takes those summaries, identifies quotable moments, and drafts a LinkedIn post every day."

### Example: Invoice Processing (Call 8)
- **Simple:** "Forward invoices to your Zo email, and it extracts the amounts and categories into a spreadsheet."
- **Advanced:** "Add a weekly scheduled task that reconciles those against your QuickBooks data and flags discrepancies."

## The Anchor: Make It Feel Real

After describing the solution, anchor it to a specific moment in their life:

**Proven language pattern:** "Imagine [day of week], [time of day], [before they do anything] — this is already done."

Examples:
- "Imagine Monday morning, before you even open your laptop, the lead list is already prioritized in your inbox."
- "Imagine Friday afternoon, your weekly LinkedIn post is drafted and waiting for you to hit publish."

## The "One-Time Setup" Frame (For Non-Technical Callers)

From Call 8: "She'd need someone with some technical chops for the initial setup — it's a one-time, 15-minute thing. After that, it just runs."

This separates the hard part (setup) from the easy part (ongoing use). Works especially well when the caller is evaluating Zo for someone else.

## Danger Zone

- Don't over-explain implementation details. They asked what it does, not how it's built.
- Don't hedge with "it depends." Give a concrete answer, then qualify.
- Don't skip the simple version. Jumping to the advanced solution overwhelms.
