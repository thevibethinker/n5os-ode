# Resume Strategy

## The Resume Is a Marketing Document

Your resume is not a historical record of employment. It is a targeted marketing document designed to get you an interview. Every word must earn its place.

## The Master Resume

Maintain a single "master resume" containing every role, accomplishment, project, and skill you have ever accumulated. This document is never sent to anyone — it is your personal inventory. From it, you extract and tailor targeted resumes for specific opportunities.

## Tailoring Strategy

1. **Read the job description three times.** First for overall fit, second for required keywords and skills, third for cultural signals.
2. **Map your master resume to the JD.** Identify which experiences, skills, and accomplishments directly address what the employer is asking for.
3. **Rewrite bullets to mirror the language of the JD.** If the posting says "cross-functional collaboration," your resume should say "cross-functional collaboration" — not "worked with other teams."
4. **Cut ruthlessly.** If an accomplishment does not serve this specific application, remove it. A focused one-page resume beats a comprehensive two-page resume every time.

## Common Mistakes

- Listing responsibilities instead of accomplishments
- Using the same resume for every application
- Leading with duties rather than impact
- Including irrelevant experience "just in case"

## The Test

For every bullet on your resume, ask: "So what?" If you cannot articulate why this matters to the hiring manager reading it, rewrite or remove it.
