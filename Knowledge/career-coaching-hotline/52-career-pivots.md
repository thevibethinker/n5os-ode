# Career Pivots

## The Bridge Story

A career pivot succeeds or fails based on your ability to tell a bridge story — a narrative that connects where you have been to where you are going in a way that feels logical and intentional (even if the journey was messy).

The bridge story answers the question every hiring manager will ask: "Why should I hire someone from [your old field] for this [new field] role?"

## Skills Translation

Most skills are more transferable than people realize. The challenge is translating them into the language of the target industry:

- "Managed a restaurant" → "Directed a high-volume operations team in a fast-paced, customer-facing environment with thin margins and zero tolerance for quality failures"
- "Taught high school" → "Designed and delivered curriculum for diverse audiences, assessed performance against benchmarks, and managed stakeholder relationships with parents, administrators, and support staff"

The underlying competencies — leadership, communication, operations, stakeholder management — transfer. The framing must change.

## The Transferable Competencies Framework

1. **Identify your core competencies** — not job-specific skills, but underlying capabilities (analysis, persuasion, system design, people development).
2. **Map those competencies to the target role** — read 10-15 job descriptions in your target field and identify which of your competencies appear repeatedly.
3. **Build evidence bridges** — for each transferable competency, prepare a specific story from your current/past experience that demonstrates it.

## Closing the Gap

Pivots rarely require starting from zero. Identify the 2-3 specific skills or credentials you lack and fill them strategically: certifications, side projects, freelance work, volunteer roles. The goal is to remove the hiring manager's primary objection, not to earn another degree.

## The Pivot Paradox

The further the pivot, the stronger your bridge story must be. But a well-told pivot story is actually more memorable than a conventional career trajectory. Hiring managers remember the teacher-turned-product-manager who brought a unique perspective. Use your difference as an asset.
