## Two Paths to Internships

There are **institutional internships** at established companies with formal programs—approach these like a professional applicant. Then there are **self-created internships** at smaller companies without formal programs—approach these like an entrepreneur.

Not all internships are equal. A good internship gives you real responsibilities, measurable impact, and relationships that outlast the experience. A bad one has you fetching coffee and leaving with nothing to show for it.

## The "Goldilocks" Companies

For self-created internships, target companies with 5-50 employees. Big enough to have real work, small enough to actually need your help. Research their problems, engage with founders on LinkedIn, then pitch yourself with a specific case for the value you bring.

## Application Strategy That Works

Stop blasting the same generic resume everywhere. Companies have more options than you—you won't be taken seriously unless you show you're serious about *this specific role*.

**For every application:**
- Tailor your resume to match 70%+ of the job responsibilities
- Write a cover letter that couldn't apply to any other company
- Research the company deeply—Glassdoor is your friend

You are the world authority on yourself—act like it!

## During the Internship

Your goal is simple: would they want to hire you back?

- Complete everything asked of you to standard, then exceed it
- Show genuine desire to learn and marked improvement over time
- Make your teammates' lives easier, not harder
- Keep a running list of your contributions and impact

If you're doing a good job, you'll be trusted with more complex work. That's how conversions happen.

## After the Internship

Update your master resume immediately while details are fresh. Add key relationships on LinkedIn. Send personalized thank-you notes to everyone who helped you succeed. Stay in touch with managers—these relationships compound over time.

## What You Can Do Right Now

1. **Build your "authority on yourself" document**: Write detailed notes on what you want, what you've accomplished, and what skills you've developed
2. **Create a master resume**: Start comprehensive, then subtract irrelevant content for each application
3. **Identify 5-10 "Goldilocks" companies**: Research their problems and who posts frequently on LinkedIn
4. **Practice your 1-2 minute "tell me about yourself"**: Know it cold, without a script
