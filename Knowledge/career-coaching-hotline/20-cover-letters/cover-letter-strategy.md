## The Truth About Cover Letters

Here's the paradox: about 38% of hiring managers say cover letters matter, but many never get read. So when should you actually write one?

**Write a cover letter when:**
- You have a career transition or employment gap to explain
- You have a genuine connection to the company's mission
- You have a referral or insider knowledge worth mentioning
- You need to provide context (relocation, availability, visa status)

**Skip it when:**
- The application says "no cover letters"
- It's a quick-apply system
- You have nothing to add beyond your resume
- You'd just be writing generic filler

## The Structure That Works

Keep it under half a page — roughly 300 words. Address it to the hiring manager if you can find their name.

1. **Opening (25%)** — Hook them with something specific about THEM. Why this company? Why this role?
2. **Body (50%)** — One or two concrete examples that prove you can do what they need. Quantify results when possible.
3. **Closing (25%)** — Summarize your value and suggest next steps.

## Two Tests Every Cover Letter Must Pass

**The Name-Blind Test:** Remove the company name. Can someone still tell which company this is for? If your letter could work for any company, it's too generic.

**The Resume Repetition Test:** Does your cover letter add NEW information? If you're just restating your resume in paragraph form, delete it.

## Why Generic Is Worse Than None

A generic cover letter signals laziness. It tells the hiring manager you couldn't be bothered to learn anything about them. That's worse than no letter at all. If you can't write something specific, don't write anything.

Remember: The cover letter is about THEM, not you. What problem do they have? How do you solve it?

## What You Can Do Right Now

1. **Apply the name-blind test** to any cover letter you've drafted. If it's generic, either make it specific or delete it entirely.
2. **Identify 2-3 companies** where you have a genuine story to tell — a connection to their mission, a relevant project, a referral — and write cover letters only for those.
3. **Cut your current draft in half.** If it's over 300 words, you're rambling.
