## Trigger Phrases
- "I don't know what I want to do"
- "How do I figure out my strengths"
- "I can't describe what I'm good at"
- "I feel lost in my career"
- "How do I know what jobs to apply for"
- "I don't have time for introspection"

## Why Self-Reflection Comes First

You are an amorphous cloud of skills, experiences, and competencies. That's not an insult — it's actually liberating. Your professional identity isn't fixed. But you can't shape that cloud into something compelling until you actually understand what's in it.

Self-reflection is the foundation. Before resumes, before networking, before interviews — you need to know yourself. Most people skip this because it's uncomfortable and feels unproductive. They'd rather blast out applications than sit with hard questions.

That's backwards.

## Why People Resist It

Let's be honest:
- It's uncomfortable to examine yourself critically
- It feels time-consuming when you're anxious to "do something"
- Most people either undervalue their experiences or can't connect the dots
- Being objective about yourself is genuinely hard

## The Anecdote-Based Approach

Don't try to summarize your whole career at once. Pick specific stories — moments where you did something meaningful. For each anecdote, ask:

1. What was the situation?
2. What did you actually do?
3. What was the result?
4. What skills did you use?
5. What did you learn?
6. Would you do it again?

Then zoom out to meta-level questions: What patterns emerge? What skills keep showing up? What's transferable to new contexts?

## How This Improves Your Job Search

When you've done the reflection work, everything else gets easier:
- Your resume tells a coherent story
- You interview with genuine confidence
- You network authentically instead of desperately
- You negotiate from a position of self-knowledge
- You target roles that actually fit

Confidence isn't faked — it comes from truly understanding your value.

## V's Take

Even I do weekly therapy. Self-reflection isn't a one-time activity — it's maintenance. You wouldn't wait until your car breaks down to check the oil. Don't wait for a layoff to examine your career.

## What You Can Do Right Now

1. **Pick three career moments** that felt significant — good or bad. Write a paragraph about each using the six questions above.

2. **Schedule 15 minutes weekly** for career reflection. Put it on your calendar. Treat it like a meeting you can't cancel.

3. **Ask someone who knows your work** to describe your top three strengths. Compare their answer to what you'd say about yourself.
