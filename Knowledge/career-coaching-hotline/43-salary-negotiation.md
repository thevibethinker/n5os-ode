# Salary Negotiation

## The Foundation: Evidence Over Emotion

Salary negotiation is not about what you need or what you feel you deserve. It is about presenting evidence that your market value justifies a specific compensation range. Emotion loses. Data wins.

## Research Your Market Value

- Use Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Payscale, and LinkedIn Salary Insights to establish a range.
- Talk to recruiters — they know current market rates better than any website.
- Factor in geography, company stage, industry, and specialization.
- Know the difference between the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentile for your role.

## Total Compensation

Salary is one component. Negotiate the full package:

- **Base salary** — Your fixed recurring income
- **Bonus** — Target percentage and actual payout history (ask: "What was the average bonus payout last year?")
- **Equity** — RSUs, options, vesting schedule, refresh grants
- **Benefits** — Health insurance, 401k match, PTO, parental leave
- **Perks** — Remote work, signing bonus, relocation, professional development budget

## Timing

- **Never give your number first.** Ask for the budgeted range. Many jurisdictions now require employers to disclose salary ranges.
- **Negotiate after the offer, not before.** Your leverage is highest when they have decided they want you.
- **Do not negotiate against yourself.** State your ask, then stop talking. Silence is a negotiation tool.

## The Framework

1. Express enthusiasm for the role (genuine, not performative)
2. Present your research: "Based on market data for this role in this market, the range is X-Y"
3. Anchor high within the reasonable range
4. Be specific: "$135,000" is more credible than "somewhere around $130-140K"
5. If they cannot meet base, negotiate other components

## The Unspoken Rule

Companies expect you to negotiate. The initial offer is rarely the best offer. Not negotiating leaves money on the table and can signal to the employer that you undervalue yourself.
