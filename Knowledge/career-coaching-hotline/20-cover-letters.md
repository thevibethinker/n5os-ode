# Cover Letters

## The Core Distinction

Your resume is about YOU — your history, your accomplishments, your skills. Your cover letter is about THEM — why this company, why this role, why now. The cover letter bridges the gap between what you have done and what they need.

## When to Write One

- When the application explicitly requests it
- When you have a compelling connection to the company or role
- When your resume alone does not tell the full story (career pivots, gaps, relocations)
- When applying to smaller companies where it will actually be read

Skip it when: the application does not ask for one and you have nothing distinctive to say.

## The Three-Part Structure

### 1. Why Them (Opening)
Demonstrate that you have researched the company. Reference specific initiatives, values, products, or challenges. Show genuine alignment — not flattery.

### 2. How You (Body)
Connect your specific experience to their specific needs. Choose 2-3 accomplishments that directly address the role's core requirements. This is not a resume summary — it is a targeted argument for why your background solves their problem.

### 3. Reiterate and Close
Restate your enthusiasm and fit. Include a clear call to action. Keep it confident but not presumptuous.

## Rules

- Never exceed one page. Three to four paragraphs maximum.
- Address a specific person whenever possible. "Dear Hiring Manager" is acceptable but not ideal.
- Do not repeat your resume verbatim. The cover letter adds context and narrative that bullets cannot.
- Every cover letter must be tailored. A generic cover letter is worse than no cover letter.
