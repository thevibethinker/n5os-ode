# The Art of the Brag

## Self-Advocacy Is Not Boasting

There is a pervasive cultural discomfort around talking about your own accomplishments. Many people — especially women, first-generation professionals, and people from collectivist cultures — have been socialized to downplay their contributions. This is a career liability.

## The Distinction

- **Boasting:** "I'm the best project manager in the company."
- **Self-advocacy:** "I led a cross-functional team of 12 to deliver a $3M platform migration two weeks ahead of schedule, which saved the company $400K in licensing costs."

The difference is specificity and evidence. Boasting makes claims. Self-advocacy presents facts.

## Reframing the Discomfort

If talking about your accomplishments feels uncomfortable, reframe it:

- You are not bragging — you are providing evidence.
- You are not self-promoting — you are making it easy for others to advocate for you.
- You are not showing off — you are ensuring your contributions are visible in a system that does not automatically surface them.

## The Visibility Problem

In most organizations, doing great work is necessary but not sufficient. The people who advance are the people whose work is visible to decision-makers. If you do not articulate your impact, someone else will get credit — or no one will, and the work simply disappears.

## Practical Techniques

1. **Use "we" strategically.** Acknowledge the team while being clear about your role: "I led the initiative, and my team of six executed brilliantly."
2. **Let the numbers speak.** Quantified results feel less like bragging and more like reporting.
3. **Practice your brag stories.** Write them down. Say them out loud. The awkwardness fades with repetition.
4. **Keep a running accomplishment log.** Every Friday, write down what you achieved that week. This makes self-advocacy a habit, not an event.
