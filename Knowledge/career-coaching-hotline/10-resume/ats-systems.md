## What ATS Actually Does

An Applicant Tracking System is software that scans your resume before any human sees it. It parses text, extracts keywords, and scores how well you match the job description. Here's the brutal truth: roughly 75% of resumes get filtered out before a recruiter ever looks at them.

## What Gets You Through

**Exact keywords.** If the job says "project management," your resume needs to say "project management"—not "managed projects." Basic systems do literal matching.

**Clean formatting.** Simple bullet points, standard section headers like "Work Experience" and "Education," and a .docx or text-based PDF file.

**Quantifiable achievements.** Numbers pop. "Increased sales by 30%" beats "responsible for sales growth" every time.

## What Gets You Rejected

**Fancy templates.** Those beautiful Canva resumes with graphics, columns, tables, and icons? The ATS sees gibberish. Your "creative" resume becomes word salad.

**Non-standard formatting.** Headers, footers, text boxes—ATS often ignores these entirely.

**Keyword stuffing.** Repeating the same term fifteen times won't help. Two or three strategic placements is the sweet spot.

## Basic vs. Advanced Systems

Basic ATS does literal keyword matching—no synonyms, no context. Advanced systems use AI to understand that "team leadership" might equal "project management." Problem is, you never know which one you're facing. Play it safe: use the exact language from the job posting.

## The Real Goal

Your resume must work for robots AND humans. ATS gets you through the gate; a recruiter decides if you're worth calling. A keyword-stuffed mess might score well but look terrible to human eyes.

Don't game the system—align with it. Mirror the job description's language naturally throughout your experience.

## What You Can Do Right Now

1. **Pull up a job you want.** Highlight 5-7 key terms that appear multiple times. Make sure those exact phrases appear in your resume.

2. **Ditch the fancy template.** Switch to a clean, single-column format with standard fonts and simple bullet points.

3. **Check your file format.** Save as .docx or a text-based PDF—never an image-based PDF or creative format.

4. **Add numbers everywhere.** Every bullet point should answer "how much?" or "how many?" if possible.
