# Position: Talent-Optionality Thesis

## Claim

**Talent creates moral optionality.** The capability gap between ambition and talent drives antisocial behavior; the talented can afford to be good because they have a real shot at winning fairly.

## Expanded Statement

When your ambitions exceed your talent, you face a choice: accept failure, or close the gap through antisocial means (cheating, manipulation, credit-stealing, corner-cutting). The talented don't face this choice with the same intensity — they can pursue prosocial paths because those paths actually lead somewhere for them.

This isn't moral relativism. It's recognizing that:
1. **Antisocial behavior often stems from capability gaps, not inherent evil**
2. **The talented encounter the world unafraid** — they don't need manipulation to survive
3. **Capability constraints shape moral behavior** more than we acknowledge

## Implications

### For Careerspan
- The mission isn't just "better matching" — it's **reducing the ambition-capability gap** so prosocial paths become viable
- Talent development > credential verification as a moral stance
- Explains why resume fraud correlates with desperation, not character

### For Career Advice
- "Develop genuine talent" isn't just practical advice — it's moral infrastructure
- Authenticity requires capability; without it, authenticity becomes a luxury
- The under-talented person isn't evil; they're cornered

### For Society
- Meritocracy requires accessible talent development, not just equal opportunity
- Antisocial behavior in competitive domains is often structural, not individual
- "Good character" often correlates with "sufficient capability"

## Tensions

- **With radical egalitarianism:** This view implies talent differences matter and create different moral landscapes
- **With character-first ethics:** Suggests circumstances shape character more than we'd like to admit
- **With personal accountability:** Could be read as excusing bad behavior (it's not — it's explaining, not justifying)

## Falsifiability

Would update if:
- Strong evidence that antisocial behavior is equally distributed across capability levels
- Evidence that talent development doesn't reduce antisocial career behavior
- Counterexamples of highly talented people who persistently choose antisocial paths when prosocial paths are viable
