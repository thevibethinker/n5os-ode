# Out with the Old, in with the Niche

Out with the Old, in with the Niche

Out with the Old, in with the Niche

Created on 2020-04-17 12:20

Published on 2020-04-17 12:36

Change is the only constant on the internet, and this time, it’s coming for today’s social media behemoths. The once unquestionable dominance of companies like Facebook and Twitter has already begun to fade. For years now, they have been bleeding fringe users and struggling to acquire new ones. 

Millennials and Gen Zers are especially wary. They are far savvier online, more concerned by issues like privacy, and generally inclined to support companies that operate ethically. Instead, such users are increasingly turning to platforms whose communities are oriented around a particular interest, goal, or principle. I believe that this shift marks a new era in social networking—a growing rejection of the status quo and embracing of crowdculture. 

Currently, big players like Facebook are pursuing an expansive growth strategy focused on providing a broad array of services and features. Facebook, in particular, has made an effort to position itself as a one-stop-shop, not only for one’s social media needs but also for “IRL” purposes such as payment. This generalist nature and broad appeal, however, has its downsides: it does many things, but only a few them especially well. 

This overabundance of features, many of which do not operate effectively on mobile devices, has led to a convoluted platform that can charitably be described as a mess. By contrast, niche networks have taken a different route by offering specialized and well-defined value propositions. The evidence would suggest that this approach appeals to younger users, who are already used to splitting their engagement over several services. 

We need only look at the tremendous range of niche social networks to understand their appeal and explosive growth. There are established names like Pinterest and Tinder and newcomers like Tapped, a geosocial networking service for beer lovers, and Houzz, a social website about home decorating ideas. These communities are oriented around singular purposes—be it a profession or industry, hobby, or even shared experience—and designed to foster deeper connections. By contrast, on the big social sites, a person’s network is often so big that there’s no real sense of community. 

These networks also benefit from a clean slate as compared to Facebook and Twitter. These tech giants are regularly in the news for all the wrong reasons—from privacy violations to the treatment of their staff. Younger users care about whether the company they subscribe to is “problematic.” They are also more likely to seek out alternatives to mainstream brands with stronger community-oriented practices. For instance, despite weathering criticism over the years, Reddit has generally been responsive to the needs of its users—even if its response wasn’t to everyone’s satisfaction. 

Perhaps the ultimate proof is that these Goliaths of social networking are running scared is the fact that they’re very much hedging their bets. Facebook, for instance, made a conscious decision to limit integration between its primary brand and subsidiaries like Whatsapp and Instagram. As a result, they have avoided the dilution of their unique selling points and negative associations with their parent company. Indeed, these multi-billion- dollar brands are evidence of the tremendous growth potential of niche social networks. 

In many ways, these niche social networks reflect a change in what we want out of our online lives. Most of us will probably remain on mainstream platforms for the foreseeable future, but I believe our usage will increasingly reflect who we are and how we define ourselves. It’ll skew towards intimate networks that enable its users to share experiences and ideas in an authentic and personal way. Our various online presences will mirror who we are on the inside, not just how we want to present ourselves on the outside. You may not be able to do everything on niche social networks, but you’ll undoubtedly get what you came for.
