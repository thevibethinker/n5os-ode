# Making Sense of AI Productivity

Tope — you mentioned something that stuck with me. For the first time in a long time, you don't feel like you're in the top percentile of productivity sophistication. That's not a personal gap. It's a signal that the entire category is shifting underneath us, and most people haven't noticed yet.

Here's what I've learned building systems at the bleeding edge of this shift.

---

## The Old Model Is Broken

Every productivity tool you've used — Notion, Asana, Zapier, even Calendly — was designed for a world of information scarcity. Capture the thing. Organize the thing. Review the thing. Execute.

That model assumed humans could hold context and maintain state across their work. We can't anymore. We're so informationally overloaded that the cognitive labor of maintaining a productivity system now exceeds the labor it saves. That's why people adopt tools enthusiastically and abandon them within months.

The shift isn't "better tools." It's a fundamentally different architecture.

---

## What's Actually Changing: Flow Over Structure

The breakthrough is simple to state, harder to internalize: **information has to flow, not pool.**

Think of it like water infrastructure. When information sits — in an inbox, in a backlog, in someone's head — it goes stale. The value of a meeting note isn't the note itself. It's what that note triggers downstream: a follow-up, a connection to another idea, a decision that was overdue.

Pre-AI, we couldn't build flow systems. The technology didn't exist. Now it does. And the implications are massive:

1. **Organization shouldn't exist as a step.** If you have to manually organize something, the system has already failed. Organization should be native — it happens automatically through pointers, patterns, and intelligent routing.

2. **You capture anywhere, direct intelligently.** Your phone, email, voice, Slack — all valid inputs. The system figures out where each piece should go based on content, context, and your patterns.

3. **Review becomes approval, not processing.** Instead of scanning every task list yourself, the system generates enriched digests that surface what needs your attention. Your job shifts from "remember everything" to "approve what matters."

4. **Every interaction with a system has some probability of breaking it.** So the system must self-heal. Detect errors, trace causes, auto-correct. Without this, complexity destroys you at scale.

---

## The Power User as Leading Indicator

Here's the part that's directly relevant to what you're building.

Productivity is deeply personal. The optimizations that power users make today become the automations everyone uses in two years. Fashion works the same way — the runway is ahead of the culture.

When I hacked Howie to include a Calendly fallback link in its responses, that wasn't me being clever. That was a signal about what every scheduling user will eventually want: an agent that understands their preferences, adjusts dynamically, and handles the social protocol of booking — including the private instruction problem you described.

The question you raised — how do you instruct your scheduling agent without exposing your priorities to the other party? — is actually a specific instance of a much bigger design challenge: **agents amplify intent, but intent is often private.** That's game theory embedded in UX. And whoever solves it first will own the next decade of scheduling.

---

## Why "Expert" Doesn't Mean What It Used To

Your instinct that you're no longer in the top percentile is exactly right — and it applies to everyone.

Productivity expertise used to compound. You learned GTD, built habits, got better year over year. That compounding is broken. The landscape shifts faster than expertise can accumulate. The advantage now goes to rapid learners and system builders, not accumulated knowledge holders.

This has a direct product implication: **you can't sell productivity tools on features anymore.** The feature set changes too fast. You sell on architecture — specifically, on being the orchestration layer that connects to everything else, maintains context, and flows information intelligently regardless of what tools sit beneath it.

Calendly already owns the most powerful choke point in professional life: the moment when someone who doesn't know you needs to book with you. That moment isn't going away. But the value you can create around that moment is about to explode — if you build for flow rather than features.

---

## The Signal Degradation Problem

One last thread that ties this together, because it's the one I've spent years on.

AI is destroying our ability to evaluate things at scale. Resumes can be fabricated. Interview answers can be rehearsed with AI. The proxies we've used for decades — keywords, credentials, structured assessments — are losing signal fast.

This isn't only a hiring problem. It's a scheduling problem. If 15-20% of Calendly's meetings are interviews, the integrity of what happens in those meetings matters to your business. As signal degrades, whoever can provide authentic, pre-verified context about the people in those meetings becomes indispensable.

The counter to signal degradation isn't more automation. It's better data about humans — collected through empathetic, coached interactions rather than adversarial evaluations. That's what we built at Careerspan, and it's the piece I'd love to explore with you in person.

---

The common thread across all of this: **AI productivity isn't about doing more. It's about maintaining coherence as complexity scales.** The tools that win will be the ones that help humans stay oriented — not the ones that add more features to the pile.
