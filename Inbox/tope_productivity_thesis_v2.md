# The Shift Underneath Us

---

## Pools, Plumbing, Cenotes

Every productivity tool we've used was built on the same assumption: information needs a container. Notion is a container. Asana is a container. Salesforce is a container. The work is: capture, organize, retrieve.

That model is dead. Here's why.

The first era was **pools** — data scattered across disconnected places. Your contacts in one tool, your calendar in another, your notes in a third. All the public pools in your house, none of them connected.

The second era was **waterparks** — integrations, Zapier, APIs. Pools of different shapes and sizes all vaguely interconnected. But waterparks are complicated things to set up and maintain, both literally and figuratively.

The third era — the one we're entering — is **cenotes.** The underground water system *is* the data. The surface pools are access points into it. You don't own the water by building a better pool. You own it by controlling the entrances to the aquifer.

This reframes the strategic question entirely. It's not "which tool holds the data?" It's "which tool sits at the checkpoint where valuable data enters the system?"

Calendly already sits at one of the most powerful checkpoints in professional life: the moment a stranger commits time to you. That's not going away. But the value you can create around that checkpoint is about to expand dramatically — if you build for flow instead of storage.

## The CRM Nobody Uses

You nailed something in our conversation that maps directly onto this: CRMs are underutilized in the VSMB space because data entry is too hard.

That's the pool problem in action. A CRM is a container that demands manual filling. Most people abandon it within months because the cognitive labor of maintaining the system exceeds the labor it saves.

AI breaks this open in four ways, and you articulated them better than most product people I've talked to:

**One.** AI notetakers generate rich context automatically. The data entry barrier collapses — you capture relationship context without typing a word.

**Two.** AI makes sense of unstructured data. A meeting transcript becomes structured relationship intelligence without anyone building a schema first.

**Three.** Natural language replaces click-click-click. You talk to your CRM. It talks back. The interaction model shifts from database management to conversation.

**Four.** VSMB is underserved because the larger markets are saturated. Everyone's fighting over enterprise. The small business owner with 200 contacts and no admin staff? Nobody's building for them yet.

Put these together and you get something that isn't a CRM at all — not in the traditional sense. It's a relationship layer that builds itself. The Individual Data Manager. The thing that knows who you are across all contexts, not just within one tool's silo.

## The Onboarding Problem Nobody Solves

Here's where my experience building Careerspan maps onto what you're seeing at Calendly.

You described the onboarding challenge: Calendly has tried recommending event types during setup. The experiments haven't performed well. The real issue — and this is the part you've been saying internally — is that users haven't done the thinking. They haven't developed heuristics for managing their own availability.

I've spent a decade coaching people through a version of this same problem. Not with calendars, but with careers. The pattern is identical: people don't have a framework for the thing you're asking them to configure. So they stare at the blank setup screen and either abandon it or configure it badly.

The traditional product response is "better onboarding UX." That's necessary but insufficient. The real unlock is a kind of **cognitive installation** — helping people develop the mental model, not just the tool configuration.

Pre-AI, installation was expensive. It required human coaching, workshops, content. It didn't scale.

In the AI age, installation scales. An agent can teach. It can ask the right questions at the right time. It can observe how someone uses the tool and surface the heuristic they're missing. Agent-to-agent teaching means one agent can install a mental model into another — so the user's assistant arrives pre-educated about how to manage their availability.

This is the part of the AI productivity shift that almost nobody is building for. Everyone's building tools that assume the user already knows what they want. The massive unlock is building tools that help people figure out what they want.

## The Private Instruction Problem

We both landed on this independently, which tells me it's real: agents amplify intent, but intent is often private.

When I tell my scheduling agent "I want to take this meeting but I'm not excited about it — give me a short window on a Friday afternoon," I need that instruction to stay between me and my agent. The counterparty should see a clean, professional booking experience, not my internal prioritization logic.\
\
This is game theory embedded in UX. Every scheduling interaction has asymmetric information. Both parties have preferences they don't want to reveal. Today, that's handled by social protocol — the human dance of "I'm flexible, what works for you?" Agent-to-agent scheduling has to preserve that dance while executing on private instructions.

Whoever solves the private instruction problem first will own the next generation of scheduling. It's not a feature. It's an architecture decision that ripples through every interaction.

## Knock-On Tasks: The Hidden Data

One more thread. This one's newer in my thinking, but I believe it's important.

Every meeting generates knock-on tasks. A call with a potential client leads to a follow-up email, a proposal draft, a calendar hold for the next meeting, a note to a colleague. The meeting itself is a node. The knock-on tasks are the edges.

Over time, these patterns reveal something powerful: your working style. How big are your typical task chunks? What sequence do you follow after different types of meetings? Where do you procrastinate? Where do you execute immediately?

This is learnable data about a person. And whoever captures it can build dramatically better orchestration — not just scheduling, but the entire workflow around scheduling.

Calendly sees the node. The opportunity is to see the graph.

## The Through Line

The common thread: **AI productivity isn't about doing more. It's about maintaining coherence as complexity scales.**

The tools that win won't be the ones with the most features. They'll be the ones that sit at the right checkpoints, capture the right data, and help people stay oriented.

Calendly owns the scheduling checkpoint. The question is what you build around it.
