# Detailed Recap

1. **Context and participants**  
   A ~28‑minute strategy call between Zo co‑founders Tiffany Huang and Ben Guo and power user/VIP customer Vrijen Attawar. The group reconnects socially (running plans, travel, music) and then spends most of the time shaping a grassroots, club‑based growth program for Zo that deliberately leans into MLM‑style dynamics while trying to keep the incentives healthy.

2. **Opening: personal check‑in and 10k plan**  
   Vrijen shares that he has committed to a destination 10k race in Puerto Rico as a way to stay motivated: set an “exciting, exotic” race three months out, then train toward it. Tiffany is enthusiastic and offers housing if her own plans change (“you can just stay at my place for free”). They trade details about the race (Ruta del Encanto near Toa Baja), timing in late February, and the idea of running it together with friends.

3. **Framing the growth problem for Zo**  
   Tiffany describes Zo’s current GTM problem as needing to **open up the funnel and create more awareness** among the right people. There is already a small group of "very, very passionate users" like Vrijen, but growth is constrained by how much the core team can personally support and coordinate. The call is framed as a working session to pressure‑test an “MLM‑ish” club concept that could scale that enthusiasm without over‑extending the founders.

4. **Initial club concept: hand‑picked leaders with budgets**  
   Tiffany outlines the core idea: Zo will **hand‑pick extremely passionate users** as club leaders. Each leader gets to name a club (with some Zo‑flavored constraint), design a crest, and receives: a custom link with a discount and AI credits, some merch, a badge on X/Discord, and a small cash budget (e.g., $500 upfront or $100–150/month). In return, club leaders recruit at least five members, keep a dedicated chat space alive (WhatsApp/Telegram/Slack depending on culture), and run lightweight weekly/bi‑weekly rituals.

5. **Rituals, surveys, and feedback loops**  
   The program includes rhythm and accountability: leaders check in with members (e.g., Mondays) and members fill out a quick end‑of‑week survey (e.g., Fridays) about what they’re building, how Zo is helping, and what support they need. Leaders then distill that back to the Zo team as **content, bugs, and qualitative feedback**, effectively turning each club into a small, semi‑autonomous product council and marketing cell.

6. **Distribution and lore: status, badges, and “tapped on the shoulder”**  
   Tiffany emphasises that the *lore* matters as much as the mechanics: being a club leader should feel like a **status‑granting role**, not an entitlement. Borrowing from crypto communities, you don’t apply; you get “tapped on the shoulder”. This framing is meant to keep the scheme from feeling like a grindy referral program and instead like being entrusted with a small piece of Zo’s community.

7. **Incentives and potential multi‑level dynamics**  
   Vrijen pushes on what success looks like: if the metric is that each five‑person club eventually spawns **leaders who themselves start clubs**, then the design should reward that propagation. Tiffany brainstorms a sliding budget where a leader’s stipend goes up (e.g., +$10/month) for every new member recruited. They discuss that the real leverage is giving each cluster **compute, coordination, and shared motivation**—conditions that naturally produce small businesses, side hustles, or serious projects.

8. **Use‑case texture and member profiles**  
   The group notes that Zo’s best users are already builders who want to recruit others into their own products or projects. Clubs could therefore be organised around: sports/games, “side‑income labs”, personal CRM and “clout hacking”, or high‑craft AI writing. The goal is to choose themes that get people **excited to work together and share notes**, not just passively consume AI tips.

9. **“Clout hacking” and personal‑brand‑oriented clubs**  
   One concrete club archetype Vrijen proposes is a **Clout Hacking Club**: members use Zo to systematise networking, personal CRM, and reputation building—especially relevant for job‑seekers and founders. The club would focus on how to leverage networks and followers to access opportunities beyond one’s formal credentials, using Zo as the operating environment for those workflows.

10. **AI‑writing craft and controversial-but-useful themes**  
    Another theme is a **high‑quality AI writing guild**: people who want to push AI‑assisted writing to the point where it is indistinguishable from (or better than) human‑only prose. The group acknowledges this could be controversial but also sees it as a natural attractor for serious knowledge workers who care about craft. Tiffany points out that themes can range from serious (career, revenue) to playful (ADHD or bourbon‑lover communities) as long as they drive genuine engagement.

11. **Product‑level reflections: why Zo feels different**  
    In a meta reflection, Vrijen explains that Zo has changed how he approaches problems: instead of thinking “which app should I buy?”, his default is now “can I just build this myself on Zo by stringing a few things together?”. He links this to a broader cultural fatigue with consumerist app choice. A key emotional driver for him is **having everything in one environment he controls**, which he frames as a big part of why he is unusually attached to Zo relative to other AI tools.

12. **Zo VC and investing in builders with compute**  
    Building on the club idea, Vrijen floats a **“Zo VC”** concept: give high‑agency college students or builders significant compute on Zo, have them build prototypes, then showcase the best work. Tiffany and Ben like the idea of “investing AI credits in people” and see a path where demo days, product‑hunt‑style showcases, and a cross‑club hall of fame tie the ecosystem together. Tiffany also proposes a recurring voting ritual where the broader community upvotes projects and Zo can reward winners with more credits or attention.

13. **Avoiding disintermediation by external educators/KOLs**  
    Tiffany argues that Zo should **own the full stack** for people who want to learn AI: community, education, and the platform itself. Right now, many aspiring users are learning through scattered YouTube channels and KOL‑driven courses. A strong club system could keep that energy inside the Zo ecosystem instead of ceding it to external teachers, while still being fun and lightly “Ponzi‑ish” in a tongue‑in‑cheek way.

14. **Monetization timing and incentive hygiene**  
    The group agrees that introducing explicit monetization too early could distort behavior. Tiffany frames v1 as “meet people, make friends, learn AI together”; v2 is when clubs may become **micro venture studios** once there is proof that members want to build and ship. Vrijen emphasises that proving organic desire to build and ship is a prerequisite; otherwise, cash incentives risk attracting the wrong kind of activity.

15. **Network leverage and reciprocal offers**  
    In the final segment, Ben offers to leverage his founder networks (SBC, Stripe‑adjacent communities, etc.) to promote V’s work if V provides a concise blurb and jobs/roles to share. V is keen and suggests starting by getting Ben’s jobs posted, then looping in Ilya (Zo’s sales/marketing lead) so there are concrete demos to route. They also note Logan is planning to plug V to an ILab‑type institution and that a supporting doc and blurb will help that land.

16. **Personal plans, social glue, and closing**  
    The call closes with logistics: planning to meet in person on the 6th, exploring going to a music show together on the 5th, joking about drugs, and Tiffany being amused that all of this is captured by the note‑taker. The tone stays warm, irreverent, and deeply trusting throughout, with everyone aligned that the club program could become a meaningful growth and product‑discovery engine if designed carefully.
