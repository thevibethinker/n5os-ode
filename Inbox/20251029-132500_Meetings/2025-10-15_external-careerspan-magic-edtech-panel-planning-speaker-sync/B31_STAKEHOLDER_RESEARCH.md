**Perspective**: Speaking as career services professionals (Lisa Noble), EdTech community builders (Magic EdTech), and AI education specialists (Eric Stano)

### Insight 1: Career services funding/incentive misalignment makes B2B undergrad sales structurally difficult

**Evidence**: "We repeatedly found the only ones economically incentivized [to buy career tools] are MBA programs. Where the outcome rankings affect university rankings, affects income in a very concrete way. Harvard HBS has something like 26 career coaches. At the undergraduate level, God forbid you work in a private liberal arts college—every graduate is going to be making more than you in year one."

**Why it matters**: MBA programs invest heavily in career services (outcomes → rankings → enrollment → revenue) while undergrad career centers are chronically underfunded (not revenue drivers, judged on outcomes they can't control). B2B sales to undergrad institutions face structural economic barriers regardless of product quality.

### Insight 2: Students avoid career centers due to psychological barriers (fear of judgment, shame), not just unawareness

**Evidence**: "A lot of students don't go into a career center because they're afraid that going in is like going to the dentist. You're going to hear about your bad flossing habits. Why would you go? You're going to step on a scale when you go to OBGYN for reasons that are never clear to me. They think that going to a career center is a referendum on their employability. So they avoid it like the plague until they have to."

**Why it matters**: Distribution problem is psychological, not just operational. Even best-funded career centers with great advisors won't reach 50% of students. This validates B2C strategy: meet students where they are (on their phones, in private) rather than expecting them to walk into an office that feels like judgment.

### Insight 3: Career tech tools fail because they presuppose self-knowledge that users don't yet have

**Evidence**: "Basically every career tech tool sucks because they presuppose that you know yourself to the extent that you can use the tool. That's the equivalent of giving someone a sword with instructions: 'swing it around and you'll figure it out.' It's psychologically damaging because it leads people to think the problem is them not being able to use the tool, not a tool that's not designed for someone without self-awareness."

**Why it matters**: Explains why StrengthsFinder, career assessments, job boards all have terrible user outcomes. They're Step 3 tools being used at Step 1. Careerspan's differentiation is building the scaffolding for self-knowledge FIRST, not assuming it exists.

### Insight 4: Hiring systems optimize for keyword matching, not authentic fit, creating perverse incentives

**Evidence**: "When you're confronted with tens of thousands of applications for maybe 100 summer internships, you don't have time to look for special people. The people who win through those systems are the ones who've been able to AI their way into a perfectly aligned resume. There is a rare recruiter able to divine that special IT factor. You can go for the lazy approach—check all the right boxes—or is there potential, some hidden quality?"

**Why it matters**: Current ATS/screening tools select for gaming ability (prompt engineering resumes to match job descriptions) not actual transferable skills. Students with babysitting experience that taught real leadership can't articulate it in ATS-friendly language, so they lose to keyword optimizers. This is the market failure Careerspan can solve: help authentic candidates surface their actual fit.
