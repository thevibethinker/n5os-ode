#### 1. Solo technical founders systematically under-resource go-to-market when raising smaller rounds because they optimize for technical hiring over marketing talent

**Evidence:** "I do think this is one argument for raising money... if I had raised more money, I would have just hired someone good to help me with this stuff [marketing]. The thing I've heard from most law firms is that the AI is not good enough yet... I could imagine something working where it's the AI plus me and my paralegal."

**Why it matters:** Awais raised $150K (insufficient for GTM experiments) and hired paralegal instead of marketer because technical founders instinctively hire for product/delivery over distribution. This creates death spiral: can't scale marketing → can't generate leads → can't afford to hire marketer. Suggests seed rounds need to be 2-3x larger for technical founders specifically to force proper GTM resourcing, or founders need explicit advice to hire commercial-side talent first.

#### 2. Trump-era regulatory FUD is shifting immigration clients toward established firms over startups, even when startup offers superior tech and lower cost

**Evidence:** "I do think that there's a shift because of the Trump stuff, where people are going to more established companies or older lawyers... You don't want something to go wrong, and then it's like, who do you have representing you?"

**Why it matters:** Risk aversion in high-stakes regulatory environments creates significant barrier to startup adoption regardless of product quality. Clients willing to pay premium for perceived safety of established brands. This insight applies beyond immigration - any highly regulated space (legal, financial, healthcare) where mistakes have severe consequences. Suggests brand-building and trust signals are disproportionately important in these markets.

#### 3. Early-stage legal tech adoption is blocked not by AI capability but by attorney risk aversion - they need "AI plus human QA" hybrid model to meet their trust threshold

**Evidence:** "The thing I've heard from most law firms is that the AI is not good enough yet, and so they don't really trust it. I think I could imagine something working where it's the AI plus me and my paralegal... to get over that quality bar to make it worth it for them."

**Why it matters:** Explains why legal AI adoption is slower than other sectors despite clear efficiency gains. Attorneys aren't waiting for 100% AI accuracy - they're waiting for productized "AI + human verification" that meets their professional liability standards. This is go-to-market insight, not technical challenge. Pure AI products will struggle; hybrid products that explicitly include human oversight layer will win attorney trust. Suggests positioning strategy for legal tech sales.

#### 4. Commission-based BD hiring can work for high-ticket B2B services but requires genuinely independent operators who already have playbooks

**Evidence:** "I was thinking: could I find someone who can go out and find qualified O-1 candidates, and I pay you based on the number of people you find who end up working with me... I could pay like $1,000 per person that signs... Yeah, I think the specific thing is like, I feel like I just don't know if I can deliver the quality of service... The more clients I have, the more time I have to spend preparing their cases."

**Why it matters (combined implication + strategic value):** Reveals chicken-and-egg constraint in service businesses where founder does delivery - can't scale sales without scaling delivery capacity simultaneously, but commission-only BD requires autonomous operator who doesn't need coaching. For early-stage founders: commission-only BD is capital-efficient but only works if you have excess delivery capacity or can rapidly hire delivery team. Otherwise BD success creates crisis.
