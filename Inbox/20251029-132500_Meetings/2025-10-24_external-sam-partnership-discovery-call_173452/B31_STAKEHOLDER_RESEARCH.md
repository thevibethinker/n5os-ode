### Insight 1: Passive candidates with stale profiles are the highest-quality untapped talent pool; organic re-engagement systems win

**Evidence**: "The best candidates are passive. They're not updating their LinkedIn, they're not working on their stuff. Their shit is out of date. So actually the candidates that you see out there is the visibly good ones are what we're all fighting over. And there's the invisible amazing ones that just need like oh, there is this opportunity. Let me actually put in the effort to update my stuff." (Vrijen, 13:01)

**Why it matters**: Recruiting platforms competing for actively searching candidates are fighting over table scraps while highest-value talent is invisible due to data staleness. Systems that create organic update triggers (like Careerspan's coaching value) unlock differentiated supply that changes unit economics.

### Insight 2: Dual-sided marketplace dynamics in recruiting are brutally difficult; specialization (sourcing OR distribution, not both) may be more viable

**Evidence**: Multiple moments where both teams acknowledged the dual-sided challenge:
- Ash: "For us I think it would definitely need to... if we did this partnership it would definitely have to be like you guys on the sourcing side us because we do dual sided. So it becomes really, really muddy for us" (12:29)
- Vrijen: "I think we don't really want to play the VC game... is it just a bad idea to try to serve both sides of the market?" (12:05)
- Vrijen: "What we both want and is ultimately in some ways I think what no one has had the ability to crack besides us is the candidate side" (13:01)

**Why it matters**: The default assumption in recruiting tech is you need to own both sides for network effects to work. But if specialized players (Careerspan = sourcing, Sam = distribution) can partner effectively, it changes the strategic calculus for building recruiting businesses. Lower capital requirements, faster execution, better product focus.

### Insight 3: AI hiring tools face incoming legal/compliance pressure; reliability engineering and audit trails are becoming table stakes, not nice-to-haves

**Evidence**: 
- Ash bringing up AI ethics panel from Newark Tech Summit unprompted (21:57)
- Ash's specific question: "How are you all ensuring or auditing your AI to make sure that it's not kind of doing the normal AI bias?" (22:10)
- Vrijen: "With the frankly legal pressure that is going to come at all of us when people realize just how broke the hiring market is. The thing that I can say ILSA has maximally worked on is the reliability of what is produced... analyzing human beings in a consistent way that you can defend in a context where you have to justify it to a company." (17:06)

**Why it matters**: This isn't hypothetical future concern - founders building recruiting AI are already thinking about defensibility and compliance. Products built as "vibe-coded" LLM wrappers without audit trails, consistency checks, and bias monitoring will face existential risk when regulation comes. Reliability engineering is competitive moat.
