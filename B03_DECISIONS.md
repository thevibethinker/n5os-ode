# B03: Decisions Made

## Decision 1: Run the Careerspan exit hunt like a sales pipeline

**DECISION:** Treat every inbound expression of interest as "not a yes until it is a yes" and run the outreach as a disciplined sales process—keep the 30‑40 target list alive, push 2–3 of them toward serious bids, and preserve bargaining power by slow-playing some conversations while accelerating others so they land near the same decision window.
**CONTEXT:** Ray reminded us that even "organic" bidders usually narrow down quickly, so having multiple active buyers is the only way to avoid getting stuck with a single, low-value offer when we are strapped for runway. The comparison to fundraising echoed the warning that interest can disappear, and that only a handful of targets will go from curiosity to real negotiations.
**DECIDED BY:** Vrijen Attawar (in coordination with Ray’s deal experience).
**IMPLICATIONS:** Maintain an up‑to‑date tracker that captures outreach cadence, pricing cues, and timing so we can control the rhythm of each deal. Expect to be scrappy for the next three months (runway timeline) while pushing toward those few serious conversations, and only drop a deal once another is sufficiently advanced.
**ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED:** Relying on a single inbound lead (e.g., Calendly connection) or waiting for investors to chase us was deemed too fragile; likewise, treating it as a pure raise with no parallel buyers would leave us without leverage.

## Decision 2: Treat the exit as a stakes-and-network exercise with built-in hedges

**DECISION:** Accept that the cleanest outcome may look more like a PR/visibility win than a big financial payout, while still prioritizing paying back family/friends, creating off‑ramps for Logan and the Head of AI, and being willing to stay on short‑term (likely Vrijen) if a talent/acqui‑hire demand requires it.
**CONTEXT:** Vrijen and Logan already agreed that only one of them will remain in an acqui‑hire scenario, with Vrijen positioned as the product/tech face. Ray reminded us that even symbolic exits are valuable if they boost optics and strengthen the network, so we should hedge by cultivating more relationships even if the deal itself underwhelms.
**DECIDED BY:** Vrijen Attawar (with counsel from Logan and Ray).
**IMPLICATIONS:** Frame conversations not just around cash but around influence; keep building connections that can turn into future roles or introductions. If the offer is more symbolic than financial, lean into that story as a proving ground rather than a failure, while still keeping investors informed about the expectation to repay them when possible.
**ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED:** Holding out for a high signal exit with perfect terms—while emotionally appealing—was deprioritized because it would burn cash, extend runway stress, and risk missing the chance to book the network win that the outreach already affords.

## Decision 3: Model deal structures and cap earnout exposure up front

**DECISION:** Get ahead of legal and financial conversations by learning the difference between asset and share purchases, modeling upfront vs. earnout value, and insisting on earnout windows no longer than a year so we are not on the hook for indefinite compliance with a deal that might never pay out fully.
**CONTEXT:** Ray walked through how acquirers push value into earnouts, which often fail to pay out; he also emphasized the need to run base cases, scenario models, and lawyer reviews so we can counter with realistic expectations. That experience highlighted how unfavorable earnouts can wipe out the immediate value of a deal.
**DECIDED BY:** Vrijen Attawar (guided by Ray’s acquisition lessons).
**IMPLICATIONS:** Schedule a lawyer conversation to understand the cleanest path (asset vs. stock sale), build an offer model that shows a conservative base case plus a capped earnout, and only commit to stays or milestone bets that we can realistically deliver within a year. Push acquirers back when they try to load too much risk onto the earnout.
**ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED:** Accepting whatever compensation structure was handed to us or agreeing to multiyear earnouts without modeling was rejected because it exposed us to the same zero-value earnouts Ray experienced.
