# The Talent Agent Economy

As companies shrink, the impact of each remaining employee grows.

In the old paradigm, a company of 5,000 could absorb 500 coasters. In the new paradigm, a company of 50 cannot absorb five. When your team is small, every hire is a "Superunicorn" requirement.

But as we’ve seen, the current infrastructure—the resume, the ATS, the credential—is architecturally incapable of finding them. It only finds the people who are good at *appearing* competent.

So, how do we find the people who actually *are*?

The answer isn't a better job board. It’s a shift in how talent is represented and verified. It’s the move toward the **Talent Agent Economy.**

## Representation for the Elite

In sports and entertainment, the best talent doesn't "apply" for roles. They have representation.

An agent doesn't just negotiate the contract; they manage the **scouting report.** They curate the "tape" we talked about in Part 2. They verify the mental model of the person they represent.

In tech, we’re seeing the emergence of this same model. As the "Superunicorn" becomes the single most valuable asset in a company's arsenal, those individuals will stop being treated as labor commodities and start being treated as elite assets.

They won't be on LinkedIn. They'll be managed.

## The Mental Model as Verified Asset

The breakthrough happens when we stop relying on the **Credentialing Mirage (see my *Don Hirexote* article)**  and start relying on **Longitudinal Correlated Data.**

This is what we are building at Careerspan (www.mycareerspan.com).

Imagine a system that doesn't just store a resume, but maps a person's mental model over one's "career span." It tracks how they think, how they solve problems, and how they relate to others—not in a single interview, but as a persistent record of behavior and reflection.

When a founder looks at a candidate through this lens, they aren't just reading marketing material. They are looking at correlated data points:

- Does their self-reflection on a project match the feedback from their peers?
- Does their described "scrappiness" show up in the actual timeline of their builds?
- Does the "tape" confirm that they led the team through a crisis, or were they just in the room?

This is how you solve the fraud problem. You can fake a resume. You can even fake an interview. But it is nearly impossible to fake a consistent mental model across years of correlated data.

## Scaling the Non-Obvious

The "Talent Agent" of the future won't just be a human in a suit. It will be an infrastructure of verification.

By mapping these mental models, we can move from **passive filtering** to **active matching at scale.** We can find the "non-obvious" Superunicorn—the engineer in a mid-sized firm who has the agency of a founder—because the data shows us their "tape" long before their resume does.

We aren't just helping people find jobs. We are building the **Internet of Careers**—a verified graph of how the world actually builds.

## The New Scouting Report

Founders: stop hunting for the obvious unicorns in the bloodbath of the ATS.

The people Ryan Carson described exist. They are the essential players who will build the next decade of technology. But they are invisible to your current tools.

To find them, you have to stop looking at the brand names on their LinkedIn and start looking at the mental models in their wake. You have to move from the box score to the scouting report.

The Talent Agent Economy is here. The question is: who is managing your scouting report?
