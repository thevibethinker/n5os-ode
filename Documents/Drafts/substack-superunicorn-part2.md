# The Sports Brain

Hiring could learn a lot from sports.

Not the clichés—"team players," "MVPs," "bench strength." The actual mechanics of how professional sports identifies talent.

Because sports figured out decades ago what tech still hasn't: **you can't stack-rank people on one axis.**

## The Combinatorics Problem

Carson's super-unicorn has eight traits. Technical depth. Communication. Ownership. Learning velocity. Taste. Scrappiness. Low ego. High curiosity.

Notice what he's describing: a *combination*. Not one dominant skill, but a blend. The value isn't in maxing out a single stat—it's in the specific configuration of attributes that makes someone effective in a particular context.

This is exactly how sports thinks about talent.

A point guard isn't just "fast." They need court vision, decision-making under pressure, the ability to create space, defensive awareness, and the emotional intelligence to run a locker room. Speed without vision is a turnover machine. Vision without speed is a liability.

The best scouts don't ask "who's the fastest?" They ask "what combination of traits produces outcomes in *this* system?"

Hiring asks "who has the best resume?"

## The Scouting Report vs. The Stat Line

In sports, there's a distinction between the stat line and the scouting report.

The stat line tells you what happened. Points per game. Assists. Completion percentage. It's the resume—a summary of outputs, stripped of context.

The scouting report tells you *how* it happened. How does this player move without the ball? How do they respond when the play breaks down? What do they do in the fourth quarter of a close game? How do teammates talk about them?

The stat line is easy to compile. The scouting report requires watching the tape.

Modern hiring has stat lines. We have resumes, LinkedIn profiles, years of experience, company logos. What we don't have is tape.

We don't know how someone actually solved that problem they're claiming credit for. We don't know if they led the project or just attended the meetings. We don't know how they handled the moment when everything went sideways.

We're drafting based on box scores.

## The Non-Obvious Pick

Every draft has obvious picks. The consensus top prospects. Everyone knows who they are. Every team wants them.

The value isn't in identifying the obvious pick. The value is in identifying the non-obvious one—the player in the third round who outperforms their draft position because you saw something in the tape that others missed.

In the NFL, Tom Brady was pick 199. In the NBA, Nikola Jokić was pick 41. The stat lines didn't scream future MVP. But someone watched the tape.

The non-obvious superunicorn is the same. They don't have the Stanford degree. They didn't do the prestigious internship. Their LinkedIn doesn't trigger the keyword filters.

But if you watched the tape—if you understood how they think, how they build, how they respond to adversity—you'd see it.

The problem is: **there is no tape.**

## What Would Tape Look Like?

In sports, tape is footage. You can literally watch someone play.

In careers, tape would be something else: a longitudinal record of how someone thinks, how they describe their work, how others describe working with them. Not a snapshot—a pattern over time.

Imagine you could see:
- How someone articulated the same problem at three different points in their career
- What they said they learned from a failure, and whether their subsequent behavior reflected it
- How their peers and managers described their working style, unprompted
- The delta between how they present themselves and how others experience them

That's not a resume. That's not a LinkedIn profile. That's a **mental model**—a map of how someone actually operates.

If you had that map, you could correlate it against what they say in an interview. You could check for consistency. You could spot the coasters and the frauds before they cost you a year of runway.

You could scout instead of filter.

## The Shift

The ATS is a filter. It takes a pile of resumes and removes the ones that don't match keywords.

Scouting is the opposite. It starts with a question—what combination of traits produces outcomes in this context?—and then goes looking for signal.

Filtering is passive. You post a job and wait for inbound.

Scouting is active. You build a map of the talent landscape and identify the non-obvious picks before anyone else sees them.

The companies that figure this out will stop competing in the bidding war for obvious unicorns. They'll find the non-obvious ones—the people with the right mental models, the right combinations, the right tape—before the market prices them correctly.

Everyone else will keep stack-ranking resumes and wondering why their hires don't work out.
