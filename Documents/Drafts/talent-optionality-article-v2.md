# Resume Fraud Is a System Failure. Fight Me.

I'm going to say something that'll get me yelled at: I have more sympathy for resume fraudsters than I do for most hiring managers.

Not because lying is fine. It's not. But we've built a hiring system so kafkaesque, so obviously rigged, that I can't pretend to be shocked when people decide the game isn't worth playing straight. A situation so tragic, Greek playwrights would call this "a bit much."

Here's the math nobody wants to do. The single mom in Akron with a decade of running logistics? Her experience doesn't count. No degree. The self-taught coder who learned while working nights? His GitHub means nothing. Wrong pedigree.

We built a machine for turning hopes and dreams into ATS-compliant resumes. Then we act surprised when people feed it garbage in, hoping for garbage out that looks a little better than the truth.

## This Is the Talent Optionality Thesis

When talented people have real options, they don't lie.

Not because they're saints. Because the math changes. Lying has costs — getting caught, the stress, the identity erosion. When legitimate paths exist, those costs aren't worth paying.

But close every door? Tell someone their actual skills won't survive the filter? Now lying isn't a character failure. It's a desperate calculation. Often a rational one.

The thesis in one line: **Expand optionality, and the desperate behavior disappears.** Not hearts and minds stuff. Math.

## The Prison Metaphor Is Too Polite

Calling the modern job market a "prison" with credentials as the "blueprint for escape" undersells it.

Prisons have predictable rules. This system? The rules change depending on who you are. The kid whose parents paid for test prep, unpaid internships, and resume coaches — he walks through the front door. The kid who taught himself everything while supporting his family? He's told to get in line and wait for someone to vouch for him.

Then we lecture both about playing by the rules.

The height of disingenuousness.

## The Self-Averaging Effect (Why Credential Worship Is Bad Math)

Hiring scared means hiring for cover. "I picked the Stanford grad. How was I supposed to know?"

This is building for the wrong scorecard.

Here's what actually happens: when you hire for potential instead of pedigree, your misses get absorbed by your hits. The non-traditional candidate who works out doesn't just perform. They perform with something to prove. Your credentialed hire who was always told they'd succeed? They coast.

The self-averaging effect means betting on potential, at scale, wins. Not every time. Over time.

Companies that get this stop treating a human problem like it's a technical problem. Stop adding filters to the ATS. Start actually talking to people.

## Where I Draw the Line

Yes — some people lie when they have options. Genuine options. Legitimate paths they could've taken.

Those people? Different conversation.

But they're the minority. Far smaller than LinkedIn's moral police would have you think. Most "fraudsters" looked at their bills, looked at their kids, looked at a system that told them their real self wasn't sellable, and made a bad call. A human call. One I'm not interested in pretending I can't understand.

## The Fix Isn't Excusing Fraud. It's Making Fraud Pointless.

We don't need better fraud detection. We need systems that make fraud irrational.

Career infrastructure that develops people instead of sorting them. Hiring that sees past the single sheet of paper — candidate data 10x richer than a resume can hold. Self-advocacy as something you get good at, not something you have to do like swallowing medicine.

When options are real, lying becomes stupid. Not because character improved. Because circumstances did.

## Here's What I Actually Believe

The talent market isn't broken because people are dishonest.

People look dishonest because the talent market is broken.

You can moralize about fraud all you want. You can fire people. You can write LinkedIn posts about integrity. None of it fixes anything.

Or you can expand optionality. Build paths. Stop pretending credentials earn their own legitimacy.

Fix the pipes. The water clears up on its own.
