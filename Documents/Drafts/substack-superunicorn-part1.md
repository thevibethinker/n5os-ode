# The Credentialing Mirage

Ryan Carson posted a list of traits for the "super-unicorn" employee: technical depth, communication skills, ownership, learning velocity, taste, scrappiness, low ego, high curiosity.

He's right. That's exactly who you want.

Here's what he didn't say: **nothing in modern hiring can actually measure those things.**

## The Gap

We've built an entire infrastructure around hiring. Applicant tracking systems. Structured interviews. Take-home assignments. Reference checks. LinkedIn Recruiter seats that cost more than some employees.

All of it optimizes for one thing: the resume.

The resume tells you where someone worked. What title they held. How long they stayed. It tells you nothing about how they think, how they behave under pressure, or how they relate to the people around them.

We aren't hiring for competence. We're hiring for the **appearance** of competence.

And appearance can be faked.

## The Fraud Problem

I've now had three separate conversations—with founders, with security professionals, with people running technical screens—where the same thing came up: fake candidates.

Not exaggerated resumes. Not inflated titles. Actual fraudulent actors, including state-sponsored operatives, applying to technical roles at startups.

In one case, the security review was so severe it triggered a complete overhaul of the company's hiring protocols. The candidate had passed the resume screen. They'd passed the initial interviews. They looked perfect on paper.

Because paper is easy to fake.

This isn't a rare edge case. It's a systemic vulnerability. When your entire filtering system relies on self-reported credentials—credentials that can be fabricated, purchased, or stolen—you've built a castle on sand.

## The Coasting Problem

Fraud is dramatic. But there's a quieter failure mode that's far more common.

I recently spoke with a founder who wanted to hire someone from a prestigious company. Decade of tenure. Senior title. All the right logos on the resume.

Several rounds of interviews in, it became clear: this person had been coasting. They couldn't articulate what they'd actually built. They had no ownership over outcomes. They'd simply... existed in the building while the company's reputation did the heavy lifting. They dodged that bullet—but wasted a ton of time in the process. 

The brand name meant nothing. The tenure meant nothing. The only signal that would have revealed the truth was asking the right questions—and having something to check the answers against.

They didn't have that. So they hired a ghost.

## The Resume Is Marketing Material

Here's the uncomfortable truth: a resume is not a record of what someone did. It's a story they tell about themselves.

Some people are excellent at telling that story. They know the keywords. They know how to frame a lateral move as a promotion. They know that "led cross-functional initiatives" sounds better than "attended meetings."

Other people—often the ones actually building things—are terrible at it. They're heads-down. They don't optimize for legibility. They assume the work speaks for itself.

It doesn't. Not in a system designed to process appearance.

The result: we systematically over-index on people who are good at seeming competent, and under-index on people who are good at being competent.

## The Obvious Unicorn Trap

So when Carson posts his list of super-unicorn traits, and founders start mentally scanning their networks, they're not actually looking for those traits.

They're looking for people who *appear* to have those traits.

And if everyone's hunting the same obvious unicorn—the Stanford CS grad, the ex-Stripe engineer, the person with the perfect narrative—then one person gets the prize. Everyone else is Don Quixote, tilting at windmills, fighting over scraps.

The obvious unicorn is a bidding war. You'll pay over the odds. You'll probably lose anyway.

Meanwhile, the **non-obvious** unicorn—the person who actually has Carson's eight traits but hasn't optimized their LinkedIn for discoverability—remains invisible.

## The Question

If the resume is marketing material, and the ATS is just a keyword filter, and the interview is a performance, and the reference check is a formality...

What's actually left?

How do you find someone's real mental model—how they think, how they behave, how they've grown—when every signal in the system is optimized for theater?

The answer isn't to filter harder. It isn't to add more interview rounds. It isn't to build a better ATS.

The answer is to change what you're measuring.
