I'm going to say something that gets me in trouble: I have more sympathy for resume fraudsters than I do for most hiring managers.

Not because lying is fine -- it's not. But we've built a hiring system so obviously rigged that I can't pretend to be shocked when people decide the game isn't worth playing straight.

Think about the math. The single mom in Akron who managed logistics for a decade, kept teams running, solved impossible problems? She applies for a coordinator role. Gets auto-rejected. No degree. The self-taught developer who learned to code while working nights at 7-Eleven? His GitHub is impressive. Doesn't matter. Wrong school -- actually, no school.

We built a machine for turning hopes and dreams into ATS-compliant garbage, and then we're shocked when people game it. What did we think was gonna happen?

Here's what I've learned from a decade of career coaching (and what I call the talent optionality thesis): when talented people have real options, they don't lie. Not because they became better humans. Because lying has costs -- getting caught, the stress, the identity erosion -- and when legitimate paths exist, those costs aren't worth paying.

But squeeze someone's options down to zero? Make them choose between integrity and feeding their kids? Now lying isn't a character flaw. It's a survival calculation. Often a rational one.

I draw a line -- some people lie when they had good options right there for the taking. Those folks are different. But they're rare. Way rarer than the LinkedIn moralists pretend.

Most frauds aren't sociopaths. They're people who looked at a rigged game, looked at their bills, looked at their kids, and made a desperate call. A bad call, sure. But not one I'm gonna pretend I can't follow.

The fix isn't better fraud detection. The fix is making fraud pointless. Build systems that develop people instead of just sorting them. Hiring that actually sees humans, not papers. Paths forward that don't require knowing the right people or having the right parents.

When options are real, lying becomes stupid. Math changes. Behavior follows.

The talent market isn't broken because people lie. People lie because the talent market is broken. Fix the pipes, water clears up.
