# Why I Have More Sympathy for Resume Fraudsters Than Hiring Managers

Okay, look. I know how this sounds. 

I've been coaching careers for a decade. I've seen everything. The guy who invented a whole degree. The woman who promoted herself two levels on paper. The kid who listed an internship that was actually watching YouTube videos in his mom's basement.

And here's my dirty secret: I understand all of them.

Not approve. Understand.

Because here's the thing nobody on LinkedIn wants to admit — resume fraud tracks almost perfectly with desperation. Not with being a bad person. With having no other obvious play.

---

The single mom in Ohio who managed logistics for a decade, kept teams running, solved impossible problems? She applies for a coordinator role. Gets auto-rejected. No degree. 

The self-taught developer who learned to code while working nights? His portfolio's impressive. Doesn't matter. Wrong school. Actually, no school.

We built this beautiful machine for turning hopes and dreams into ATS-compliant garbage, and then we're shocked — *shocked!* — when people game it.

What did we think was gonna happen?

---

So here's what I actually believe. Call it the talent optionality thesis:

**When talented people have real options, they don't lie.**

Not because they became better humans. Because lying has costs and when you have legitimate paths, the costs aren't worth it. But squeeze someone's options down to zero? Make them choose between integrity and feeding their kids? Now lying isn't a character flaw. It's a survival calculation.

Often? A rational one.

---

I draw a line. Some people lie when they had good options. Legitimate paths right there for the taking. Those folks are different.

But they're rare. Way rarer than the moralists pretend.

Most frauds aren't sociopaths. They're people who looked at a rigged game, looked at what they needed to survive, and made a desperate call. A bad call, sure. But not one I'm gonna pretend I can't follow.

---

The fix isn't better fraud detection. 

The fix is making fraud pointless.

Build systems that develop people instead of just sorting them. Hiring that actually sees humans, not papers. Paths forward that don't require someone to know the right people or have the right parents.

When options are real, lying becomes stupid. Math changes. Behavior follows.

---

Here's my ask: stop moralizing about fraud for five minutes. Ask instead — what would make fraud unnecessary?

Answer that, and you've got something.

The talent market isn't broken because people lie. People lie because the talent market is broken.

Fix the pipes. Water clears up.
