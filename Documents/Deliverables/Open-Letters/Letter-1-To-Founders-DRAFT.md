# You Don't Have a Hiring Problem. You Have a Signal Problem.

## An Open Letter to Founders Who Are Tired of Hiring Feeling Impossible

---

You've felt this, even if you couldn't name it.

More candidates than ever. Hiring harder than ever. Your inbox floods with applications. Your recruiters are drowning. And somehow — impossibly — the quality of your pipeline feels *worse* than when you had a tenth of the volume.

This isn't your imagination. Something broke.

---

### The Old Deal

Let me take you back to when resumes worked.

The resume was invented in an era when producing one required effort. Typewriters. Carbon paper. Real thought about what to include. That friction — the effort of constructing the document — *was* the signal. A well-crafted resume meant something because it was hard to produce. The artifact wasn't the data. The *effort to create the artifact* was the data.

For decades, this worked well enough. Not perfectly — plenty of great candidates have always been bad at self-marketing, and plenty of charlatans have always been great at it — but the ratio was manageable. The signal-to-noise held.

Then we handed everyone a bullshit machine.

---

### The Collapse

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **the marginal cost of bullshit is now zero.**

Any candidate — good, bad, unqualified, overqualified — can generate a "perfect" resume in 30 seconds. AI didn't break the resume. AI *revealed* that the resume was always a fragile proxy. It was a fax machine we kept using because we forgot what fax machines were for.

The resume is a self-authored marketing document. Unverified. Unvetted. Optimized for keywords your ATS is looking for. And now, produced at infinite scale by anyone who can type a prompt.

When fabrication is free, the signal value of the artifact collapses to zero.

---

### The Paradox Resolved

So here's your paradox, explained:

**You're not short on candidates. You're short on confidence about your candidates.**

The volume went up. The signal-to-noise ratio went down. Way down. Your funnel is flooded with applications you can't evaluate — because the evaluation artifact itself can't carry truth anymore.

The resume is a 70-year-old data structure we've been treating like gospel. But the map is not the territory. The proxy is not the signal. And we've all been hoodwinked into believing there's only one way — that the resume is the gold standard.

It's not. It never was. It was just the best we had.

---

### The Trap You're In

When inbound fails, you reach for outbound.

"We need better sourcing."

I've said it. You've said it. Every founder I know has said it at some point. And it makes sense — if the applications coming in are garbage, go find the good people yourself.

But sourcing is dessert. Fine for a few critical hires. Unsustainable as a diet. You can't hand-source every role. You don't have the time. Your recruiters don't have the bandwidth. And the candidates you're chasing? They're being chased by everyone else too.

Here's what we don't say out loud: **sourcing isn't your bottleneck. Screening is.**

You default to "we need more sourcing" because you've accepted the premise that someone has to read those resumes. A hundred. A thousand. Someone has to do it.

But what if the premise is wrong?

What if the bottleneck isn't *finding* candidates, but *evaluating* them? And what if your evaluation tool — your ATS, your keyword filters, your "AI-powered screening" — is just a very expensive machine for processing garbage?

Garbage in, garbage out. You can't filter your way to signal when the input medium can't carry signal.

---

### The Resume Industrial Complex

Let me introduce you to a system that profits from your frustration.

There is an entire ecosystem — ATS vendors, resume optimization services, HR software platforms, recruiting agencies — built around the resume as the canonical hiring artifact. I call it the **Resume Industrial Complex**.

This ecosystem doesn't work for you. It works for itself.

- **ATS vendors** sell you tools to process resumes at scale. But they can't profitably process garbage to find gold, so they just... process garbage. Fast.
- **Resume services** exist because your filter is broken. They sell candidates optimization for a broken game.
- **The whole machine** depends on the resume staying central, even as everyone quietly admits it doesn't work anymore.

None of these players are incentivized to fix the underlying architecture. Their revenue depends on its brokenness.

This isn't a conspiracy. It's worse. It's an emergent alignment of incentives against your interests — and against the interests of every candidate who deserves to be seen clearly.

---

### What Good Recruiters Actually Do

You know what actually works? Good recruiters.

Not the ones running spray-and-pray campaigns. The ones who have *put in the time* to actually know their candidates. Who've sieved through hundreds of people to surface the five who might actually fit.

When a great recruiter sends you someone, they're not just forwarding a resume. They're putting down a deposit. They're staking their reputation — their capital — on the claim that this person is worth your time.

That's why referrals work. That's why recruiter relationships matter. It's not nepotism. It's *trust signal*. Someone with skin in the game is vouching for the candidate, and that vouching carries information the resume structurally cannot.

The problem? It doesn't scale. You can't have that recruiter relationship for every role. You can't rely on referrals for every hire. And so you're back to the inbox, drowning in applications that all look the same.

---

### The High Road: What You Can Actually Do

Here's my ask. Stop asking "how do we get more resumes?" Start asking **"how do we get more signal per candidate?"**

That means:

**1. Set up hurdles — the right ones.**
Not friction that filters out the lazy. Friction that *reveals*. Ask for something that takes effort to produce and is hard to fake. A short video. A written response to a real problem. A work sample. The effort itself is data.

**2. Make the hurdles scalable.**
Don't create linear friction where you have to hand-review every submission. Design friction that compounds — where the candidate invests once, and that investment travels. Stop asking people to jump through the same hoops for every application.

**3. Bring people in.**
Paid work trials. Collaboration days. Give candidates a chance to demonstrate — and give yourself a chance to evaluate — in an environment that resumes can't fake. Yes, it takes time. It's also the only way to get signal that actually predicts performance.

**4. Build relationships before you need them.**
The best hires don't come from a job post. They come from a pool of people who already know your company, already have signal of intent, already trust you — and you them. Start cultivating that pool *before* you're desperate.

**5. If you're in a rush, pay for expertise.**
A great recruiter isn't an expense. They're a translation layer. They've already done the sieve work. When you don't have time to build the pool yourself, paying someone who has is the rational move.

---

### The World That's Coming

I'll be blunt: the resume is dying. Slowly, painfully, but it's dying.

The next generation of hiring infrastructure won't be built on self-authored marketing documents that anyone can fake. It'll be built on demonstrated work, portable trust, and signal that scales.

The founders who figure this out first will win the talent market. They'll spend less time screening garbage and more time meeting people they're actually excited about. They'll build teams that out-execute the competition because they hired for fit, not for keyword density.

Everyone else will keep wondering why hiring feels so hard.

---

I'm writing this because I've been in your shoes. I've stared at a hundred applications and felt nothing but exhaustion. I've defaulted to "we need better sourcing" when I should have asked better questions.

The system is broken. But you're not powerless. You can opt out of the resume industrial complex — not all at once, but one role at a time. One better process at a time. One investment in signal at a time.

The candidates worth hiring? They're out there. They're drowning in the same broken system you are.

Build a process that sees them.

---

*— Vrijen Attawar*
*Founder, Careerspan*
