# How to Scout the 0.1% (The "Tape" vs. The "Box Score")

Most hiring processes are designed to filter out the incompetent, not to identify the exceptional.

And especially not those 

And yet 46% of hires fail by 18-month mark and 89% of the reason they fail is a values, mindset, or soft skills issue.

That is not an exaggeration. If you look at how the modern ATS (Applicant Tracking System) works, it is explicitly built to reject 95% of applicants based on keyword matching before a human ever sees them.

Especially if you consider yourself a rigorous hiring manager, you are the most likely to hire like a bureaucrat.

I mean, it's all over LinkedIn. There isn't a day that goes by where you see a hiring manager with a verifiably high bar fall into obvious traps.

There are plenty of "perfect on paper" candidates who are one minor ambiguity away from a total meltdown, and there are plenty of "non-traditional" candidates who are abnormally high-agency, strategic, and impactful.

Rather than talking about "culture fit," "behavioral interviewing," or anything else that you can ask ChatGPT to teach you about, I want to give you something different.

We're going to start with "Box Score" hiring and work our way towards "Game Tape" scouting.

If you read carefully, you will experience what it's like to see talent on a new level.

## If your process is so rigorous, why do you keep mis-hiring?

> "The map is not the territory." – Alfred Korzybski

Hiring tip number one:

If you want to understand what elite talent looks like, it helps to understand what it does not look like.

That way, we can identify and avoid bureaucratic hiring.

When we observe what bureaucratic hiring looks like, we come to a few insights:

- **Bureaucratic hiring is keyword-obsessed.** You try to jam a complex human into a set of boolean search strings.
- **Bureaucratic hiring is reductionistic.** Experts in hiring try to reduce a career to "years of experience" or "logos on a resume."
- **Bureaucratic hiring is risk-averse.** You only trust candidates who have already done the exact same job at a similar company (which usually means they are bored and expensive).
- **Bureaucratic hiring does not question.** Your justification is "that's just how the req was written."

As a whole, bureaucratic hiring is about closing your mind off once you've reached the limits of the resume.

It is to stop thinking too early. To reach a point where you react with your pre-programmed bias toward "Prestige" and fail to reach any novel insight about the actual human capacity in front of you.

With that, we can start to guess at a worthy definition of Talent Scouting, which could be:

**The ability to evaluate the "Tape" over the "Box Score."**

The "Box Score" is the lagging indicator. It's the title, the tenure, the university, the certifications. It tells you what happened, but not *how* it happened or *who* made it happen.

The "Tape" is the leading indicator. It's the artifacts, the decision memos, the code commits, the specific way they deconstruct a problem in real-time. It is the immutable evidence of their mental models in action.

The question then is, what's the difference between a Resume and a Portfolio? And how do you develop the ability to see the latter in the former?

A Resume is horizontal. It lists facts about specific domains where someone was present. People with great resumes are "qualified," but we don't need more qualified people in today's world. We need effective people.

A Portfolio (or "The Tape") is vertical. It shows the sophistication of their operating system. One can have a sparse resume but a dense portfolio of thinking, and use that capacity to generate outcomes that a "senior" person could never touch.

The "Perfect Candidate" failure mode stems from being horizontally qualified but vertically empty. A VP who has all the right logos but can't navigate ambiguity. A PM who knows every framework but can't ship.

That's why learning to scout is so important.

Because your team is how you interact with the market.

## Altitudes of Talent

Let's work from the ground up.

**Lines of Talent** are skills you can verify.\
These are the keywords. Python, SQL, GAAP Accounting, Enterprise Sales. When you filter for these, you are filtering for the baseline. This is necessary, but insufficient.

**Levels of Talent** are how they apply those skills.

When we observe how talent develops over time, we notice patterns:

- **Commodity (Level 1)** – They execute tasks as defined. They follow the ticket. "I did what you asked."
- **Competent (Level 2)** – They own the outcome of the task. They solve the immediate problem. "I fixed the bug."
- **Strategic (Level 3)** – They own the system that generates the tasks. They prevent the problem from recurring. "I refactored the module so this class of bugs is impossible."
- **Generative (Level 4)** – They create new value that wasn't asked for. They see the market opportunity hidden in the technical debt. "I realized this bug was actually a feature request, so I built a prototype."

Levels 1 and 2 are "Box Score" hires. You can find them with keywords.

Levels 3 and 4 are "Tape" hires. You can only find them by auditing their thinking.

The "Superunicorn" (Level 5) is the person who transcends the role entirely. They don't just fill a seat; they change the trajectory of the company. They are not looking for jobs on LinkedIn. They are typically hiding in plain sight, working on their own projects, or buried in a role that doesn't fit them.

## The Talent Agent Economy

Most companies are still operating in the "Job Board Economy."

They post a req (The Box Score demands), they get 1,000 applicants (The Commodity supply), and they use AI to filter it down to the "Safe Bets."

This is a race to the bottom.

The future belongs to the **Talent Agent Economy.**

In a world where AI makes average work free, the value of elite work goes to infinity. The difference between a Level 3 engineer and a Level 5 engineer isn't 2x; it's 100x.

To find these people, you have to stop acting like a gatekeeper and start acting like a scout.

1. **Stop reading resumes first.** Look at the portfolio, the blog, the GitHub, the side project. Look at the *Tape*.
2. **Stop interviewing for knowledge.** Interview for *understanding*. Give them a real problem you are facing right now and watch them work.
3. **Stop hiring for the role you have.** Hire for the trajectory they are on.

If you only hire within the confines set by HR, the ATS, and "Best Practices," the outcome of your team will be average.

So, the next time you see a resume that doesn't quite fit the box score, but has a flash of brilliance in the tape...

Do not collapse in on yourself.

Sit with it. Question it. Realize that your bureaucratic brain wants to feel safe.

But safety is death in a high-performance environment.

Those who scout the tape will win.
