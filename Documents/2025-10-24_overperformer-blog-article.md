# The Overperformer Signal: Why the Best Hires Don't Apply to Everything
**Author:** Vrijen Attawar  
**Published:** 2025-10-24  
**Reading time:** 6 minutes

---

There are some people so good that you make space for them—even when you didn't know you had the need.

You've probably experienced this. A candidate walks into an interview and within fifteen minutes, you're already rethinking your org chart. They fit the role so well that you realize the job description you wrote was just a rough sketch of what you actually needed.

This is the category of talent every company wants: **over-performers**. People who deliver above-normal results. People who don't just fill a role—they elevate it.

But here's the painful truth: the way we hire today makes it nearly impossible to find these people efficiently.

---

## The Volume Problem

Most hiring managers are drowning in applications.

You post a job on LinkedIn or Indeed. Within 48 hours, you have 300+ applicants. Some are qualified. Most aren't. And now you're stuck manually screening resumes, trying to separate signal from noise.

This is the promise—and the curse—of modern ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems). They give you volume. They give you process management. But they don't give you quality.

And quality is what matters.

Because when you're hiring, you're not just filling a seat. You're betting on someone's ability to solve problems you haven't even articulated yet. You're looking for over-performers—people who can deliver results that exceed the job description.

But traditional ATS platforms aren't built to surface those people. They're built to collect resumes and let you filter by keywords.

That's not good enough.

---

## The Candidate Side of the Problem

Now flip the perspective.

If you're a high-performing professional—someone who consistently delivers above expectations—how do you signal that to the market?

You can't just write "I'm great" on your resume. Everyone does that.

You can list accomplishments, sure. But how does a hiring manager know those accomplishments were actually impressive relative to your role? How do they know you weren't just coasting in an easy environment?

The answer: they don't.

Which means high-performers and average-performers look roughly the same on paper. The only way to separate them is through manual screening, interviews, and reference checks—all of which take time and energy.

So high-performers face a frustrating reality: **they're competing in a system that doesn't distinguish them from the crowd.**

And worse, many high-performers don't even realize they're over-performing. They feel the friction—working harder than everyone around them, delivering results, but not seeing the recognition or compensation to match. They just assume that's how work is.
